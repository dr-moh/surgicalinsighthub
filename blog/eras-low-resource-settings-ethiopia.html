ERAS in Low-Resource Settings: Adapting Enhanced Recovery for Tertiary Hospitals in Ethiopia

Evidence Review Global Surgery June 11, 2026

A modified Delphi study deconstruction on how to implement feasible, sustainable, and context-appropriate perioperative care guidelines in LMICs.

Global Surgery Evidence Review This article is an editorial analysis and clinical commentary on the study "Adaptation of Enhanced Recovery After Surgery Protocol for Elective Gastrointestinal and Hepatopancreaticobiliary Surgeries for Tertiary Hospitals in Ethiopia: A Modified Delphi Study" (published in the journal Surgery, March 2026) led by Wongel Tena Shale (General Surgeon and Assistant Professor, Jimma University Medical Center, Ethiopia) and colleagues.

The implementation of Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols has transformed perioperative care globally, significantly reducing post-operative complications and hospital length of stay (LOS). However, the vast majority of standard ERAS guidelines have been designed and validated in high-income countries (HICs). When surgical teams attempt to implement these protocols directly within low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), they encounter significant barriers.

Resource constraints, differences in local dietary patterns, socio-demographic factors, and varying levels of hospital infrastructure mean that standard Western protocols—which may assume ready access to epidural kits, active thermal warmers, commercial high-carbohydrate drinks, and home-care nursing networks—are often unfeasible. To bridge this gap, a research team led by Dr. Wongel Tena Shale from Jimma University conducted a modified Delphi consensus study. By utilizing a panel of 32 interdisciplinary experts and conducting two rounds of surveys, they established a tailored 27-recommendation ERAS protocol optimized for elective gastrointestinal (GI) and hepatopancreaticobiliary (HPB) surgeries in Ethiopian tertiary hospitals.

Preoperative Stage Recommendations

8 Recommendations
  • Preadmission Counseling: Focused patient and family counseling tailored to language diversity and literacy levels.
  • Nutritional Screening & Optimization: Mandatory assessment of severe malnutrition (common in rural patient cohorts) using simple, cost-effective measures.
  • Avoidance of Prolonged Fasting: Implementation of a strict 6-hour limit for solids and a 2-hour limit for clear fluids.
  • Carbohydrate Loading: Utilizing locally-prepared carbohydrate alternatives instead of high-cost commercial formulas.
  • Bowel Preparation: Restricting mechanical bowel prep only to specific left-sided colonic or rectal surgeries.
  • Selective Thromboembolism Prophylaxis: Context-sensitive risk assessment and pharmacotherapy administration.
  • Avoidance of Routine Pre-Medication: Minimizing sedatives to ensure prompt post-operative mobilization.
  • Antimicrobial Prophylaxis: Mandatory administration of narrow-spectrum, cost-effective antibiotics within 60 minutes of skin incision.
Global Surgery Commentary: Standard Western protocols recommend commercial maltodextrin carbohydrate drinks. In rural Ethiopia, these are unavailable or prohibitively expensive. The Delphi panel resolved this by recommending locally-available alternatives, such as sweetened black tea or traditional barley water (*Kolo* water), which provide equivalent glycemic loading at a fraction of the cost. Furthermore, nutritional screening is adapted to use mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) and serum albumin, avoiding advanced body composition diagnostics.

Intraoperative Stage Recommendations

7 Recommendations
  • Standardized Anesthetic Protocol: Utilizing regional anesthesia (e.g. TAP blocks or wound infiltration) as cost-effective alternatives to thoracic epidurals.
  • Selective Nasogastric Tube (NGT) Use: Restricting NGT use to decompressing the stomach intraoperatively; avoiding routine post-operative placement.
  • Hypothermia Prevention: Promoting passive insulation methods (double-sheet wrapping and warmed IV fluids) due to active warmer shortages.
  • Clinical Goal-Directed Fluid Therapy (GDFT): Titrating fluids based on clinical perfusion endpoints (urine output, heart rate, blood pressure).
  • Surgical Access: Using minimally invasive surgery (MIS) when equipment is available, but prioritizing rapid, open techniques to limit anesthesia exposure when necessary.
  • Selective Abdominal Drainage: Avoiding routine drain placement; reserving drains only for high-risk anastomoses (e.g. duodenal leak concerns).
  • Selective Urinary Catheterization: Removing catheters immediately post-operation or within 24 hours.
Global Surgery Commentary: In HICs, intraoperative fluid optimization is guided by advanced stroke-volume variation monitors or esophageal Doppler devices. In low-resource settings, these are absent. The Jimma University study adapts GDFT by establishing a clinical algorithm that uses heart rate stability, mean arterial pressure (MAP) targets, and capillary refill time as pragmatic, non-invasive guides to avoid fluid overload, which causes bowel edema and anastomotic failure.

Postoperative Stage Recommendations

6 Recommendations
  • Early Mobilization: Getting patients out of bed and walking within 2 to 4 hours post-operatively.
  • Early Oral Intake: Reintroducing clear fluids and soft foods on Day 0, rather than waiting for flatus or bowel sounds.
  • Multimodal Non-Opioid Analgesia: Maximizing round-the-clock Paracetamol and NSAIDs, limiting opioids to rescue doses.
  • Early Urinary Catheter Removal: Discontinuing catheterization within 24 hours for non-pelvic surgeries.
  • Standardized PONV Prevention: Using readily available anti-emetics (such as metoclopramide and dexamethasone) systematically.
  • Adapted Discharge & Follow-up: Ensuring patients are clinically stable and counseling them based on distance and travel infrastructure.
Global Surgery Commentary: Traditional post-operative surgical practice in East Africa often kept patients nil-by-mouth (NPO) until they passed flatus, fearing early feeding would rupture bowel anastomoses. The Delphi study aligns with modern science, demonstrating that early feeding on Day 0 reduces post-operative ileus and limits catabolism. Multimodal non-opioid analgesia is emphasized because opioids are frequently out of stock and can cause severe constipation and respiratory depression on unmonitored wards.

6 Novel Context-Specific Components

Local Adaptations
  1. Family Caregiver Integration: Formally training patient relatives to assist with early mobilization, wound inspections, and fluid monitoring. This leverages strong family ties to offset severe nursing shortages on public wards.
  2. Pre-admission Logistics Coordination: Organizing patient transportation and lodging near tertiary hospitals for individuals coming from remote rural villages, preventing delays in preoperative care.
  3. Local Nutritional Substitutes: Creating nutritional guidelines based on local staple grains (such as *teff* gruel, *barley* water, and *sorghum* porridge) rather than expensive commercial nutritional formulas.
  4. Resource-Limited GDFT Titration: Tilting fluid administration using clinical indicators of tissue perfusion (capillary refill, mental status, urine output) instead of high-cost invasive hemodynamic monitors.
  5. Alternative Warming Protocols: Using passive methods, such as double-cotton blankets, reflective surgical drapes, and warmed intravenous fluids, to prevent hypothermia when electric warmers are unavailable.
  6. Post-discharge Communication Networks: Coordinating follow-up using phone calls or local Health Extension Workers (HEWs) in rural villages, ensuring patient safety without requiring long, costly travel back to the tertiary hospital.

📊 Comparison: Standard Western vs. Adapted Ethiopian ERAS

This table summarizes how standard, high-resource ERAS components are adapted to be feasible and effective in Ethiopian tertiary centers:

ERAS Element Standard Western Protocol (HIC) Adapted Ethiopian Protocol (LMIC)
Glycemic Loading Commercial maltodextrin carbohydrate drinks Locally-prepared sweetened black tea or barley water
Nutritional Screening DEXA scans, bio-impedance, specialized formulas Mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) & serum albumin
Anesthetic Block Thoracic epidural analgesia (requires intensive care unit) TAP blocks or local wound infiltration (ward-safe)
Fluid Management Invasive stroke-volume variation monitors Clinical perfusion endpoints (urine output, capillary refill)
Hypothermia Control Active forced-air warming blankets Passive insulation (double blankets, warmed IV fluids)
Nutrition Support Commercial liquid nutrition formulas Gruel/porridge prepared from local staples (*teff*, *barley*)
Patient Mobilization Led by physiotherapists and nursing staff Led by integrated family caregivers
Post-op Follow-up Digital patient portals and home nursing visits Mobile phone calls and Health Extension Worker networks
NaPQN and the NaPQN Registry

To ensure the modified Delphi recommendations translate into actual clinical practice, the Ethiopian National Perioperative Quality Improvement Network (NaPQN) has established a national registry. This registry tracks compliance with the adapted guidelines, providing essential audit loops to refine protocols, reduce surgical site infections, and improve patient recovery across all tertiary centers in the country.

⚡ "Triple-ERAS" Focus

Global health research identifies three core, high-impact elements associated with the greatest reduction in LOS:

Early Ambulation Out of bed within 2–4 hours post-op. Promotes bowel motility and prevents deep vein thrombosis.
Early Feeding Oral fluids/staple gruels on Day 0. Reduces post-operative catabolism.
Early Catheter Removal Catheter removal within 24 hours. Minimizes urinary tract infections and facilitates mobility.
