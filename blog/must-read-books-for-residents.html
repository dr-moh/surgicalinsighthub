Beyond the Textbook: The Narrative Medicine Reading List for Surgical Trainees

Literature Review Resident Well-being June 4, 2026

Cultivating empathy, decision-making clarity, and psychological resilience through non-academic literature during surgical training.

Editorial Review & Clinical Commentary This article is an expanded review and surgical commentary on the original essay "A Resident Shares His Must-Read Books for Fellow Residents" written by Dr. Brent Schnipke, MD and originally published on Doximity Op-Med. In compliance with copyright guidelines, we summarize Dr. Schnipke's core recommendations while adding professional training insights from the Surgical Insight Hub editorial staff.

Medical residency is an intense, transformative period in the development of a physician. Having graduated from medical school and earned the title of "Doctor," trainees find themselves at the very beginning of practical clinical practice. This period is marked by a challenging dichotomy: residents possess access to vast medical knowledge but often lack the practical experience to apply it confidently in high-stakes environments.

This challenge is further compounded by the structured obligations of training—lectures, mandatory readings, case preparation, research, and board examinations. Yet, relying solely on textbooks and academic journals can lead to cognitive exhaustion and emotional isolation. Grounded in the principles of Narrative Medicine (originally established by Dr. Rita Charon at Columbia University), clinical outcomes improve when physicians learn to listen, process, and engage with stories. Non-academic literature provides a softer, reflective language that expands a resident's capacity for empathy, decision-making, and coping with the daily weight of patient care.

Furthermore, under the ACGME Common Program Requirements (Section VI.C), sponsoring institutions are mandated to provide resources that support resident well-being. Augmenting technical studies with narrative reading is a highly effective, personal wellness practice. Below is an expanded review of the 10 books recommended by Dr. Brent Schnipke for residents, followed by our dedicated surgical training commentary for each.

1. When Breath Becomes Air

by Paul Kalanithi, MD
Memoir

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke identifies this short, poignant memoir as a modern staple. It follows the life of a neurosurgery fellow who faces a terminal lung cancer diagnosis just as his training is concluding. The book provides a profound look at mortality and the patient experience from the perspective of an elite surgical trainee.

Surgical Insight Perspective: In surgical fields, we are trained to intervene, repair, and control outcomes. Kalanithi's memoir is a vital reminder of the limits of surgery. For junior residents, it shifts the focus from purely technical success (e.g., "resection margins") to holistic patient goals. It is a masterclass in learning how to have honest, empathetic discussions about prognosis and end-of-life care with patients and their families.

2. Better: A Surgeon's Notes on Performance

by Atul Gawande, MD
Essays

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke highlights this book for its focus on the core values—diligence, doing right, and ingenuity—that allow clinicians to improve their practice. While Gawande’s essays center on surgical practice, his insights into human behavior and systemic improvement are universal.

Surgical Insight Perspective: Diligence is the absolute cornerstone of surgical safety. Gawande's focus on micro-level practices (such as the mechanics of hand hygiene or surgical check-lists) demonstrates how small, repetitive actions directly prevent major post-operative complications. It is an essential read for interns to understand that the tedious tasks of floor work and patient preparation are critical safety barriers.

3. The House of God

by Samuel Shem, MD
Satire

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke includes this classic satire with caveats, noting that much of the content is outdated and gratuitous. However, the book captures a universal psychological truth about the stress of caregiving, utilizing dark humor to expose the coping mechanisms of sleep-deprived trainees.

Surgical Insight Perspective: While Shem’s depiction of residency is extreme, the "laws" of the House of God reflect the raw, defensive psychology of clinical overload. In surgical residency, where physical exhaustion is combined with high patient acuity, recognizing the line between healthy humor and destructive cynicism is key to long-term survival and preventing empathy fatigue.

4. Intern

by Sandeep Jauhar, MD
Memoir

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke recommends this book as a realistic, grounding memoir of a cardiology fellow navigating the clinical hierarchy, sleeplessness, and profound self-doubt of early training. It offers solidarity to residents facing similar transitions.

Surgical Insight Perspective: Imposter syndrome is common in surgical programs, particularly when residents first take trauma calls or lead minor procedures. Jauhar’s honesty about making mistakes and questioning his own competence helps normalize the anxiety of training. It reinforces that confidence and safe execution are built through structured supervision and deliberate practice.

5. Hot Lights, Cold Steel

by Michael J. Collins, MD
Surgical Memoir

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke commends this best-selling memoir of an orthopedic surgery resident at the Mayo Clinic. The book uses humor, wit, and engaging anecdotes to capture the rigors of surgical training and the deep privilege of operating and healing.

Surgical Insight Perspective: This is the ultimate surgical memoir. Collins describes the extreme physical demands and operational hours of surgical training. His reflections on working under demanding attendings, performing late-night reductions, and facing personal limits illustrate the grit and resilience required in surgical programs, while maintaining a love for the craft.

6. Bellevue

by David Oshinsky
History

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke recommends this historical account of Bellevue Hospital to provide critical system context. The book traces three centuries of medicine in America, illustrating how modern medical education and hospital structures were established.

Surgical Insight Perspective: Surgical systems did not develop in a vacuum. Bellevue's history details the emergence of modern trauma care, sterile techniques, and public health initiatives. Understanding how the Halstedian model of surgical training evolved helps residents navigate modern hospital hierarchies and appreciate the systems designed to ensure patient safety.

7. Becoming a Doctor

by Lee Gutkind
Anthology

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke suggests this collection of creative nonfiction essays for its diversity of voices. Written by medical students and junior physicians, it highlights the shared challenges and milestones of medical training, helping to counter the isolation of residency.

Surgical Insight Perspective: Surgical training can often feel like a silo. Reading about the challenges faced by colleagues in emergency medicine, pediatrics, and internal medicine builds interdisciplinary empathy. It also reminds surgical trainees that the anxiety of transitioning from an observer to an active operator is a universal experience.

8. How Doctors Think

by Jerome Groopman, MD
Cognitive Science

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke highlights this book for its investigation into the cognitive processes and biases of physicians. Groopman illustrates how heuristics, anchoring, and premature closure can lead to diagnostic errors, and offers tools to prevent these cognitive traps.

Surgical Insight Perspective: In surgical emergency settings (such as assessing an acute abdomen), diagnostic anchoring can lead to disastrous delays. Assuming a post-operative fever is always atelectasis, for example, can result in a missed anastomotic leak. Groopman's cognitive frameworks help surgeons remain open-minded, challenge initial assumptions, and systematically evaluate differential diagnoses.

9. The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat

by Oliver Sacks, MD
Case Studies

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke frequently recommends this classic by Oliver Sacks. The vignettes treat patients with deep respect, exploring complex neurological deficits while highlighting the human capacity for adaptation and dignity.

Surgical Insight Perspective: Sacks demonstrates the peak of narrative case reporting. Instead of presenting diseases as mere lists of diagnostic criteria, he focuses on how the pathology alters the patient's daily life. This teaches surgical residents to look beyond clinical imaging and lab results, ensuring they treat the person rather than just the disease.

10. What Patients Say, What Doctors Hear

by Danielle Ofri, MD
Communication

Summary of Recommendation: Dr. Schnipke recommends this practical guide to doctor-patient communication. Ofri demonstrates how active listening and clear communication are key to accurate diagnosis, treatment compliance, and building trust.

Surgical Insight Perspective: Effective communication is a vital surgical safety tool. A significant portion of surgical malpractice claims stem not from technical errors, but from communication failures during pre-operative planning and informed consent. Ofri’s strategies help surgical residents actively listen, manage expectations, and establish trust before operating.

ACGME Section VI.C: Sponsoring Institution Mandate

Under the ACGME Common Program Requirements, residency programs must provide a professional, respectful, and supportive environment. Sponsoring institutions must guarantee that trainees have access to mental health support, fatigue mitigation resources, and tools that promote physical and emotional well-being. Incorporating narrative medicine reading into your off-duty routine is a validated method to combat emotional exhaustion and maintain clinical empathy.

⚡ Reading for Well-being

Tips for busy surgical residents to integrate narrative reading into their schedules:

The 15-Minute Rule Read just 15 minutes of non-medical literature before sleep to promote cognitive detachment from hospital stress.
Audiobook Integration Listen to memoirs or narrative history during your daily commute or post-call walks.
Read Short Forms First Start with essays (like Gawande's) or short case vignettes (like Sacks') during shift breaks.
