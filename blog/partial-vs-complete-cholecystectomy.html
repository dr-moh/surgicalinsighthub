Partial vs. Complete Cholecystectomy: A Propensity-Matched Reality Check

Clinical Evidence May 24, 2026

An NLP analysis of 15,000+ cases reveals 5x higher mortality and 3x higher complication rates for partial cholecystectomies. A vital bailout, but at what cost?

The "difficult gallbladder" remains one of the most treacherous encounters in general surgery. When faced with severe inflammation, distorted anatomy, or dense adhesions that obscure the critical view of safety, surgical dogma and SAGES guidelines point to a reliable bailout: the partial (subtotal) cholecystectomy.

While this technique undeniably mitigates the catastrophic risk of major bile duct injury, it brings its own distinct complication profile. A new 2026 study in the Journal of Surgical Research by Nigam et al. leverages Natural Language Processing (NLP) to cut through coding ambiguities and provide a rigorous, propensity-matched look at just how significant that morbidity actually is.

The Methodology: NLP and Propensity Matching

Because partial cholecystectomy lacks specific CPT and ICD-10 codes in clinical practice, capturing accurate data has historically been a challenge. To solve this, the researchers applied a validated BioClinicalBERT NLP classifier to over 15,000 operative reports across 14 hospitals from 2010 to 2025.

This AI-driven approach achieved a 95.9% accuracy rate, successfully identifying 107 partial cholecystectomies. To control for selection bias (as partial cases inherently involve sicker patients and worse pathology), they utilized a 4:1 propensity score matching based on age, sex, race, ethnicity, and comorbidities. The final analytic cohort consisted of 518 patients.

The Data: The Hard Truth

The propensity-matched outcomes were sobering. Even after adjusting for baseline patient factors, the partial cholecystectomy cohort experienced drastically higher complication rates across nearly every metric.

Key Findings (Partial vs. Complete)

  • 30-Day Mortality: 3.8% vs 0.7% (5x higher, P = 0.033)
  • Overall Complications: 75.0% vs 26.6% (2.8x higher, P < 0.001)
  • ICU Admission: 8.7% vs 2.7% (3.3x higher, P = 0.009)
  • Postoperative ERCP: 13.5% vs 1.4% (P < 0.001)
  • Drain Placement: 68.3% vs 19.8% (P < 0.001)

The Takeaway

The five-fold higher mortality after propensity matching suggests that unmeasured intraoperative complexity variables—such as the severity of inflammation, adhesions, and Calot's triangle obliteration—heavily contribute to postoperative risk.

This does not mean partial cholecystectomy should be abandoned. It remains a crucial, potentially life-saving maneuver to avoid devastating biliary and vascular injuries when the critical view of safety is unattainable. However, this data underscores that partial cholecystectomy is a marker of a highly perilous clinical scenario with profound postoperative consequences.

For the surgical practitioner, these findings highlight the absolute necessity of explicit preoperative counseling. Patients presenting with complex gallbladder disease must be informed of the significantly increased risks of bile leak, drain placement, ERCP, and mortality should a partial cholecystectomy become necessary.
