Current Debates in Surgery: What Do Surgeons Actually Prefer?

Debates May 23, 2026

A fascinating dive into a cross-sectional study of 778 Indian surgeons, revealing the stark contrast between standard guidelines and what surgeons would choose for themselves.

If you're anything like me, your surgical training is a blur of post-call rounds, emergency consults, and trying to keep up with the endless stream of "new gold standards" presented at grand rounds. It’s easy to feel like you’re falling behind if your center isn’t doing purely robotic, single-port everything. But how much of that is genuine evidence, and how much is just industry hype pushing its way into the OR?

I recently stumbled upon a fascinating 2012 cross-sectional survey authored by Chintamani, Rohan Khandelwal, et al. They polled 778 Indian surgeons on some of the most contentious issues we face. The results were a massive reality check. It turns out there's a huge gap between what gets hyped at international conferences and what surgeons are actually comfortable doing—especially when they imagine themselves on the operating table.

🤖 Robotic Surgery: Let's Be Real

We've all seen the slick videos of robotic arms peeling grapes. But down in the trenches of general surgery, do surgeons actually buy into it?

In this survey, a massive 74.3% of surgeons admitted that robotic surgery is "a hope for some surgeries and hype for most." Only 12% thought it was going to replace standard lap or open surgery anytime soon. Outside of radical prostates or tricky pelvic dissections, general surgeons aren't ready to ditch traditional laparoscopy just yet.

🩺 Breast Conservation: The Textbook vs. The Ward

Textbooks preach Breast Conservation Surgery (BCS) like it's the only option. But on the ground, 47% of the surveyed surgeons felt that the Modified Radical Mastectomy (MRM) needs to remain the gold standard when adequate infrastructure isn't there. Furthermore, 43.3% noted a serious lack of awareness about BCS among both patients and junior docs. You can't just do a BCS without guaranteed reliable post-op radiation and rock-solid margins. Sometimes, MRM is the safest, most practical call.

🔪 Hernia Repairs: What if it Was Your Groin?

This was the best part of the study: asking surgeons what they would choose if they were the patient.

  • Unilateral Inguinal Hernia: Despite everyone showing off their lap TAPP/TEP skills online, 70.1% of these surgeons said they’d want a classic Open Lichtenstein’s mesh repair for themselves. Tried, true, and done under local or spinal.
  • Bilateral Inguinal Hernia: Only here did the lap preference win out, with 45.6% opting for Lap TEP.
  • Gallbladders: Forget SILS (Single Incision Laparoscopy). 79.5% of surgeons wanted the conventional 4-port lap cholecystectomy for their own biliary colic.

📱 Social Media: Education or Ego?

We all follow surgical influencers on Instagram and Twitter. But 36.8% of the surgeons surveyed worried that social media is turning into a propaganda tool rather than an academic one. It’s too easy to post your one perfect case and hide the complications. We have to be careful we aren't practicing "eminence-based" medicine instead of evidence-based medicine.

🌍 A Trainee’s Take: Practicing in Africa & The Developing World

Reading this study made me think a lot about what we face back home. When you're scrubbing in at a district hospital in Africa or any developing nation, the debates you hear at fancy international conferences just don't apply to our daily grind.

  • Hype vs. Hardware: We struggle with power cuts, limited sterilization, and broken scopes. Dropping millions on a robotic platform isn't just "hype" here—it’s borderline irresponsible. As trainees, our time is way better spent perfecting safe open surgery and mastering conventional laparoscopy where the equipment allows.
  • The "Gold Standard" is a Privilege: That stat about 47% of surgeons sticking with MRMs? I feel that in my bones. BCS sounds great until your patient lives 200 miles away from the nearest functioning radiotherapy machine and can't afford the travel. In our setting, a clean MRM isn't outdated medicine; it’s definitive, life-saving care. We have to treat the patient in front of us, not the textbook.
  • Filtering the Noise: It’s incredibly easy to get imposter syndrome when scrolling through Twitter and seeing guys doing single-incision everything. But this survey proves that even the veterans prefer a rock-solid Open Lichtenstein for themselves. We need to focus on cost-effective, proven interventions that work for our communities instead of chasing surgical clout.

Bottom Line: A good surgeon knows how to operate; a great surgeon knows their environment. Let's focus on mastering the basics that actually save lives on our wards, rather than getting distracted by the shiny new toys we don't even need.
