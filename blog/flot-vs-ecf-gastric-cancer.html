FLOT vs ECF/ECX — Which Neoadjuvant Regimen for Gastric & GEJ Cancer?

Oncology May 14, 2026

A landmark trial breakdown for the operating surgeon. Analyzing the FLOT4 vs MAGIC trial data, regimen selection, and operative implications.

For over a decade, the MAGIC trial (2006) dictated the standard of care for resectable gastric and gastroesophageal junction (GEJ) adenocarcinomas in Europe and much of the world, establishing perioperative ECF (epirubicin, cisplatin, and fluorouracil) or ECX (epirubicin, cisplatin, and capecitabine) as the gold standard. However, the publication of the FLOT4-AIO trial fundamentally shifted this paradigm.

The MAGIC Trial (ECF/ECX)

The MAGIC trial demonstrated that adding perioperative ECF to surgery significantly improved overall survival (OS) compared to surgery alone (36% vs 23% at 5 years). This established the concept that systemic micro-metastatic disease must be treated early.

  • Regimen: 3 pre-operative cycles and 3 post-operative cycles of epirubicin, cisplatin, and 5-FU/capecitabine.
  • Drawbacks: Significant hematological and gastrointestinal toxicity (especially severe emesis, mucositis, and neuropathy) and a relatively low complete pathological response (pCR) rate. Many patients failed to complete the post-operative cycles due to surgical morbidity and slow physical recovery.

The FLOT4-AIO Trial

The German FLOT4 trial compared the MAGIC regimen (ECF/ECX) head-to-head against a docetaxel-based triplet regimen, FLOT (fluorouracil plus leucovorin, oxaliplatin, and docetaxel).

  • Regimen: 4 pre-operative cycles and 4 post-operative cycles of FLOT, administered biweekly.
  • Results: FLOT significantly improved median overall survival to 50 months compared to 35 months with ECF/ECX.
  • Pathological Response: The pathological complete response (pCR) rate was dramatically higher with FLOT (16% vs 6%), leading to a significantly higher proportion of R0 resections.

Clinical Pearl While FLOT is superior in survival and response rates, it is an aggressive regimen. Patient selection is critical. Fit, younger patients (ECOG 0-1) with good performance status are ideal candidates. In older, frail patients, the toxicity of FLOT (particularly neutropenia and diarrhea) may outweigh the benefits, and alternative doublets or upfront surgery might be considered.

Surgical Implications

For the surgeon, the choice of neoadjuvant regimen directly impacts the operative field, surgical planning, and the patient's physiological reserve.

A higher pathological complete response rate (pCR) with FLOT means surgeons are more likely to encounter downstaged tumors, potentially facilitating R0 resections in previously borderline cases. However, oxaliplatin and docetaxel can induce distinct tissue toxicities, hepatotoxicity (blue liver syndrome/hepatic steatosis), and transient immune suppression, which may complicate the dissection or impact anastomotic healing.

Ideally, surgery should be planned 4 to 6 weeks after the completion of the 4th cycle of neoadjuvant FLOT to allow the bone marrow and liver function to recover while avoiding tumor regrowth.

Conclusion

FLOT is now the undisputed standard of care for fit patients with resectable gastric or GEJ adenocarcinoma, replacing ECF/ECX. The focus must remain on multidisciplinary assessment to ensure patients can tolerate the regimen and proceed safely to curative-intent resection.
