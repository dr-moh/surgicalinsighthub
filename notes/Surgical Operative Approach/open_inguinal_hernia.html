Open Inguinal Hernia Repair

Technique: Lichtenstein Tension-Free Level: Core Junior/Senior Time: 45-60 mins
📋

1. Pre-operative Optimization & Preparation

Success starts in the holding bay. Inguinal hernia repair requires precise consent and planning.

  • Consent Criticals: Must mention Chronic Groin Pain (10-12%), Recurrence (1-2%), Mesh infection, and Ischaemic Orchitis.
  • Anesthesia: Local with sedation is preferred for day cases; Spinal or GA for larger/bilateral cases.
  • Positioning: Supine. Ensure the groin is shaved and prepped down to the mid-thigh.
🎯

2. The Operative "Game Plan"

1

Incising the External Oblique

Make a 5-6cm skin incision 2cm above and parallel to the inguinal ligament. Carry dissection down to the External Oblique aponeurosis. Identify the superficial inguinal ring. Incise the aponeurosis in the direction of its fibers, taking care to preserve the Ilioinguinal nerve underneath.

2

Cord Mobilization & Sac Identification

Mobilize the cord at the pubic tubercle. Lift the cord with a Tape. Identify the sac. Indirect sacs are found anterolateral to the cord (reduce or ligated). Direct sacs are a bulge in the floor of the canal (Hesselbach's triangle)—reduce these without opening the fascia transversalis.

3

Mesh Placement (Lichtenstein)

Use a 7x15cm Prolene mesh. Suture the lower edge to the inguinal ligament. Crucially, the mesh must overlap the pubic tubercle by 1.5-2cm to prevent recurrence. Slit the mesh to accommodate the cord at the internal ring. Suture the upper edge to the conjoint tendon with "relaxed" sutures to allow for mesh shrinkage.

💎

3. Technical Pearls & "Bail-out" Strategies

The "Three Nerve" Pearl

Always identify and protect the Ilioinguinal, Iliohypogastric, and the Genital branch of the Genitofemoral nerve. Inguinodynia (chronic pain) is usually caused by nerve entrapment in mesh or sutures.

Bail-out: Large Scrotal Indirect Sac

If the sac is massive and extends deep into the scrotum, do not attempt a full dissection. This leads to massive hematomas and ischaemic orchitis. Instead, open the sac, ensure it's empty, transect it at the mid-canal, and leave the distal portion open to drain.

🛌

4. Post-operative Care & Follow-up

Most patients are discharged within 4 hours of surgery.

  • Analgesia: Regular paracetamol and ibuprofen. Avoid codeine (constipation leads to straining).
  • Activity: Early mobilization. No heavy lifting (>5kg) for 4-6 weeks. Driving allowed once an emergency stop can be performed safely.
  • Follow-up: Clinical review at 2-6 weeks. Check for seroma vs recurrence.
⚠️

5. Complication Management

Managing the most common surgical pitfalls:

Hematoma / Seroma

Common. Most resolve with reassurance and scrotal support. Only aspirate if infected.

Ischaemic Orchitis

Caused by aggressive cord dissection or tight internal ring mesh slit. Leads to testicular atrophy.

Anatomic Landmarks

  • Pubic Tubercle
  • Inguinal Ligament (Poupart's)
  • Inferior Epigastric Vessels
  • Conjoint Tendon

Critical Zones

The Medial Overlap: Recurrence most commonly occurs at the pubic tubercle. Ensure the mesh covers this bone by 2cm.
