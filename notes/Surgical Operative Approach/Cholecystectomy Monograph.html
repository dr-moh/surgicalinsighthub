Cholecystectomy

Laparoscopic, Open, and Subtotal Variants

Biliary Surgery Tokyo Guidelines Strasberg Classification
Upfront Philosophy Laparoscopic cholecystectomy was covered in the first monograph of this series. This entry does not repeat that ground; it expands it. The focus here is on the open technique, the conversion decision, the subtotal cholecystectomy in detail, the acute cholecystitis operation specifically, and the management of intraoperative cholangiography findings.

The residents who get into trouble with cholecystectomy are not the ones who lack procedural knowledge. They are the ones who fail to recognise when to stop, when to convert, and when the anatomy they are seeing is not the anatomy they think it is. This monograph addresses the gap between procedural knowledge and operative judgment.
PILLAR 1 Pre-operative Optimization & Preparation
1.1

Clinical Workup: Beyond the Basics

Tokyo Guidelines 2018 (TG18) Severity Grading

Apply this to every acute cholecystitis presentation:

Tokyo Guidelines 2018 (TG18) for Acute Cholecystitis
GradeCriteriaOperative Implication
Grade I (Mild)Acute cholecystitis without organ dysfunction or severe local findings.Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy within 72 hours; safe and preferred.
Grade II (Moderate)WBC >18,000; symptom duration >72 hours; palpable tender mass; marked pericholecystic inflammation; no organ dysfunction.Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy feasible at experienced centres; percutaneous cholecystostomy if unfit.
Grade III (Severe)Organ dysfunction (cardiovascular, neurological, respiratory, renal, hepatic, haematological).Urgent source control; percutaneous cholecystostomy as bridge; high-risk operative mortality if operated in the acute phase.

Duration of Symptoms

  • <72 hours: The inflammatory process is oedematous and relatively soft. Planes are identifiable. Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy is safe and recommended.
  • 72 hours to 7 days: Inflammation becomes organised. Planes are harder to develop, and the conversion rate increases. Still feasible at experienced centres.
  • >7 days: A fibrotic inflammatory mass (the "difficult gallbladder"). Consider an interval approach or plan for a subtotal cholecystectomy from the outset.

Mirizzi Syndrome

A large gallstone impacted in the Hartmann's pouch or cystic duct compresses the common hepatic duct externally. Presents as jaundice and biliary obstruction on imaging. MRCP or ERCP defines the anatomy before the operation.

Mirizzi Syndrome Classification

Type I involves external compression only, whereas Types II-V involve a fistula between the gallstone and the CHD requiring biliary reconstruction. If Mirizzi is suspected, plan for an open operation with a senior HPB surgeon. Laparoscopic approaches carry a very high bile duct injury risk.

Specific Pathology Considerations

  • Gallbladder empyema: Tense, pus-filled gallbladder on imaging. Decompress with a laparoscopic suction needle through the fundus before applying traction to reduce rupture risk.
  • Gangrenous cholecystitis: Wall necrosis on CT. Higher perforation risk, higher conversion rate, and higher perioperative mortality. Subtotal cholecystectomy should be planned from the outset.
  • Porcelain gallbladder: Calcification of the wall. Selectively calcified walls have higher malignancy risk, indicating cholecystectomy. Diffuse complete calcification has lower risk and can be observed in poor operative candidates. The wall is brittle; use a gauze-covered grasper intraoperatively.
  • Choledocholithiasis: Follow ASGE 2019 criteria. High probability requires ERCP. Intermediate requires MRCP/EUS. Low probability proceeds with cholecystectomy plus intraoperative cholangiography (IOC).
  • Gallbladder polyps: Polyps >10mm require cholecystectomy. Polyps 6-9mm with risk factors (age >50, sessile morphology) require cholecystectomy. Polyps <6mm require surveillance.

1.3

Immediate Pre-operative Preparation

  • Antibiotic prophylaxis: Uncomplicated elective cases have weak evidence for prophylaxis; follow institutional protocol. High-risk elective cases require Cefazolin 1-2g IV within 60 minutes of incision. For acute cholecystitis, continue therapeutic antibiotics perioperatively.
  • Consent additions: For acute cases, explicitly consent for higher conversion rates (20-30%), potential subtotal cholecystectomy, higher bile duct injury risk, and longer hospital stays. If choledocholithiasis is suspected, consent for IOC, potential CBD exploration, or post-operative ERCP.
PILLAR 2 The Operative Game Plan
2.1

Modified Laparoscopic Approach for Acute Inflammation

The standard approach of dissecting the hepatocystic triangle from the lateral peritoneum often fails in severe acute cholecystitis due to obliterated planes, friable walls, and medially displaced CBDs.

Fundus-First (Retrograde) Dissection

Instead of starting at the hepatocystic triangle, begin at the fundus and dissect downward toward the neck. This provides better orientation as you approach the obliterated triangle from a known point.

Fundus-First Retrograde Dissection
  • Decompression: Tense gallbladders cannot be grasped safely. Insert a laparoscopic suction needle through the fundus and aspirate bile/pus before applying traction.
  • Separating adhesions: Bluntly take down omentum, duodenum, and colon adhesions before attempting triangle dissection. Do not enter the triangle through omental adhesions.

Intraoperative Cholangiography (IOC)

Setup involves clipping the gallbladder side of the cystic duct, making a small transverse incision (30-50% of circumference), and inserting the cholangiogram catheter. Inject water-soluble contrast under fluoroscopy.

Intraoperative Cholangiogram (IOC) Anatomy
  • Normal findings: Smooth tapering of the distal CBD, free flow into the duodenum, no filling defects, and bilateral proximal hepatic duct filling.
  • Filling defect (stone): Small stones (<6mm) can be retrieved transcystically. Larger/multiple stones require laparoscopic choledochotomy or post-operative ERCP.
  • No flow to duodenum: Indicates distal obstruction. Reposition patient (head-down tilt) and repeat injection. If confirmed, plan for post-operative ERCP.
  • Proximal ducts not filling: May be an air bubble artifact or misapplied clip on the proximal duct. Check clip positions immediately.
  • Abnormal anatomy: A low insertion of the right posterior sectoral duct into the cystic duct is highly dangerous. If the duct appears to have a double lumen, stop and seek senior input.
2.2

PROCEDURE B: Open Cholecystectomy

Indications include conversion from laparoscopic, dense adhesions from prior surgeries, suspected gallbladder carcinoma, Mirizzi syndrome Types II-V, or severe coagulopathy/haemodynamic instability precluding pneumoperitoneum.

  • Incision: Right subcostal (Kocher) incision is standard, providing excellent exposure to the hepatoduodenal ligament. Upper midline is an alternative but provides inferior right upper quadrant exposure.
  • Exposure: Pack the hepatic flexure inferiorly and the duodenum medially. Elevate the right lobe of the liver with a retractor to expose the hepatoduodenal ligament.
  • Retrograde Technique: For difficult gallbladders, grasp the fundus with a sponge forceps (ring forceps with gauze). Dissect the gallbladder off the liver bed downward using scissors and diathermy in the areolar plane.
  • Tactile Feedback: Open approaches allow palpation. The CBD should feel soft and compressible without a thrill. The hepatic artery is pulsatile. Ensure the cystic duct feels like a distinct tubular structure separate from the CBD.
  • Critical View of Safety (CVS): CVS applies equally to open surgery. The triangle must be cleared, the lower third separated from the liver bed, and two (and only two) structures must be seen entering the gallbladder.
  • Haemostasis: Open surgery allows direct digital pressure and suture ligation (3-0 Prolene figure-of-eight) for liver bed bleeding.
2.3

PROCEDURE C: Subtotal Cholecystectomy

Subtotal cholecystectomy is a planned, correct operation when CVS cannot be achieved. It is indicated when the triangle is obliterated, the wall is gangrenous and cannot be retracted, or the cystic duct cannot be identified after 30-45 minutes of safe dissection.

Subtotal Cholecystectomy: Reconstituting vs Fenestrating
  • Type 1: Reconstituting: The gallbladder is removed above the obliterated triangle, leaving a small infundibular cuff. The mucosal opening is closed from the inside with a purse-string or figure-of-eight 2-0 Vicryl suture. The posterior wall is left attached to the liver. This has a lower post-operative bile leak rate.
  • Type 2: Fenestrating: The gallbladder is opened widely and stones removed, leaving the posterior wall in situ. The cystic duct is not identified or closed. All visible mucosa is ablated with diathermy. The anterior opening is left open (fenestrated) for ongoing drainage. Used when a cuff cannot be safely created.
  • Post-operative Management: A drain adjacent to the liver bed is mandatory for all subtotal procedures. Fenestrating procedures have a high bile leak rate, usually managed effectively with ERCP and stenting to reduce CBD pressure.
2.4

The Conversion Decision: When and How

The decision to convert is clinical judgment, not a failure. The failure is not converting when you should.

  • Indications: Inability to achieve CVS after 30-45 minutes of systematic dissection, suspected bile duct injury, uncontrolled haemorrhage, unexpected pathology (carcinoma, fistula), or technical equipment failure.
  • How to Convert: Do not panic. The conversion incision is rarely an emergency unless dealing with active haemorrhage. Communicate with anaesthesia, plan a right subcostal incision, and re-establish your orientation once open. Laparoscopic and open views differ drastically.
  • When CVS cannot be achieved: Call for a senior surgeon, perform an IOC to clarify anatomy, convert to open, or proceed with a subtotal cholecystectomy.
PILLAR 3 Technical Pearls & Bail-Out Strategies
3.1

Consultant Pearls

Consultant Pearl #1: The Posterior Window Is Where Injuries Begin Most bile duct injuries occur because the posterior peritoneal window was never adequately developed. The anterior window looks open, but without the posterior window, the CBD can be tented into the field and mistaken for the cystic duct. CVS is not achieved until both windows are open.
Consultant Pearl #2: Time at the Triangle Is Not Progress After 30 minutes of dissection in an obliterated triangle without achieving CVS, you are not making progress; you are making danger. Change your strategy. Try fundus-first dissection, perform an IOC, call a senior, or convert to open.
Consultant Pearl #3: The Dropped Stone Is a Medicolegal Time Bomb Bile spillage and stone loss occurs in 10-30% of laparoscopic procedures. Retrieve every stone you can see, use copious irrigation, and explicitly document the spillage in the operative note. Downplaying the spillage is far more damaging if a patient presents years later with a stone-containing abscess.
3.2

The Bail-Out Point

Stop and escalate when:

  • Bile in the field from an unknown source: Stop dissection immediately. Do not place clips until the source is identified. Call a senior.
  • Anatomy changes after placing a clip: If the gallbladder no longer moves freely or the stump is shorter than expected, you may have clipped the CBD. Do not divide. Perform an immediate IOC.
  • Bleeding from the hepatoduodenal ligament: Assume it is the hepatic artery or portal vein. Apply direct pressure, avoid blind cautery, and call a senior.
  • Dissection exceeds 45-60 minutes without CVS: Recognize that the current approach is failing. Change strategy.
  • Suspicion of gallbladder carcinoma: Do not continue laparoscopically. Convert to open to avoid peritoneal seeding.
PILLAR 4 Post-operative Care & Longitudinal Follow-up
4.1

Immediate Recovery

  • Uncomplicated Laparoscopic (Same-day target): Scheduled acetaminophen and NSAIDs. Use port-site and intraperitoneal bupivacaine infiltration to reduce shoulder tip pain. Provide free fluids and a light meal within 2-3 hours. Address PONV prophylactically with ondansetron.
  • Open Cholecystectomy (POD2-5 target): Epidural analgesia for 48-72 hours. Early mobilization. Progress diet as tolerated. Remove drain when output is <30ml/24h and serous.
  • Acute Cholecystitis: Continue therapeutic antibiotics for 5-7 days for gangrenous/perforated cases. Leave drains in situ until output is low and serous.
  • Subtotal Cholecystectomy: Monitor drain output closely. Bilious output indicates a leak requiring ERCP stenting.
4.2

Clinic Follow-up

  • 2-week visit: Inspect wounds. Investigate ongoing right upper quadrant pain or jaundice (rule out retained stone or bile leak). Every gallbladder specimen must be sent for pathology. Incidental carcinoma (T1b or T2) requires oncological referral and potential liver resection.
  • 6-week visit: Return to full activity. Address post-cholecystectomy diarrhoea (occurring in 5-10% due to unregulated bile acid delivery) with bile acid sequestrants like cholestyramine if it persists.
PILLAR 5 Complication Management
5.1

Bile Duct Injury

Intraoperative response: Stop dissection immediately. Call an HPB surgeon. Do not attempt laparoscopic repair. Convert to open, perform an IOC, and plan a definitive Roux-en-Y hepaticojejunostomy for complete transections.

5.2

Post-operative Bile Leak

  • Recognition: Bile-stained drain output, or RUQ pain/fever/rising bilirubin without a drain. CT reveals a biloma.
  • Sources: Cystic duct stump leak (most common), Duct of Luschka (accessory hepatic ductule), or bile duct injury.
  • Correction: For contained leaks, ERCP + biliary stenting is first-line with a >90% success rate. Bilomas without a drain require CT-guided percutaneous drainage in combination with ERCP. Free bile peritonitis requires an emergency laparotomy for washout and source control.
5.3

Retained Common Bile Duct Stone

  • Recognition: Ascending cholangitis (Charcot's triad) immediately post-op, or delayed post-cholecystectomy syndrome with intermittent jaundice and elevated ALP/GGT.
  • Workup: LFTs, Ultrasound, MRCP to confirm. Do not use ERCP purely diagnostically.
  • Correction: Ascending cholangitis requires emergency IV antibiotics and urgent ERCP (within 24-48 hours) for sphincterotomy and extraction. Asymptomatic stones can be managed electively. Stones failing ERCP extraction may require lithotripsy or surgical CBD exploration.
