Bowel Obstruction

Pathophysiology, Diagnostic Workup, and Operative Decision-Making

Emergency GI Bologna Guidelines Closed-Loop Obstruction
The Brutal Rule "When colicky pain goes constant — that bowel is dying. Stop waiting. Operate."

The shift from intermittent colic to constant pain is strangulation until proven otherwise. Miss this sign once and you will bury someone. Every minute after that shift is necrosis accruing.
PILLAR 1 Foundations & Presentation
1.1

Pathophysiology & Classification

Obstruction creates a one-way trap — secretions accumulate, intraluminal pressure rises, and the bowel wall fails in a predictable sequence: distension → ischaemia → necrosis → perforation. Strangulation accelerates the cascade by cutting off venous return first, then arterial inflow — this is why a patient can look deceptively well while bowel is already dying inside.

Mechanism Consequence
Luminal block → 7–9 L/day secretions accumulate Distension, ↑ intraluminal pressure
Third-spacing into bowel wall + peritoneum Hypovolaemia, AKI, haemoconcentration
Vomiting (proximal SBO): H⁺, Cl⁻, K⁺ loss Hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis
Bacterial stasis → gram-neg + anaerobe overgrowth Endotoxin production → bacterial translocation
Venous outflow blocked first → arterial inflow cut Strangulation cascade → necrosis → perforation
Closed-loop: both limbs occluded (volvulus/hernia) No decompression possible. Fastest progression to necrosis.
Laplace's Law: wall tension = (P × r) / thickness Caecum perforates first — largest diameter in the colon
Caecal Thresholds Concern at 9 cm; critical at 12 cm — perforation risk rises sharply beyond this.

Classification Matrix

Vascular status is the most clinically critical axis — it determines theatre urgency, not just the management pathway. A closed-loop obstruction is the worst scenario: both limbs blocked, no decompression possible, ischaemia develops rapidly and silently.

Type Definition Urgency
Simple Lumen blocked; blood supply intact Urgent
Strangulated Blood supply compromised; ischaemia/necrosis Emergency
Closed-loop Both limbs occluded — volvulus or hernia IMMEDIATE
Mechanical Category Examples
Intraluminal Gallstone, bezoar, intussusception, faecal impaction
Intramural Malignancy, Crohn's stricture, radiation stricture, haematoma
Extramural Adhesions, hernias, volvulus, extrinsic tumour or abscess
1.2

SBO & LBO Aetiology

Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)

Adhesions from prior surgery account for nearly three-quarters of all SBO. In a patient with no prior abdominal surgery (virgin abdomen), think hernia first. Femoral hernias are small, easily missed in the groin crease, and carry the highest strangulation risk of any hernia type.

Cause Frequency Key Point
Adhesions 60–75% Post-op. #1 overall cause of SBO.
Hernia (incarcerated)10–15% Femoral = highest strangulation risk. Always examine the groin.
Neoplasm 5–10% Primary small bowel tumour or extrinsic compression
Crohn's stricture 5% Chronic transmural inflammation → fibrotic stricture
Intussusception 1–3% Adults: always exclude malignant lead point
Gallstone ileus <1% Bouveret's syndrome or Rigler's triad on AXR

Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)

Colorectal carcinoma dominates — most commonly at the sigmoid colon where the lumen is narrowest. Volvulus demographics differ starkly: sigmoid volvulus in elderly, institutionalised patients; caecal volvulus in younger adults with incomplete peritoneal fixation.

Cause Frequency Key Point
Colorectal carcinoma 60–70% Sigmoid most common site. Often presents late.
Sigmoid volvulus 10–15% Elderly, institutionalised, chronic constipation
Caecal volvulus 5% Younger patients; incomplete peritoneal fixation
Diverticular stricture 5–10% Chronic diverticulitis → fibrotic narrowing
Ogilvie's syndrome Functional No mechanical cause; colonic atony in ICU/post-op
1.3

Clinical Presentation

The four cardinal features — pain, vomiting, distension, constipation — vary in timing and severity by obstruction level. Proximal SBO presents with early, severe vomiting and minimal distension; distal SBO/LBO presents with prominent distension and late vomiting. Absolute constipation means no flatus and no stool.

Feature SBO LBO
Pain Colicky, central, periumbilical Colicky, lower abdominal / hypogastric
Vomiting Early, profuse — bilious → faeculent Late, faeculent
Distension Less prominent (minimal if proximal) Prominent, tympanic
Constipation Late sign Early presenting feature
1.4

Strangulation & Special Cases

Organ Failure & Strangulation Red Flags The clinical exam is unreliable in early strangulation — a patient can look deceptively well while bowel is already non-viable. Escalation markers include:
  • Colicky pain shifting to constant pain — the single most important clinical sign
  • Localised tenderness, rebound, or guarding
  • Fever >38.5°C combined with tachycardia >100 bpm
  • Serum lactate >2 mmol/L or WBC >15 × 10⁹/L
  • CT findings: pneumatosis intestinalis, portal venous gas, absent bowel wall enhancement

Special Clinical Entities

Condition Classic Signs & Pathophysiology
Gallstone ileus Rigler's triad: pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic gallstone. Occurs via cholecystoduodenal fistula.
Sigmoid volvulus Coffee bean / omega loop sign. Apex pointing to RUQ. Common in elderly.
Caecal volvulus Kidney bean sign. Caecum displaced to LUQ. Younger patients.
Intussusception Redcurrant jelly stool + sausage-shaped RIF mass. Peak age 3m–3y in children; adult cases indicate malignancy.
Ogilvie's syndrome Massive colonic distension without mechanical obstruction on CT. Common in ICU or post-op.
PILLAR 2 Initial Resuscitation & Workup
2.1

Bedside Actions & Targets

Immediate management must precede diagnostic imaging. Time is bowel — protect the gut and decompress immediately.

  1. IV Access & Resuscitation: Establish two large-bore IVs; start **Hartmann's** fluid immediately.
  2. NG Tube Decompression: Insert wide-bore Ryles tube to free drainage. Prevents aspiration and decompresses stomach.
  3. Urinary Catheterization: Place Foley catheter to monitor output strictly.
  4. Nil By Mouth: Restrict all oral intake immediately.
  5. Serial Evaluation: Re-examine abdomen every **4–6 hours** by same surgical team to detect strangulation early.
  6. Analgesia: Administer IV opioids; do not withhold analgesia as it does not mask peritonitis.

Resuscitation Targets

Parameter Target Value
MAP ≥65 mmHg
Urine Output ≥0.5 mL/kg/h
Serum Lactate <2 mmol/L
K⁺ pre-op Correct if <3.5 mmol/L
Fluid of Choice Hartmann's solution is preferred over 0.9% Normal Saline. Normal Saline causes a hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis, worsening the patient's biochemical profile.
2.2

Electrolyte Disturbances

Electrolyte shifts reflect the level and duration of bowel obstruction. Recognising these patterns is high-yield for clinical management and board examinations.

Clinical Scenario Electrolyte & Acid-Base Profile
Proximal SBO (Profuse vomiting) Hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis (loss of HCl and K⁺ in gastric secretions).
Late Strangulated Obstruction Lactic metabolic acidosis (tissue hypoperfusion and anaerobic metabolism in ischemic bowel).
Aggressive 0.9% NaCl Infusion Hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis (renal bicarbonate wasting due to high chloride load).
2.3

Investigations & AXR Patterns

CT with IV contrast is the undisputed gold standard for bowel obstruction. While plain radiography is useful for quick screening, it misses the etiology in half of SBO cases.

Investigation Clinical Role / Finding
★ GOLD CT Abd/Pelvis with IV contrast Identifies transition point, cause, and vascular compromise. Sensitivity >95%.
AXR (Erect + Supine) Screening for dilated loops, gas patterns, and volvulus. Misses cause in 50%.
Erect CXR Routinely ordered to exclude pneumoperitoneum (free air under diaphragm).
Serum Lactate Best single marker for ischemia. >2 mmol/L indicates hypoperfusion.
Gastrografin Challenge 100 mL oral/NG. Read at 24 h. Contrast in colon = partial SBO; predicts resolution.

Pathognomonic AXR & CT Patterns

Plain film distinctions are board-critical: valvulae conniventes cross the full width of the lumen, whereas haustra do not.

AXR Sign Radiological Pattern Diagnosis
Valvulae conniventes Central loops, lines cross full lumen width Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)
Haustra Peripheral loops, incomplete septa Large Bowel Obstruction (LBO)
Coffee bean / Omega loop Bent inner tube appearance pointing to RUQ Sigmoid Volvulus
Kidney bean sign Dilated caecum displaced to LUQ Caecal Volvulus
Rigler's triad Pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic gallstone Gallstone Ileus
Pneumatosis intestinalis Gas bubbles within the bowel wall Strangulation — urgent theatre
PILLAR 3 Management Playbook
3.1

Non-operative & Gastrografin

Non-operative trial is indicated **only for partial adhesive SBO** in the absence of strangulation. The window of trial is strictly **48 to 72 hours**.

Trial Protocol

  • NG tube to free drainage + strict fluid balance
  • Aggressive Hartmann's hydration
  • Serial abdominal examinations every 4 hours
  • Serial biochemical checks (WBC, CRP, Lactate)
Bail-out Marker Abandon non-operative management immediately if the patient develops fever, worsening pain, leukocytosis, metabolic acidosis, or signs of localized peritonitis.

Gastrografin Protocol (Bologna Guidelines)

Gastrografin is a water-soluble, hyperosmolar contrast medium that is both diagnostic and therapeutic in adhesive partial SBO. By drawing fluid into the lumen, it reduces bowel wall edema and stimulates peristalsis.

  1. Administer **100 mL** of Gastrografin orally or via NG tube.
  2. Obtain an abdominal X-ray or CT scan at **24 hours**.
  3. Contrast in the colon: Predicts successful resolution of SBO without surgery.
  4. No contrast in the colon: Predicts failure of conservative trial; proceed to operative management.
Efficacy The Gastrografin protocol reduces the overall emergency operative rate by approximately 20% in adhesive partial SBO.
3.2

Operative Procedures & Viability

Operative intervention is mandatory for complete mechanical obstruction, hernias, tumors, volvulus, or whenever strangulation is suspected.

Procedure Indication Technical Pearls
Adhesiolysis Adhesive SBO Laparoscopic preferred. Open entry via Hasson technique if prior midline laparotomy.
Hernia reduction & repair Incarcerated hernia Assess viability of herniated bowel *before* reducing it into the abdomen.
Resection & Anastomosis Necrotic bowel; stable patient Ensure healthy, bleeding margins with good vascularity.
Resection & Stoma Necrotic bowel; unstable patient Perform stoma (e.g. Hartmann's) in gross contamination or hemodynamic instability.
Enterotomy & Lithotomy Gallstone ileus Extract stone through enterotomy proximal to obstruction. Close transversely.

Intraoperative Bowel Viability Assessment

Assessing bowel viability is a clinical skill that determines whether to resect or preserve. Never base resection decisions on pre-operative imaging alone.

Bowel Viability Status Clinical Signs Intraoperative Action
Viable Pink color, active peristalsis, visible arterial pulsations, bleeds when cut. Preserve and close.
Non-viable Black, green, or dull gray color; flaccid; no peristalsis; no pulsations. Resect immediately.
Doubtful / Questionable Dusky color, sluggish peristalsis, weak pulsations. Apply warm saline packs for 5 minutes + 100% O₂. Still doubtful → resect.
Extensive doubtful segment Questionable viability over a very long segment. Keep in situ + plan a second-look laparotomy in 24–48 hours.
3.3

Volvulus Management

Sigmoid and caecal volvulus require entirely different surgical pathways. Sigmoid volvulus can often be managed endoscopically first, while caecal volvulus is an immediate operative emergency.

Sigmoid Volvulus

Endoscopic decompression is the first-line treatment for uncomplicated cases.

  1. Endoscopic Decompression: Insert sigmoidoscope/colonoscopy to untwist. Success rate **70–85%**. Leave a flatus tube in situ for 24–48 h.
  2. Elective Resection: Elective sigmoid colectomy must be performed **during the same admission**. Recurrence without surgery is 50–90%.

Caecal Volvulus

Endoscopic decompression is **completely contraindicated** due to high perforation risk and technical failure.

  • Proceed directly to **emergency laparotomic right hemicolectomy**.
  • Perform primary ileocolic anastomosis in stable patients; end stoma in unstable patients.
  • Caecopexy is associated with high recurrence and is rarely recommended.
3.4

Malignant LBO & Ogilvie's

Malignant Large Bowel Obstruction

In stable patients presenting with left-sided colorectal cancer obstruction, a Self-Expanding Metal Stent (SEMS) acts as a bridge to elective surgery. This avoids emergency stoma operations and allows for standard staging.

Clinical Scenario Surgical Management
Stable, left-sided obstruction Deploy SEMS under fluoroscopy → elective resection in 4–8 weeks.
Right-sided malignant obstruction Emergency right hemicolectomy + primary ileocolic anastomosis.
Perforation / Peritonitis Emergency resection + end stoma (Hartmann's procedure).

Ogilvie's Syndrome (Acute Colonic Pseudo-obstruction)

A functional colonic atony without mechanical cause. A **diagnosis of exclusion** — CT must rule out mechanical obstruction first.

Caecal Diameter Therapeutic Action
<9 cm Conservative: IV fluids, stop opioids, correct electrolytes, mobilize.
9–12 cm IV Neostigmine 2 mg slow over 3–5 mins. Atropine must be at bedside.
>12 cm (No peritonitis) Colonoscopic decompression with decompression tube insertion.
Perforation / Ischemia Emergency resection + stoma or caecostomy.
Neostigmine Contraindications Do not administer neostigmine in mechanical LBO, severe bradycardia, active bronchospasm, or unstable coronary ischemia.
PILLAR 4 Post-Op Care & Eponyms
4.1

Post-op Care & Stomas

Post-operative care is guided by ERAS (Enhanced Recovery After Surgery) protocols. Close monitoring is required to detect early anastomotic leak and ileus.

Clinical Domain Post-operative Action Plan
NG Tube Exit Remove when output is <200 mL/day and bowel sounds / flatus return.
Analgesia IV PCA (opioids) transitioned to paracetamol. Avoid NSAIDs due to anastomotic leak risk.
VTE Prophylaxis Enoxaparin 40 mg subcut OD starting night of surgery + compression stockings.
ERAS Pathway Early mobilization day 1; sips of water day 1; advance diet as tolerated.

Stoma Management Playbook

  • High-output ileostomy (>1000 mL/day): Risk of severe dehydration and AKI. Treat with oral rehydration salts (ORS), loperamide, codeine, or octreotide.
  • Hartmann's Reversal: Typically planned at **3 to 6 months** post-operatively. Must evaluate the rectal stump via contrast enema and colonoscopy first to ensure healing.
4.2

Follow-up & Eponyms

Longitudinal Follow-up Schedule

  • 10–14 Days: Clinic visit for surgical wound inspection and suture/staple removal.
  • 4–6 Weeks: Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) oncology referral if the obstruction was due to CRC.
  • 4–8 Weeks: Plan elective resection if SEMS was successfully deployed as a bridge.

Classical Board Eponyms

Eponym Surgical / Pathological Definition
Bouveret's syndrome Gastric outlet obstruction caused by a gallstone impacted in the duodenum.
Rigler's triad Pneumobilia + small bowel obstruction + ectopic gallstone (diagnostic of gallstone ileus).
Ogilvie's syndrome Acute colonic pseudo-obstruction in the absence of a mechanical blockage.
Laplace's Law Wall tension = (Pressure × radius) / wall thickness. Explains caecal perforation risk.
Hartmann's procedure Sigmoidectomy + end colostomy + closure of the rectal stump.

5.2

Distinctions That Fail People

Typical Exam / Viva Question High-Yield Answer & Rationale
What is the most common cause of SBO in a virgin abdomen? Hernia. Adhesions cannot form without previous abdominal surgery.
What is the earliest clinical sign of bowel strangulation? Colicky pain shifting to constant, localized pain.
Why does the caecum perforate first in a distal LBO? Laplace's Law. The caecum has the largest resting radius in the colon, generating the highest wall tension.
Can you decompress a caecal volvulus endoscopically? NO. Endoscopic untwisting is contraindicated. Go straight to theatre.
How do you manage adult intussusception conservatively? You don't. Adults require resection due to a highly likely malignant lead point.
What must you have drawn up at the bedside before neostigmine? Atropine. Profound symptomatic bradycardia is a predictable side effect of neostigmine.
What is the IV fluid of choice in bowel obstruction? Hartmann's. 0.9% NaCl causes a hyperchloraemic acidosis.
Ward Round Safety Checklist
  • groin examination: Always examine the groin of an elderly woman presenting with SBO to rule out a femoral hernia.
  • Sigmoid volvulus: Decompress endoscopically but book their elective sigmoidectomy before discharge (recurrence is 50-90%).
  • Pneumatosis intestinalis: If seen on CT, prepare the patient for theatre regardless of how clinically well they appear.
