Bowel Obstruction

Pathophysiology, Diagnostic Workup, and Operative Decision-Making
Surgical Insight Hub
Monograph Series · ABSITE
PGY-3 Framework · Page 1 / 9
The Brutal Rule
"When colicky pain goes constant, that bowel is dying. Stop waiting. Operate."
The shift from intermittent colic to constant pain is strangulation until proven otherwise. Miss this sign once and you will bury someone. Every minute after that shift is necrosis accruing.
01

Pathophysiology & Classification

Obstruction creates a one-way trap: secretions accumulate, intraluminal pressure rises, and the bowel wall fails in a predictable sequence: distension → ischaemia → necrosis → perforation. Strangulation cuts off venous return first, then arterial inflow. This is why a patient can look deceptively well while bowel is already dying.

Mechanism Consequence
Luminal block → 7–9 L/day secretions accumulate Distension, ↑ intraluminal pressure
Third-spacing into bowel wall + peritoneum Hypovolaemia, AKI, haemoconcentration
Vomiting (proximal SBO): H⁺, Cl⁻, K⁺ loss Hypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis
Bacterial stasis → gram-neg + anaerobe overgrowth Endotoxin production → bacterial translocation
Venous outflow blocked first → arterial inflow cut Strangulation cascade → necrosis → perforation
Closed-loop: both limbs occluded (volvulus/hernia) No decompression possible. Fastest progression to necrosis.
Laplace's Law: wall tension = (P × r) / thickness Caecum perforates first (largest diameter in the colon)
Caecal Thresholds: Concern at 9 cm; critical at 12 cm (perforation risk rises sharply beyond this).
02

Classification Matrix

Vascular status is the most clinically critical axis: it determines theatre urgency, not just the management pathway. A closed-loop SBO is an absolute emergency.

Type Definition Urgency
Simple Lumen blocked; blood supply intact Urgent
Strangulated Blood compromised; ischaemia/necrosis Emergency
Closed-loop Both limbs occluded (volvulus or hernia) IMMEDIATE
Category Examples
Intraluminal Gallstone, bezoar, intussusception
Intramural Crohn's/radiation stricture, malignancy
Extramural Adhesions, hernias, volvulus, abscess

03

SBO Aetiology

Adhesions from prior surgery account for nearly three-quarters of all SBO. In a patient with no prior abdominal surgery (virgin abdomen), think hernia first. Femoral hernias carry the highest strangulation risk of any hernia type.

Cause Freq Key Point
Adhesions 60–75% Post-op. #1 overall cause of SBO.
Hernia (incarcerated) 10–15% Femoral = highest strangulation risk. Always examine the groin.
Neoplasm 5–10% Primary small bowel tumour or extrinsic compression
Crohn's stricture 5% Chronic transmural inflammation → fibrotic stricture
Intussusception 1–3% Adults: always exclude malignant lead point
Gallstone ileus <1% Bouveret's syndrome or Rigler's triad on AXR
Virgin abdomen SBO: Hernia until proven otherwise. Adhesions cannot form without a prior surgical insult or peritonitis.
04

LBO Aetiology

Colorectal carcinoma dominates, most commonly at the sigmoid colon where the lumen is narrowest. Volvulus demographics differ starkly by age and chronic constipation status.

Cause Freq Key Point
Colorectal carcinoma 60–70% Sigmoid most common site. Often presents late.
Sigmoid volvulus 10–15% Elderly, institutionalised, chronic constipation
Caecal volvulus 5% Younger patients; incomplete peritoneal fixation
Diverticular stricture 5–10% Chronic diverticulitis → fibrotic narrowing
Ogilvie's syndrome Functional No mechanical cause; colonic atony in ICU/post-op
LBO with competent ileocaecal valve: Closed-loop obstruction of the colon. The caecum takes the full pressure load and will perforate first (Laplace's Law).
05

Clinical Presentation: SBO vs LBO

The four cardinal features (pain, vomiting, distension, constipation) vary in timing by obstruction level. Proximal obstruction vomits early and distends minimally; distal obstruction distends massively and vomits late.

Feature SBO LBO
Pain Colicky, central, periumbilical Colicky, lower abdominal / hypogastric
Vomiting Early, profuse (bilious → faeculent) Late, faeculent
Distension Less prominent (minimal if proximal) Prominent, tympanic
Constipation Late sign Early presenting feature

06

Strangulation Red Flags

The clinical exam is unreliable in early strangulation: a patient can look deceptively well while bowel is already non-viable on CT. Lactate is your best single biochemical marker.

Operate, Do Not Wait
  • Colicky pain shifting to constant pain: the single most important clinical sign
  • Localised tenderness or peritonism / guarding
  • Fever >38.5°C combined with tachycardia >100 bpm
  • Serum lactate >2 mmol/L or WBC >15 × 10&sup9;/L
  • CT: pneumatosis intestinalis, portal venous gas, absent bowel wall enhancement
07

Special Presentations

Each entity below has a pathognomonic AXR or CT finding; recognise these instantly. Gallstone ileus and volvulus are classic traps.

Condition Classic Signs & Pathophysiology
Gallstone ileus Rigler's triad: pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic calcified gallstone. Cholecystoduodenal fistula.
Sigmoid volvulus Coffee bean / omega loop sign. Apex pointing to RUQ. Elderly, institutionalised.
Caecal volvulus Kidney bean sign. Caecum displaced to LUQ. Younger patient.
Ogilvie's syndrome Post-op or ICU. Massive colonic distension. No transition point on CT.
08

Bedside: Do These First

  1. IV access ×2 + Hartmann's resuscitation immediately
  2. NG tube to free drainage: decompress, prevent aspiration
  3. Urinary catheter: target UO ≥0.5 mL/kg/h
  4. Nil by mouth
  5. Serial abdominal exam every 4–6 hours to detect early strangulation
  6. Examine all hernial orifices + PR examination: mandatory every time
09

Resuscitation Targets

Parameter Target
MAP ≥65 mmHg
Urine output ≥0.5 mL/kg/h
Lactate <2 mmol/L
Preferred fluid Hartmann's (Ringer's lactate)

Investigations & AXR Patterns

Page 4 / 9
11

Investigations

CT abdomen with IV contrast is the gold standard and must be obtained whenever there is diagnostic uncertainty or concern for strangulation. AXR is fast but misses the cause in 50% of SBO.

Test Key Finding / Role
★ GOLD CT Abd/Pelvis IV contrast Transition point, cause, strangulation signs. Sensitivity >95%. Identifies cause in 90%.
AXR (Erect + Supine) Dilated loops, volvulus patterns, Rigler's triad. Rapid first-line imaging.
Erect CXR Free air under diaphragm = perforation. Always obtain.
Serum Lactate >2 mmol/L = hypoperfusion. >4 = shock. Best single strangulation marker.
FBC / U&Es WBC >15 = strangulation. Correct K⁺ before anaesthesia.
Gastrografin challenge 100 mL oral/NG. Contrast in colon at 24 h = partial SBO.
Never delay CT when you suspect strangulation. The exam and bloods can mislead, but CT rarely does.
12

AXR Patterns: Memorise These

Valvulae conniventes cross the full width of the small bowel lumen, while haustra do not. Volvulus signs are pathognomonic and require immediate action.

Sign Pattern Diagnosis
Valvulae conniventes Central loops, lines cross full lumen width SBO
Haustra Peripheral, incomplete septa, large calibre LBO
Coffee bean / omega loop Apex → RUQ; bent inner tube appearance Sigmoid volvulus
Kidney bean sign Caecum displaced to LUQ Caecal volvulus
Rigler's triad Pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic calcified gallstone Gallstone ileus
Pneumatosis intestinalis Gas within the bowel wall Strangulation: theatre now
Portal venous gas Branching intrahepatic gas on CT/AXR Advanced ischaemia: grave sign

Management: SBO Protocols

Page 5 / 9
13

SBO: Non-operative Management

Appropriate only for partial adhesive SBO without strangulation. The window is 48–72 hours maximum. Beyond that, failure to improve mandates operative intervention.

  • Indications: Partial SBO, stable, no peritonitis.
  • NG tube to free drainage
  • IV Hartmann's + electrolyte replacement
  • Nil by mouth
  • Serial exam every 4–6 hours
  • Window: Up to 72 h. Abandon immediately if strangulation signs, deterioration, or no improvement at 72 h.
Bail-out Marker

Abandon trial immediately if patient develops fever, worsening pain, leukocytosis, metabolic acidosis, or signs of localized peritonitis.

14

Gastrografin Protocol (Bologna Guidelines)

Gastrografin is both diagnostic and therapeutic in adhesive SBO. Its hyperosmolar properties draw fluid into the bowel lumen, reducing mural oedema and potentially resolving SBO.

  1. Administer 100 mL gastrografin orally or via NG tube
  2. Obtain AXR or CT at 24 hours
  3. Contrast in colon at 24 h = SBO resolved → continue non-operative management
  4. Contrast NOT in colon at 24 h = complete SBO → operative management
Mechanism: hyperosmolar → draws fluid into lumen → reduces bowel wall oedema. Reduces operative rate by ~20% in SBO.
15

Bowel Viability: Intraoperative Assessment

Viability assessment is a clinical skill: four criteria (pink colour, active peristalsis, arterial pulsations, and bleeding when cut). When in doubt after a warm wrap, resect.

Status Signs Action
Viable Pink, peristalsis, pulsations, bleeds when cut Preserve
Non-viable Black or green, no peristalsis, no pulsations Resect immediately
Doubtful Borderline colour, sluggish peristalsis Warm wrap × 5 min. Still doubtful → resect.
Extensive uncertain Large segment questionable viability Second look laparotomy at 24–48 h

Management: SBO Operative Playbook

Page 6 / 9
16

Operative Adhesiolysis (Laparoscopic & Open)

Operative intervention is mandatory for complete SBO or suspected strangulation. In prior abdominal surgeries, enter with caution.

Critical Access Rules for Prior Laparotomy Scars:
  • Hasson Entry (Open Technique): Make incision at least 5 cm away from prior laparotomy scars. Enter under direct vision.
  • Palmer's Point Access: If prior extensive midline scars exist, establish entry at left upper quadrant (Palmer's point).
  • Pneumoperitoneum: Establish pneumoperitoneum at a safe pressure (12 mmHg). Inspect for sub-umbilical adhesions.
  • Initial Port Placement & Exposure: Place working ports under direct vision. Elevate abdominal wall to expose loops.
  • Systematic Inspection: Work systematically from known normal zone to the abnormal zone. Start at the ileocaecal valve and follow collapsed terminal ileum retrogradely.
  • Bowel Retraction Pearl: Retract dilated loops gently using graspers. Grasp only the mesentery. Never grasp the distended, friable bowel wall directly: it tears easily.
  • Dissection Method: Perform "cold" dissection with sharp scissors or harmonic shears near the bowel wall. Avoid hot diathermy near the gut wall to eliminate thermal spread.
  • Bail-out Strategy: If loops are fused in a dense "cocoon" and planes are unidentifiable, perform immediate conversion to open laparotomy to prevent unrecognized enterotomies.
17

Small Bowel Resection & Anastomosis

When bowel viability is compromised, resection is mandatory. A stable resident must master both stapled and handsewn techniques.

  1. Margin Selection: Select resection margins at least 5–10 cm proximal and distal to the necrotic/non-viable border in clearly viable tissue.
  2. Mesenteric Division: Create windows in the mesentery. Divide mesentery in a V-shape. Ligate mesenteric vessels securely using 3-0 Silk ties.
  3. Bowel Division: Apply linear cutting staplers (e.g. GIA 60 or 80 mm, blue load/3.5mm staples) across margins. Fire and divide. Remove specimen.
  4. Functional Side-to-Side Anastomosis (Stapled):
    - Align proximal and distal loops in isoperistaltic or antiperistaltic side-to-side configuration. Place 3-0 Silk stay sutures.
    - Exise small corners of staple lines at antimesenteric borders of both loops (enterotomies). Insert forks of linear cutting stapler fully into both. Align and fire.
    - Close the common enterotomy opening in two layers: an inner continuous transmural layer (3-0 Vicryl or PDS) and an outer interrupted seromuscular layer (3-0 Silk Lembert sutures).
  5. Alternative: Handsewn Two-Layer Anastomosis (End-to-End):
    - Inner Layer: running continuous 3-0 Vicryl or PDS, starting at posterior wall and progressing anteriorly using Connell/Cushing stitch to ensure mucosal inversion.
    - Outer Layer: reinforce with interrupted 3-0 Silk Lembert sutures placed 2-3 mm apart, fully invaginating the inner layer.
  6. Mesenteric Defect Closure: Close mesenteric defect securely using continuous 3-0 Silk or Vicryl suture. This is mandatory to prevent internal herniation.

Management: LBO & Volvulus

Page 7 / 9
18

Sigmoid & Caecal Volvulus Resections

Sigmoid and caecal volvulus represent distinct clinical and technical entities requiring different operative maneuvers.

  • Sigmoid Volvulus Resection: Indicated when endoscopic decompression fails or as definitive elective surgery during same admission.
  • Mobilization: Mobilize dilated sigmoid loop by dividing lateral peritoneal reflection (white line of Toldt).
  • Ureteric Identification: Identify and protect left ureter and gonadal vessels.
  • Resection: Dissect and divide mesosigmoid. Ligate branches. Resect loop using staplers.
  • Reconstruction: Perform Hartmann's procedure (end colostomy) or primary stapled end-to-end colorectal anastomosis.
  • Caecal Volvulus Resection: Always requires surgical intervention. Endoscopic decompression has no role.
  • Exposure: Midline laparotomy. Elevate mobile caecum and ascending colon.
  • Hemicolectomy: Mobilize right colon. Divide terminal ileum and transverse colon.
  • Vascular Control: Ligate ileocolic and right colic arteries.
  • Anastomosis: Re-establish continuity via stapled side-to-side ileotransverse anastomosis. Close defect.
19

Malignant LBO: CRC

In a stable patient with left-sided colorectal cancer obstruction, a Self-Expanding Metal Stent (SEMS) acts as a bridge to elective surgery.

Clinical Scenario Surgical Management
Stable, left-sided obstruction Deploy SEMS under fluoroscopy → elective resection in 4–8 weeks.
Right-sided malignant obstruction Emergency right hemicolectomy + primary ileocolic anastomosis.
Perforation / Peritonitis Emergency resection + end stoma (Hartmann's procedure).
20

Ogilvie's Syndrome: Neostigmine Protocol

Neostigmine restores parasympathetic tone to the colon. Have atropine drawn up before you inject it.

Caecal Diameter Therapeutic Action
<9 cm Conservative: IV fluids, stop opioids, correct electrolytes, mobilize.
9–12 cm IV Neostigmine 2 mg slow over 3–5 mins (Atropine must be at bedside).
>12 cm Colonoscopic decompression with decompression tube insertion.
Perforation / Ischemia Emergency resection + stoma.
Neostigmine Safety Checklist: Ensure a 12-lead ECG is completed before administration. Have Atropine (0.6–1.0 mg IV) pre-drawn and available at bedside. Infuse Neostigmine 2.0 mg IV slowly over a minimum of 3 to 5 minutes. Keep patient supine and monitor vitals.

Post-op Care & Eponyms

Page 8 / 9
21

Intussusception

In children, intussusception is idiopathic in the vast majority; air enema reduction is first-line. In adults, there is almost always a malignant lead point requiring operative resection.

Group Cause Management
Children (peak 3m–3y) Idiopathic (lead point rare) Air-enema reduction first-line. Success 75–90%. Avoids surgery.
Adults Malignant lead point in majority Operate. No role for radiological reduction. Always resect.
22

Post-op Care (0–72 h)

Management centres on four pillars: adequate resuscitation, early detection of anastomotic leak, VTE prevention, and ERAS-driven early recovery. Avoid NSAIDs after any anastomosis.

Domain Post-operative Action Plan
NG Tube Exit Remove when output is <200 mL/day and bowel sounds / flatus return.
Analgesia IV PCA (opioids) transitioned to paracetamol. Avoid NSAIDs due to leak risk.
VTE Prophylaxis Enoxaparin 40 mg subcut OD starting night of surgery + compression stockings.
ERAS Pathway Early mobilization day 1; sips of water day 1; advance diet as tolerated.
23

Stoma Management

Problem Action
High-output (>1000 mL/day) ORS + loperamide + codeine.
Hartmann's Reversal Assess at 3–6 months with CT + colonoscopy rectal stump.
24

Follow-up Schedule

Timing Action
10–14 days Wound review, staple/suture removal
4–6 weeks Oncology MDT for malignant cases
4–8 weeks Elective resection if SEMS was used

Distinctions That Fail People

Typical Exam / Viva Question High-Yield Answer & Rationale
SBO in a virgin abdomen Hernia. Adhesions cannot form without prior surgery.
First sign of strangulation Colicky pain shifting to constant, localized pain.
Why the caecum perforates first in LBO Laplace's Law. Largest radius = highest wall tension.
Caecal volvulus: endoscopic decompression? NO. Right colon inaccessible. Operate immediately.
Adult intussusception: non-operative? NO. Operate. Always exclude malignant lead point.
Neostigmine: what to have at bedside Atropine drawn up (bradycardia is predictable).
IV fluid of choice in bowel obstruction Hartmann's. 0.9% NaCl causes hyperchloraemic acidosis.
28

Ward Round: Never Miss

Never-Miss Safety Checklist
  • Femoral hernia in SBO: examine the groin every time, never rely on admission notes.
  • Sigmoid volvulus decompressed → book elective colectomy before discharge (50–90% recurrence).
  • Pneumatosis intestinalis on CT → operate regardless of how clinically well the patient appears.
  • Portal venous gas on CT → prepare for bowel resection immediately (advanced bowel necrosis).
  • Constant pain in obstruction = strangulation until proven otherwise = immediate theatre.
