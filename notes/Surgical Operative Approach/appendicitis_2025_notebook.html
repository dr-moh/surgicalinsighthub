SECTION 1 The Operative Atlas
1.1

Surgical Anatomy: Fascial Planes, Neurovascular Danger Zones & Critical Variations

Anatomical Position of the Appendix

  • Base arises from the posteromedial cecal wall, 2–3 cm below the ileocecal valve, at the convergence of the three taeniae coli, reliable intraoperative landmark
  • McBurney's point: junction of lateral one-third and medial two-thirds of a line from ASIS to umbilicus; corresponds to the appendiceal base in most patients

Positional Variations

PositionFrequencyClinical Implication
Retrocecal65–70%RLQ pain atypical; psoas sign positive; risk of missed diagnosis on clinical exam alone
Pelvic / Descending25–30%Pelvic/suprapubic pain; may mimic gynaecological pathology; rectal/vaginal tenderness
Preileal~5%Anterior; classic presentation; easiest surgical access
Postileal~2%Behind terminal ileum; highest risk of delayed diagnosis
SubhepaticRareRUQ pain; mimics cholecystitis; associated with malrotation/situs inversus

Vascular Supply & Neurovascular Danger Zones

  • Appendicular artery: terminal branch of the ileocolic artery (from SMA); runs in the free border of the mesoappendix
  • DANGER ZONE 1: Mesoappendical base: vessels run here; inadvertent traction tears vessel, causing uncontrolled retroperitoneal bleeding extending to the ileocolic pedicle
  • DANGER ZONE 2: Terminal ileum mesentery: aggressive dissection risks ileocolic artery injury
  • DANGER ZONE 3: Right ureter: lies on psoas medial to cecum; especially vulnerable in retrocecal dissection
  • DANGER ZONE 4: Right gonadal vessels: cross anterior to psoas, medial to ureter; at risk in deep pelvic/retrocecal dissection
  • Nerve supply: sympathetic T10 (visceral pain → periumbilical) + parietal T11–L1 (localises to RLQ with peritoneal involvement)
★ Board PearlThe taeniae coli converge at the appendiceal base, if you cannot find the appendix, follow the anterior taenia inferiorly to the cecum.

Step-by-Step Procedure

A. Laparoscopic Appendicectomy: SAGES 2025 Preferred

  • Position: supine, left lateral tilt 15–20°, Trendelenburg; catheterise only if pelvic anatomy anticipated
  • Ports: 12mm umbilical (camera), 5mm suprapubic, 5mm LIF; 30-degree scope preferred
  • Systematic survey first: rule out Meckel's, tubo-ovarian, Crohn's, right-sided diverticulitis
  • Identify cecum by following taeniae; grasp appendix tip with atraumatic grasper
  • Develop window in mesoappendix at base using hook diathermy or Harmonic; clip appendicular artery (×2 proximal, ×1 distal) or vessel sealer
  • Skeletonise base to 5mm; apply two Endoloops or two 5mm endo-GIA staple fires; divide between
  • Mandatory: specimen retrieval in Endobag: prevents port-site infection and seeding
  • Irrigate RIF and pelvis (1–2L warm saline) if perforation or peritonitis; no routine drain in uncomplicated cases
  • Close: 12mm port fascia only; subcuticular absorbable for skin

B. Open Appendicectomy (Lanz / Gridiron)

  • Lanz: transverse at McBurney's point (cosmetically superior). Gridiron: oblique along Langer's lines
  • Layers: skin → Scarpa's → external oblique (split along fibres) → internal oblique + transversus abdominis (split with Langenbeck retractors) → transversalis fascia → parietal peritoneum
  • Mesoappendix: transfixion ligature of appendicular artery with 2/0 Vicryl; divide in sections
  • Base: purse-string suture (2/0 Vicryl) + crushing clamp 5mm from base; ligate under clamp; stump inversion optional (no evidence mandates it but standard UK practice)
  • Closure: loop PDS or nylon for all layers; subcuticular absorbable for skin

C. Robotic Appendicectomy (da Vinci Xi)

  • Emerging role in complex/retrocecal cases, obese patients, and simultaneous pelvic procedures
  • 3-arm configuration: periumbilical camera + two 8mm robotic ports + 5mm assistant port LIF
  • No Level-1 evidence for superiority over standard laparoscopy in routine appendicectomy
  • Reserved for complex presentations or concurrent procedures
★ Operative PearlEndoloop vs. stapler at the base: SAGES 2025 and Cochrane meta-analyses show no significant difference in stump leak rate for non-inflamed bases. Inflamed/friable base or diameter >10mm, staple.

1.3

The Difficult Case

Scenario A: Retrocecal Appendix with Extensive Adhesions

  • Retrograde technique: divide base first, then develop the retroperitoneal plane posteriorly to lift appendix off psoas; avoids blind traction on adherent tip
  • Identify right ureter early (peristalsis; crossing iliac vessels at pelvic brim) before any posterior dissection
  • Subtotal appendicectomy: if appendix cannot be safely freed, divide at safe point; oversew distal stump mucosa with argon/Harmonic; insert drain; document clearly

Scenario B: Appendiceal Mass (Phlegmon/Abscess)

  • APPAC II and WSES 2020: initial non-operative management (antibiotics ± drainage) + interval appendicectomy at 8–12 weeks is safe for select cases
  • Surgery indicated for: peritonitis, free perforation, failed conservative management (>72h no improvement), suspicion of tumour, immunocompromised patients
  • Intraoperative mass: convert to open if laparoscopic dissection unsafe; drain placement mandatory if abscess encountered

Scenario C: Haemorrhage from Mesoappendix

  • Immediate: instrument/finger pressure at mesoappendical base against psoas
  • Clip or suture-ligate the ileocolic origin if appendicular artery avulsion extends proximally
  • Low threshold for conversion to open, uncontrolled mesoappendical haemorrhage + limited working space = convert immediately

Scenario D: Incidental Findings

  • Carcinoid >2cm: right hemicolectomy required: do not miss mesoappendix for nodal harvest
  • Mucocele: no rupture: intact removal in Endobag; aspirate only if impending rupture during extraction
  • Normal appendix at laparoscopy: systematic survey (Meckel's 50cm back, fallopian tubes, ovaries, sigmoid); remove appendix to avoid future diagnostic confusion, standard practice
⚠ Danger ZoneNever attempt to aspirate a mucocele laparoscopically before bagging. A single breach of the mucin-containing wall causes pseudomyxoma peritonei (PMP), an incurable seeding of mucinous tumour throughout the peritoneal cavity.

2.3

Consultant's Debate: Grand Rounds Controversy

Grand Rounds QuestionAntibiotics Alone for Uncomplicated Appendicitis: Paradigm Shift or Overreach?

FOR: Non-Operative Management

  • APPAC I/II and CODA demonstrate 70–73% success at 1 year, the majority avoid an operation
  • No perioperative mortality or major complications from antibiotics alone
  • Patient preference: 80% of CODA participants would choose antibiotics again
  • Cost-effective; reduces theatre burden in high-volume systems

AGAINST: Operative Standard

  • 27–30% recurrence requiring eventual surgery, two episodes of illness, more total antibiotics, longer cumulative LOS
  • Appendicolith on CT predicts failure (OR 3.2 per Cochrane 2021); present in 30–40% of CT-diagnosed 'uncomplicated' cases, the cohort is not homogeneous
  • Appendiceal neoplasm missed rate: 1–3% of appendicitis cases harbour occult malignancy (carcinoid, LAMN); antibiotics delay diagnosis
  • WSES 2020 and most European guidelines: surgery remains first-line; antibiotics for specific patient groups only

Current Nuanced Position: Board-Defensible

Current Consensus Antibiotics acceptable for: uncomplicated appendicitis WITHOUT appendicolith, fit patient, informed consent, reliable follow-up, access to emergency surgery if deteriorating.

Surgery preferred for: appendicolith, paediatric patients, pregnancy, immunocompromised, diagnostic uncertainty.

Framing: antibiotics = 'initial treatment with planned reassessment', not 'definitive cure'.
SECTION 3 Senior Fellowship & Board Review
3.1

High-Order Viva Scenarios

Viva 1
A 24-year-old woman presents with RLQ pain and fever. CT shows a 7mm appendix with periappendiceal fat stranding and a 5mm appendicolith. Alvarado score = 7. She requests antibiotic treatment. What do you advise?
  • High-risk features: Alvarado 7 (moderate-high probability), CT-confirmed appendicolith, inflammatory changes
  • Appendicolith is the critical modifier, CODA and Cochrane data show OR 3.2 for antibiotic failure in this subgroup
  • Advise: laparoscopic appendicectomy. Antibiotic failure risk ~50% with appendicolith vs. 27% overall; surgery is safer, definitive, and excludes occult tumour
  • Document informed consent discussion. If patient declines after full counselling, antibiotics with close inpatient monitoring are acceptable, NOT the default recommendation
Viva 2
20 minutes into laparoscopic appendicectomy, the appendicular artery avulses proximally and retracts into the mesoappendix. There is active bleeding obscuring your view. Next steps?
  • Immediate: direct pressure with grasper or rolled swab at appendicular base against psoas, do NOT remove the grasper and chase the vessel
  • Suction + irrigation to clear field; increase intra-abdominal pressure to 15mmHg for tamponade
  • Attempt laparoscopic clip/suture-ligation of the stump from the mesoappendix side; if vessel retracted to ileocolic origin: clip the ileocolic artery segment
  • Low threshold for immediate conversion to open, uncontrolled haemorrhage with poor laparoscopic access = convert without delay; this is not failure
  • Post-op: if ileocolic artery ligated, monitor for right colonic ischaemia
Viva 3
CT shows a 6cm appendiceal mass in a 65-year-old diabetic. He is afebrile, haemodynamically stable. Inflammatory markers elevated. Management strategy?
  • Step 1: CT-guided drainage if abscess >3cm + IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (pip-taz)
  • Step 2: Monitor response at 48–72h; lower threshold for early surgery in diabetic (>72h no improvement)
  • Step 3: Colonoscopy at 6–8 weeks mandatory, ~10% of appendiceal masses in >50-year-olds are caused by an underlying caecal carcinoma
  • Step 4: Interval appendicectomy at 8–12 weeks, APPAC II shows 37% 5-year recurrence without interval surgery; recommend in this patient given age + diabetes
Viva 4
Histology confirms a 2.3cm well-differentiated appendiceal carcinoid (NET). What are the staging and management implications?
  • Most common appendiceal neoplasm (>50% of all appendiceal tumours)
  • Size-based management (ENETS/NANETS):
  • <2cm: simple appendicectomy curative in >95%
  • 2–2.9cm (this case): RIGHT HEMICOLECTOMY recommended, lymph node involvement 20–30% at this threshold; mesoappendical invasion >3mm also indicates R0H
  • >2cm: right hemicolectomy + staging CT C/A/P + baseline Chromogranin A + 24h urine 5-HIAA
  • This patient: proceed to right hemicolectomy; ENETS surveillance protocol post-op
Viva 5
A 28-week pregnant woman presents with RLQ pain, fever, and leucocytosis. USS is non-diagnostic. Next step?
  • Imaging: MRI abdomen/pelvis WITHOUT gadolinium, sensitivity 90–100%; safe at all gestational ages; recommended by ACR and RCOG
  • If MRI unavailable within 6 hours and clinical picture deteriorating: CT acceptable, fetal radiation dose <50mGy is below teratogenic threshold; diagnostic delay carries higher risk than radiation
  • If confirmed: laparoscopic appendicectomy: safe up to 28–30 weeks; beyond 30 weeks open approach may be preferred due to uterine size
  • Obstetric: tocolytics not routinely required; foetal monitoring perioperatively; left lateral tilt to avoid aortocaval compression
  • Perforation in pregnancy: foetal mortality rises to 20–35%, do not delay surgery awaiting further imaging
Viva 6
Post-op day 5 following laparoscopic appendicectomy for perforated appendicitis. Patient develops spiking fevers, diarrhoea, RLQ tenderness. CT shows 4cm RIF fluid collection. Management?
  • Diagnosis: intra-abdominal abscess (IAA): most common complication after perforated appendicitis (5–10%)
  • Clavien-Dindo Grade IIIa: intervention required, no general anaesthesia
  • First-line: CT-guided percutaneous drainage + IV antibiotics (pip-taz/ertapenem)
  • If <3cm or inaccessible: IV antibiotics alone; repeat CT at 48–72h
  • If deteriorates despite drainage → return to theatre (laparoscopic washout) = Clavien-Dindo IIIb
  • Rule out: stump leak (check staple line), C. difficile colitis (send stool), pelvic collection
Viva 7
A 4cm intact mucocele of the appendix is identified laparoscopically. No peritoneal implants visible. How do you proceed?
  • Mucocele = mucinous dilatation: hyperplastic polyp (benign), LAMN, HAMN, or mucinous adenocarcinoma
  • CRITICAL: NO rupture, NO aspiration, spillage of mucin causes pseudomyxoma peritonei (PMP), an incurable peritoneal seeding
  • Technique: enclose appendix in retrieval bag BEFORE any dissection; divide mesoappendix and base within bag; extract through 12mm port; if bag damaged, convert to open immediately
  • If LAMN with R0 resection: surveillance (CEA, CT at 1 year). If HAMN or adenocarcinoma: right hemicolectomy + surgical oncology referral
  • If PMP discovered incidentally: refer to specialist HIPEC centre (PSOGI guidelines); do not attempt debulking at index procedure

4.3

ERAS 2025 Protocol: Appendicectomy

Preoperative

  • Clear fluids up to 2h preoperatively (ERAS Society); carbohydrate loading only if GA with no aspiration risk
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis: cefuroxime 1.5g + metronidazole 500mg IV at induction; do NOT continue >24h in uncomplicated cases (SIGN 2021)
  • Antibiotic therapy (not prophylaxis) for complicated appendicitis: 4–5 days pip-taz or cefuroxime/metronidazole; de-escalate to sensitivities
  • VTE prophylaxis: LMWH 12h pre-op for all but emergency cases; TEDs + pneumatic compression intraoperatively

Intraoperative

  • Goal-directed fluid therapy: balanced solutions (Hartmann's) over normal saline; avoid crystalloid excess
  • Normothermia: active warming blanket; warmed irrigation fluid
  • Multimodal analgesia: paracetamol + NSAID + port-site infiltration 0.5% levobupivacaine; avoid routine opioids
  • CO₂ insufflation to 12mmHg maximum; reduce to 10mmHg once visualised, reduces post-op shoulder tip pain

Postoperative

  • Resume oral fluids within 4h of uncomplicated laparoscopic appendicectomy
  • Early mobilisation: out of bed at 4–6h; ambulation at 12h
  • Ambulatory appendicectomy (SAGES 2025): same-day or 23-hour discharge feasible for uncomplicated cases, criteria: tolerating oral fluids, pain controlled on oral analgesia, afebrile 6h, WBC downtrending
  • Drain policy: no routine drain in uncomplicated cases; drain in RIF for perforated appendicitis with significant contamination; review at 48–72h
  • IV to PO antibiotic switch at 48h if apyrexial and tolerating orally in complicated appendicitis
4.4

Complication Management: Clavien-Dindo Grade III–V

Grade IIIa: Intra-Abdominal Abscess (IAA)

  • Incidence: 2–5% uncomplicated; 10–20% perforated appendicitis
  • CT-guided percutaneous drainage first-line for >3cm accessible collection; 8–12F pigtail catheter; irrigate 2× daily with 10ml normal saline
  • IV antibiotics: pip-taz or ertapenem; target Bacteroides, E. coli
  • Failure at 48–72h → Grade IIIb

Grade IIIb: Return to Theatre

  • Stump blow-out (<1%): presents as peritonitis Day 3–5; usually staple failure on inflamed/oedematous base
  • Management: laparoscopic re-exploration; washout; if stump unrepairable, ileocolic resection or right hemicolectomy ± defunctioning ileostomy
  • Faecal peritonitis: thorough 4-quadrant washout; source control paramount

Grade IV–V: Sepsis with Organ Dysfunction

  • Sepsis-3: suspected infection + SOFA score ≥2
  • Septic shock: vasopressors required despite adequate fluid resuscitation (>30ml/kg)
  • Surgical source control within 6–12h of septic shock, do not delay operation beyond vasopressor initiation; every hour of delay worsens mortality
SECTION 5 Spatial Awareness & Radiological Correlation
5.1

Surgeon's 3D Spatial Awareness at Laparoscopy

Standard Laparoscopic View (30° Camera, Trendelenburg + Left Tilt)

  • ANTERIOR: anterior cecal wall; taeniae coli converge centrally toward appendiceal base; appendix tip can point anywhere from anterior to deep pelvis
  • POSTERIOR (key danger zone): retroperitoneum visible as a whitish sheen through posterior parietal peritoneum; right ureter courses here; psoas visible as a firm ridge if peritoneum opened for retrocecal dissection
  • MEDIAL: terminal ileum enters ileocecal valve; preserving the ileocolic pedicle is non-negotiable; stay lateral during mesoappendical division
  • INFERIOR: pelvic brim visible in deep Trendelenburg; right ovary and fallopian tube must be visualised in all female patients; pelvic appendix may loop behind the uterus
  • SUPERIOR: hepatic flexure in the upper field, do not mistake a subhepatic appendix for cholecystic disease

The Retrocecal Appendix, Spatial Reconstruction

  • Posterior to the cecum, anterior to the psoas, posterior to the parietal peritoneum, not in the peritoneal cavity until mobilised
  • Mobilise by incising the white line of Toldt laterally and developing the retroperitoneal plane; the appendix delivers anteriorly like a banana peel off the psoas
  • Key landmark: right ureter crosses the pelvic brim anterior to the common iliac bifurcation, always identify BEFORE dividing any posterior structures
