Surgical Drain Placement

Abdominal, Pelvic, Thoracic, and Specialty Drains: The diagnostic and therapeutic conduit.

PILLAR 1 Decision Framework

1.1 The Decision to Drain

  • Mandatory: Pancreatectomy (POPF detection), CBD exploration (T-tube), biliary/enteric leaks.
  • Selective: Major liver resection, complex colorectal anastomosis, total thyroidectomy.
  • Contraindicated: Uncomplicated laparoscopic cholecystectomy, hernia repair (unless massive).
PHILOSOPHY: A drain drains what it is sitting in, nothing else. Position is everything.
PILLAR 2 Procedural Technique

2.1 Drain Selection

  • Active (Jackson-Pratt/Blake): Negative pressure for low-viscosity fluid.
  • Passive (Penrose/Robinson): Gravity/capillary action for viscous material.
  • Sump (Salem): Double-lumen to prevent suction collapse (fistulas).

2.2 Placement Steps

  1. Choose a dependent exit site separate from the main incision.
  2. Tunnel from inside-to-outside to prevent visceral injury.
  3. Secure with a non-absorbable "Roman sandal" knot.
PILLAR 3 Technical Pearls
Consultant Pearl: Drains left too long cause the problems they try to prevent (ascending infection, erosion). Remove based on objective output criteria, not time.
