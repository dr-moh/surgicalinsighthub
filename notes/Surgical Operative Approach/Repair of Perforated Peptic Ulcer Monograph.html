Repair of Perforated Peptic Ulcer

Open Graham Patch, Laparoscopic Repair, and Damage Control Variants

Emergency General Surgery Boey Score Omental Patch
Upfront Philosophy Perforated peptic ulcer (PPU) is an emergency where the decision-making window is measured in hours, not days. Delays at any step directly increase mortality. The Boey score predicts this mortality with brutal accuracy. The technical repair (an omental patch over a hole) is genuinely one of the simpler maneuvers in abdominal surgery. That simplicity is deceptive. The complexity lies in resuscitation, the decision to operate, approach selection, managing the contaminated peritoneum, and post-operative care of comorbid, septic patients. Master these components. The patch will follow.
PILLAR 1 Pre-operative Optimization & Preparation
1.1

Clinical Workup

History: What you need in the first 5 minutes

  • Classic presentation: Sudden onset severe epigastric pain rapidly becoming generalised. The patient can often tell you the exact minute it started. History of dyspepsia, GORD, H. pylori treatment, or NSAID use.
  • Risk factors: NSAID use (ask specifically about over-the-counter NSAIDs like ibuprofen/diclofenac), H. pylori infection, smoking (doubles risk), steroid use (masks peritoneal signs), anticoagulation, prior gastric surgery (changes anatomy fundamentally), corrosive ingestion.
  • Duration of symptoms: <24 hours means contamination is primarily chemical; 24-48 hours means established bacterial peritonitis; 48 hours indicates generalised faecal-equivalent peritonitis with multi-organ dysfunction.
  • Atypical presentations: Elderly/immunosuppressed (mild discomfort, confusion), steroid-dependent (blunted rigidity, haemodynamic instability may be the first sign), posterior perforation (pain localised to back, free gas absent), right iliac fossa pain (contents track down paracolic gutter, mimics appendicitis).

Mandatory Investigations

  • Erect chest X-ray: First-line investigation; identifies free subdiaphragmatic gas in 70-80% of cases. Absent free gas does not exclude perforation (20-30% do not produce visible free gas on CXR).
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast: Definitive investigation (sensitivity ~98%). Reveals site of perforation, volume of free fluid, extraluminal oral contrast (confirms active leak), and gastric wall thickening (>1cm raises suspicion of malignancy). Do not withhold IV contrast for renal concerns in a sick patient.
  • Laboratory: ABG (lactate >4mmol/L = severe physiological compromise; changes threshold for damage control). CBC, BMP, LFTs, amylase/lipase. Coagulation. Blood cultures. Group and screen (crossmatch if haemodynamically compromised).
  • H. pylori testing: Every patient with PPU must be tested and treated. Failure to eradicate H. pylori after repair is the most common reason for recurrence. Plan for CLO test on the ulcer biopsy.
1.2

Risk Stratification & The Boey Score

Memorise this. Apply it to every PPU patient before you scrub.

Boey Score Criteria
Boey FactorDefinition
Serious medical illnessASA III or higher; significant cardiopulmonary, renal, or hepatic disease.
Pre-operative shockSystolic BP <90mmHg at any point pre-operatively.
Duration >24 hoursSymptom onset to operative intervention >24 hours.
  • Score 0: mortality <1% (Proceed with standard repair; laparoscopic approach appropriate).
  • Score 1: mortality ~10%.
  • Score 2: mortality ~45% (Open approach preferred; HDU post-op; frank mortality discussion with family).
  • Score 3: mortality >80% (Is operative intervention appropriate at all? These patients may die on the table or in the ICU regardless of technical success. Consider non-operative management or palliative intent).
1.3

Non-Operative Management & Immediate Preparation

Non-operative Management (Taylor's Method)

Not every PPU requires surgery. Appropriate for haemodynamically stable patients with a clearly sealed/localised perforation on CT, improving symptoms over 6-12 hours observation, and no signs of malignancy. Also considered for elderly high-risk patients (Boey 3) where operative risk exceeds non-operative risk.

Taylor's Method Protocol
  • Strict NBM, NGT on free drainage, IV PPI, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, IV fluid resuscitation.
  • Serial clinical assessment every 2-4 hours. Convert to operative management immediately for haemodynamic deterioration, worsening peritonism, failure to improve within 12-24 hours, or rising inflammatory markers.

Immediate Pre-operative Resuscitation

  • Fluids: Crystalloid boluses (250-500ml) to restore haemodynamic stability (target MAP >65mmHg). Consider early vasopressors if not responding to 1-1.5L. Ongoing fluid loading in a non-responsive patient causes oedema without benefit.
  • Nasogastric tube: Insert immediately to decompress stomach, reduce ongoing leak, and lower aspiration risk.
  • PPI Therapy: IV omeprazole 80mg bolus then 8mg/hour infusion started pre-operatively to reduce gastric acid secretion.
  • Antibiotics: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV Q8H (or Meropenem + Metronidazole for penicillin allergy). Start immediately upon confirmation, not after theatre is booked.
  • Consent: Conversion from laparoscopic to open, re-operation if repair breaks down, stoma formation (rare but possible), gastric resection if malignant/massive tissue loss, and mortality risk explicitly stated.
PILLAR 2 The Operative Game Plan
2.1

Setup & Anesthesia

  • Anesthesia: Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI) is mandatory. All PPU patients have a full stomach regardless of NBM status. Aspirate NGT immediately before induction. Use etomidate/ketamine for unstable patients. Epidural strongly recommended for open cases. Active warming required (patients arrive hypothermic).
  • Positioning: Supine, arms out. For laparoscopic: reverse Trendelenburg 15-20° + left lateral tilt. For open: table flat.
  • Prep: Nipples to pubis, including both flanks for subhepatic space washout access.
2.2

PROCEDURE A: Open Repair (Graham Patch Omentoplasty)

Phase 1: Identification of the Perforation

Upper midline laparotomy. Rapid entry. Take a sample of peritoneal fluid for Microbiology (MC&S) immediately upon opening. Identify the perforation (usually anterior first part of duodenum or anterior lesser curve). If not visible, perform a Kocher maneuver to inspect the posterior wall. A typical acute PPU has a punched-out appearance with indurated margins.

Phase 2: Biopsy (Mandatory for Gastric Ulcers)

Every gastric ulcer perforation must be biopsied. 5-10% of perforations are perforated gastric cancers. Take 4-6 biopsies from the ulcer margin, not the necrotic base. Send for histopathology and H. pylori testing (CLO test from the antrum). Duodenal perforations do not require routine biopsy.

Phase 3: Graham Patch Repair

The definitive repair for the vast majority of PPU cases.

Graham Patch Omentoplasty
  • Place 3 absorbable sutures (2-0 Vicryl/PDS) across the perforation, longitudinally along the duodenum (parallel to long axis) to avoid narrowing the lumen. Take full-thickness bites through healthy tissue. Leave untied.
  • Identify a well-vascularised, mobile tongue of greater omentum. Do not use avascular or fatty omentum.
  • Place omental tongue over perforation. Tie the sutures gently over the omentum. Do not tie too tightly, or the sutures will cut through the omentum and lose the seal.
  • Confirm the repair: gently compress the stomach; no bile/fluid should emerge.
  • If defect >2cm: use more sutures (5-7) or consider a pedicled omental flap/sandwich technique.
  • If margins are friable: push omental plug into the defect and hold with sutures placed well away from the margin. Consider formal resection (distal gastrectomy) for massive unrepairable defects in fit patients.

Phase 4 & 5: Peritoneal Washout and Closure

Systematic washout of all four quadrants (Morison's pouch, left subphrenic, paracolic gutters, pelvis) with a minimum of 6-8 litres of warm normal saline. Do not use antiseptic solutions. Place a Blake/Robinson drain in the right subhepatic space adjacent to the repair. Mass closure of midline with 1-loop PDS (Jenkins rule). Delayed primary skin closure at 48 hours for heavily contaminated cases.

2.3

PROCEDURE B: Laparoscopic Repair

Appropriate for Boey 0-1, haemodynamically stable patients, symptom duration <24h, experienced surgeon, and perforation <10mm. Do not perform on Boey 2-3, unstable patients, or those with generalized faeculent peritonitis.

  • Port placement: 10-12mm umbilical (camera), 5mm RUQ (retraction), 5mm LUQ (working), 5mm epigastric left of falciform (working).
  • Identification: If perforation not visible, ask the anaesthetist to insufflate air through the NGT to see bubbles, or inject methylene blue. Do not spend >20 mins searching.
  • Repair: Intracorporeal placement of 3 sutures longitudinally. Bring omentum over defect. Tie knots intracorporeally or extracorporeally. Fibrin glue is an acceptable adjunct, but never the sole repair mechanism.
  • Washout: 6-10 liters warm saline using suction-irrigation. If fibrinous exudate is thick, consider conversion to open for mechanical debridement.
  • Closure: Place drain in right subhepatic space under direct vision. Close 10-12mm fascial defect.
2.4

The Critical View in PPU Repair

Non-negotiable confirmations before closing:

  • The perforation is identified and fully repaired.
  • The omentum is vascularised and under no tension.
  • The NGT insufflation test is negative (no bubbles from the repair site).
  • All four quadrants have been irrigated (document volume).
  • Biopsies have been taken from gastric ulcers before closure.
  • The drain is positioned in the right subhepatic space.
PILLAR 3 Technical Pearls & Bail-Out Strategies
3.1

Consultant Pearls

Consultant Pearl #1: The Repair That Fails Is A Tension Problem Every Graham patch failure traces to sutures placed through friable tissue that cut through, or omentum under tension that pulled away. Before tying sutures, pull the omentum to the repair site. If it requires traction, find a longer tongue. If a suture pulls through after tying, place another before irrigating. A repair you have doubts about will break down.
Consultant Pearl #2: The Perforated Gastric Ulcer Is Cancer Five to ten percent of perforated gastric ulcers are malignant. Take 4-6 biopsies from the margin before patching, plus a CLO test from the antrum. Document it. Ensure the pathology is followed up. A malignant result ignored is a medico-legal catastrophe. If frozen section returns malignancy in a fit patient, gastrectomy is definitive. If unfit, patch and plan a staged gastrectomy.
Consultant Pearl #3: The Post-operative Drain Tells You Everything A right subhepatic drain is your most sensitive monitor. Decreasing volume is reassuring. Bile-stained or green output on POD2-3 equals repair breakdown until proven otherwise. A bile-stained drain demands a CT with IV contrast and a low threshold for re-operation or IR drainage. Do not attribute it to "normal post-operative drainage".
3.2

The Bail-Out Point

Stop and escalate when:

  • Perforation too large for omental patch: Call senior. May require formal gastric resection. Do not proceed with an inadequate patch on a large defect.
  • Omentum absent/avascular: Consider falciform ligament patch, jejunal serosal patch, or resection.
  • Posterior perforation not accessible anteriorly: Extend Kocher maneuver; if still inaccessible, call senior.
  • Perforated gastric cancer with invasion: Do not resect without a consultant/oncological plan. Pack, close, plan staged approach.
  • Haemodynamic instability on the table: Apply damage control principles (rapid patch, minimal washout, TAC closure, get to ICU). A prolonged washout in a crashing patient kills.
  • Laparoscopic repair not technically feasible: Convert to open without hesitation. The decision to convert is surgical judgment, not failure.
PILLAR 4 Post-operative Care & Longitudinal Follow-up
4.1

Immediate Post-op (First 24 Hours)

  • Disposition: Boey 0 to ward. Boey 1-2 to HDU (decompensate rapidly). Boey 3 or unstable to ICU.
  • Monitoring: Tachycardia on POD2-3 equals repair breakdown, collection, or PE. Monitor hourly drain output. Bile-stained output requires CT. Monitor urine output for AKI.
  • NGT Management: Keep on free drainage 24-48 hours to decompress stomach. Remove when output is minimal (<200ml/24h) and no clinical concern about repair.
  • Pain Management: Epidural for open cases (48-72h). TAP blocks for laparoscopic. Scheduled acetaminophen + NSAIDs (with PPI cover; consider COX-2 inhibitors). Opioids PRN rescue.
  • PPI Therapy: IV omeprazole 40mg BD for first 48-72h, then switch to oral. Continue for minimum 8 weeks.
  • Diet: NBM 24-48h with NGT decompression. Water sips POD2. Free fluids POD2-3. Soft diet POD3-4. If diet causes pain/nausea, assess repair via CT.
  • H. pylori eradication: Standard triple therapy (omeprazole, amoxicillin, clarithromycin) for 14 days started on discharge. Confirm eradication at 4-6 weeks via stool antigen or breath test (not serology).
4.2

Clinic Follow-up

  • 2-week visit: Wound inspection, diet tolerance review. Pathology review for gastric ulcers (if malignant, immediate oncological referral). Review NSAID use.
  • 6-8 week visit: H. pylori eradication confirmation (mandatory). Upper GI endoscopy (OGD) is mandatory for all gastric ulcers to confirm healing and re-biopsy if needed. An unhealed gastric ulcer at 8 weeks is malignant until proven otherwise.
  • 3-6 month visit: Functional recovery. Monitor for recurrence symptoms (dyspepsia, haematemesis). Reinforce smoking cessation (halves recurrence risk) and NSAID avoidance.
PILLAR 5 Complication Management
5.1

Complication 1: Repair Breakdown / Duodenal Fistula

  • Recognition: Feared complication (2-10%). Bile-stained drain output on POD2-5. Worsening abdominal pain, fever, tachycardia. Late presentation (>POD7) involves persistent ileus.
  • Workup: CT abdomen/pelvis with IV and oral/NG contrast confirms leak and containment. Drain fluid bilirubin level higher than serum confirms bile leak.
  • Correction (Contained / Controlled Fistula): NBM, NGT decompression, TPN, IV antibiotics, Octreotide, IV PPI infusion. Output usually decreases over 2-3 weeks. Persistent fistula >6 weeks requires definitive surgical repair or ERCP stenting.
  • Correction (Free Leak / Peritonitis): Resuscitate and return to theatre. Re-repair if tissue allows. If tissue is poor, perform duodenal exclusion (oversew pylorus, gastrojejunostomy, tube decompression). If unstable, damage control.
5.2

Complication 2: Intra-abdominal Collection / Post-operative Abscess

  • Recognition: Peak onset POD5-10. Persistent fever >38.5°C, tachycardia, ileus, RUQ or shoulder pain. Most common in right subhepatic space.
  • Workup: CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast. Drain fluid culture.
  • Correction: CT or US-guided percutaneous drainage is first-line (85-90% success). For multiple collections or failed IR drainage, return to theatre for operative washout. Transrectal/transvaginal US drainage excellent for pelvic abscesses.
5.3

Complication 3: Delayed Gastric Emptying / Gastric Outlet Obstruction

  • Recognition: Functional delayed emptying (ileus) presents early with nausea/vomiting. Mechanical obstruction presents as vomiting on POD8-10 (repair oedema) or intolerance of diet >2 weeks (scarring/stricture).
  • Workup: Water-soluble contrast swallow (Gastrografin meal). CT if repair breakdown is a differential. OGD for late obstruction.
  • Correction (Functional): NGT decompression, correct electrolytes, minimise opioids, consider prokinetics (erythromycin/metoclopramide). Nasojejunal enteral nutrition if prolonged.
  • Correction (Mechanical): Early oedema resolves with conservative management over 3-4 weeks. Late stricture (>6 weeks) requires endoscopic balloon dilatation (first-line). Refractory stricture requires elective Heineke-Mikulicz pyloroplasty or gastrojejunostomy.
