I. Clinical Foundations & Philosophies

Page 1 / 8
Surgical Axiom
"The decision to operate is a clinical judgment; the decision not to operate is a consultant decision."
In PPU, the chemical peritonitis of the first 6 hours is a golden window. Beyond 24 hours, you are chasing established sepsis and multi-organ failure.
01

The Upfront Philosophy

Perforated peptic ulcer is an emergency where the decision-making window is measured in hours, not days. Delays at any step directly increase mortality.

02

Risk Stratification — The Boey Score

Memorize this. Apply it to every PPU patient before you scrub. It is the single best predictor of mortality.
Boey FactorDefinition
1. Medical IllnessASA III or higher (significant Cardiopulmonary/Renal/Hepatic disease)
2. Pre-op ShockSystolic BP < 90 mmHg at any point pre-operatively
3. Duration > 24hSymptom onset to operative intervention > 24 hours
03

Mortality Projections

  • SCORE 0 < 1% Mortality. Standard repair; laparoscopic approach highly appropriate.
    SCORE 1 ~ 10% Mortality. Escalated monitoring required.
    SCORE 2 ~ 45% Mortality. Open approach preferred; HDU post-op; frank discussion with family.
    SCORE 3 > 80% Mortality. Is surgery appropriate? Consider Taylor's Method or palliation.
04

Etiology & Triggers

  • H. pylori: Present in >75% of duodenal ulcers. Eradication reduces recurrence from 30% to < 5%.
  • NSAID Abuse: Synergistic risk with smoking. Steroids mask the peritonism (The "Silent Perforation").
  • Smoking: Doubles the risk of perforation and halves the rate of healing.

II. Presentation & Diagnostic Workup

Page 2 / 8
01

The Clinical Triage

The patient with PPU has a "Timed Entry" into the healthcare system. The exact minute of pain onset is often remembered.

  • Sudden Onset: Epigastric pain that rapidly generalizes. Patient lies motionless (peritonitis).
  • Board-like Rigidity: Involuntary guarding is the classic sign. Percussion tenderness > Palpation.
  • The "Valentino Sign": Right iliac fossa pain due to gastric contents tracking down the right paracolic gutter. Mimics appendicitis.
  • Atypical Elderly: May only present with confusion, mild discomfort, or hypotension. Always check the Boey score.
02

Investigations — Mandatory Triple-Check

InvestigationKey Finding / Significance
Erect CXRFree gas under diaphragm (75%). Absent gas does NOT exclude PPU.
CT Abdo/PelvisGold Standard (98% sens). Identifies site, fluid volume, and extraluminal contrast.
ABG / LactateLactate > 4 mmol/L = Severe compromise. Prepare for Damage Control.
CBC / BMPLeukocytosis, AKI (dehydration), and metabolic acidosis.
03

The Imaging Trap

Caution: The "Normal" CXR

Approximately 20–25% of PPU patients will NOT show free air on a Chest X-ray. If clinical suspicion is high (rigidity/pain), proceed to CT with IV contrast immediately. Do not wait for serial X-rays.

04

Differential Diagnosis

  • Acute Pancreatitis: Pain radiates to back; elevated amylase (note: PPU can cause mild amylase rise).
  • Acute Cholecystitis: RUQ pain, Murphy's sign, ultrasound findings.
  • Myocardial Infarction: Epigastric pain in elderly. Always check the ECG.
  • Oesophageal Perforation: Boerhaave syndrome; history of vomiting, subcutaneous emphysema.

III. Pre-operative Optimization

Page 3 / 8
01

Non-Operative Management (Taylor's Method)

Not every PPU requires surgery. Appropriate for stable patients with a sealed/localised perforation on CT.

Taylor's Protocol: Strict NBM + NGT to free drainage + IV PPI + IV Antibiotics + IV Fluids. Serial exams q2-4h. If symptoms worsen → Theatre immediately.
02

Immediate Resuscitation Bundle

  1. Fluids: Crystalloid boluses (250-500ml) to target MAP > 65mmHg. Avoid over-resuscitation (oedema).
  2. NGT: Insert immediately. Aspirate stomach to stop ongoing leak and reduce aspiration risk during induction.
  3. PPI: IV Omeprazole 80mg bolus → 8mg/h infusion. Start pre-operatively.
  4. Antibiotics: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV Q8H. Start immediately. Delayed antibiotics = Increased mortality.
  5. Urinary Catheter: Monitor output (target 0.5ml/kg/hr). Reflects renal perfusion.
03

Anesthesia — The PPU Special

  • RSI Mandatory: Every PPU is a "Full Stomach" regardless of fasting. High risk of aspiration on induction.
  • Warming: Active forced-air warming. Patients arrive cold; cold patients bleed and have poor wound healing.
  • Analgesia: Epidural recommended for open laparotomy; TAP blocks for laparoscopic.
04

The Consent Discussion

Mandatory Consent Items
  • Conversion from Laparoscopic to Open (~15% rate).
  • Risk of breakdown / Duodenal fistula (2–10%).
  • Possible need for gastric resection if malignant or massive tissue loss.
  • Mortality risk: Use the Boey Score to provide specific percentages.

IV. Operative Atlas — Open Graham Patch

Page 4 / 8
01

Phase 1: Exposure & Identification

Upper midline laparotomy. Rapid entry. Take peritoneal fluid for MC&S immediately.

  • Location: Usually anterior first part of duodenum or lesser curve.
  • Identification: If not obvious, perform Kocher maneuver to inspect posterior wall. Look for bile-staining.
  • Gastric Ulcers: MANDATORY BIOPSY. Take 4–6 bites from the ulcer margin to rule out malignancy (5–10% risk).
02

Phase 2: The Graham Patch Omentoplasty

The goal is to seal the hole with a vascularised "plug," not to close the hole under tension.
  1. Suture Placement: Use 3 absorbable sutures (2-0 Vicryl/PDS). Place longitudinally (parallel to long axis) to avoid stricture.
  2. Bites: Take full-thickness bites through healthy, non-friable tissue 5-10mm from the edge. Do not tie yet.
  3. Omental Plug: Identify a mobile, well-vascularised tongue of greater omentum.
  4. Tying: Place omentum over defect. Tie sutures GENTLY. Tying too tight will cheese-wire through the omentum.
03

Phase 3: The Safety Check

  • Insufflation Test: Ask anaesthesia to inject air via NGT while the repair is submerged in saline. No bubbles = Sealed.
  • Methylene Blue: If bubbles are ambiguous, inject blue dye via NGT.
04

Phase 4: Washout & Closure

The 6-Litre Rule

Irrigate all four quadrants with minimum 6 litres of warm normal saline. Do not use antiseptics. Aim to remove all fibrinous exudate, especially from Morison’s pouch and the subphrenic spaces.

Drainage: Place a Blake/Robinson drain in the right subhepatic space (Morison's pouch). This is your POD2 monitor.

VI. Laparoscopic Repair & The Critical View

Page 5 / 8
01

Laparoscopic Selection Criteria

Appropriate for stable patients with early presentation (< 24h) and Boey score 0–1.

IndicationContraindication
Boey Score 0–1Boey Score 2–3 (Shock / Delay)
Small defect (< 10mm)Large defect / Malignant suspicion
Minimal faeculent peritonitisGeneralized faeculent peritonitis
02

Laparoscopic Technique

  • Port Placement: Umbilical (10mm), RUQ (5mm), LUQ (5mm), Epigastric (5mm).
  • Intracorporeal Suturing: Same principles as open. Place 3 sutures. Use omental plug.
  • Fibrin Glue: Useful adjunct for small leaks, but never the primary repair.
  • Mechanical Washout: Requires patient repositioning (Trendelenburg/Tilts) to clear all quadrants. Minimal 6L.
03

The Critical View (Final Safety Check)

Perform this checklist before every closure:
  1. Perforation Identified? Yes.
  2. Gastric Biopsy Taken? (If gastric ulcer) Yes.
  3. Omentum Vascularised? No signs of congestion/necrosis.
  4. Repair Air-tight? NGT insufflation test negative.
  5. Washout Complete? 6L+ used; fluid returning clear.
  6. Drain Positioned? Right subhepatic space.
04

Damage Control Variant

The "Crashing" Patient

If the patient is Boey 2–3 and haemodynamically unstable on the table: Rapid Patch + Minimal Washout + Abdominal Pack + Temporary Closure (TAC). Get to ICU for resuscitation. Return in 24–48h for definitive washout. Prolonged washout kills unstable patients.

CONSULTANT NOTES: MAY 2026

VI. Technical Pearls & Bail-Out Strategies

Page 6 / 8
01

Consultant Pearls — High Value Tips

Pearl 1: Tension kills. Every Graham patch failure traces to sutures that cut through under tension. Pull the omentum to the repair site; if it requires traction, find a longer tongue.
Pearl 2: The Malignant Trap. 5–10% of gastric PPU are cancer. A malignant result ignored is a medico-legal catastrophe. Biopsy every gastric ulcer.
Pearl 3: The POD2 Drain. A right subhepatic drain is your most sensitive monitor. Bile-stained or green output equals repair breakdown until proven otherwise.
02

The Bail-Out Strategies

DifficultyBail-Out Plan
Defect > 2cmOmental plug (sandwich technique) or Gastric resection.
Absent OmentumUse Falciform ligament or Jejunal serosal patch.
Friable MarginsPlace sutures well away from margin; push plug deep.
Massive BleedingPylorotomy + suture ligation of Gastroduodenal Artery.
03

Decision to Convert (Lap to Open)

  • Cannot identify perforation after 20 minutes.
  • Excessive faeculent contamination / thick fibrinous peel.
  • Inability to safely suture due to friable tissue.
  • Anaesthetic instability (hypercarbia / hypotension).
04

Management of the Malignant Ulcer

Malignancy Found on Table

If frozen section returns malignancy in a fit patient: Formal Gastrectomy is definitive. If patient is unstable: Patch and Exit. Plan for a staged oncological resection once resuscitated.

RECOVERY PROTOCOL: MAY 2026

VII. Post-Op Care & Complications

Page 7 / 8
01

Immediate Recovery (POD 0–2)

  • NGT Management: Keep on free drainage until output < 200ml/24h and clinical concern for leak is gone.
  • PPI Therapy: IV infusion for 48h then switch to high-dose oral.
  • H. pylori: Start eradication (Triple Therapy) on POD 2-3 once tolerating oral. Mandatory.
  • Diet: Water sips POD 1; Free fluids POD 2; Soft diet POD 3.
02

Complication: Repair Breakdown (Duodenal Fistula)

Most common on POD 3–5. Signs: Bile in drain + Sepsis.
StatusManagement Plan
Stable / ContainedNBM + TPN + Octreotide + IV PPI. Success rate 85%.
Unstable / PeritonitisReturn to theatre. Re-repair or duodenal exclusion.
03

Complication: Intra-abdominal Collection

  • Recognition: Fever spikes POD 5–10. Persistent ileus.
  • Diagnosis: CT with IV contrast. Look for right subhepatic or pelvic pockets.
  • Treatment: Percutaneous IR drainage is first-line. Re-operation only for failed IR.
04

Long-Term Follow-up (Mandatory)

The "Malignancy" Safety Net

Every gastric ulcer must have follow-up OGD at 6–8 weeks to confirm healing. Any unhealed ulcer at this stage is considered malignant until proven otherwise by repeat biopsy. Document eradication of H. pylori via breath/stool test.

MCQ Challenge — High Yield

Q: A 72-year-old male with a Boey Score of 2 and symptoms for 36 hours presents with rigidity. What is the preferred approach?
A: Open Laparotomy. Delayed presentation (>24h) and high Boey score significantly increase mortality risk and complexity of washout, favouring open mechanical debridement over laparoscopy.
