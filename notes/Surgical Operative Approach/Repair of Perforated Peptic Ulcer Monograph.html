Repair of Perforated Peptic Ulcer

Graham Patch, Omentopexy, and Laparoscopic Salvage

Upper GI Emergency Surgery Omental Patch
Upfront Philosophy A perforated peptic ulcer (PPU) is a surgical emergency where the "time to theatre" is the strongest predictor of survival. Every hour of delay increases mortality by 2%. Your goal is not complex reconstruction; it is source control, thorough washout, and preventing recurrence.
PILLAR 1 Pre-operative Optimization & Preparation

Insight 2025/26 Robotic CME & 10-Year Appendicitis Data

2025 updates highlight long-term non-operative success and robotic colorectal standardization.

  • APPAC 10-Year Data: 55.7% of uncomplicated appendicitis patients avoid surgery long-term with antibiotics.
  • ColoRobotica: New international metrics established for standardized robotic CME.
  • CME Hemicolectomy: Robotic approach shows significantly lower conversion rates than laparoscopic.
  • Oncologic Efficacy: Robotic lymph node yields are now equivalent or superior to open CME.
1.1

Clinical Workup

History: The Sudden Catastrophe

  • Onset: Classic "sudden, sharp, agonizing" epigastric pain that rapidly becomes generalized. If the pain radiates to the right lower quadrant, consider Valentino's sign (succus entericus tracking down the right paracolic gutter).
  • Risk Factors: Chronic NSAID use (most common), H. pylori infection, smoking, and physiological stress (Curling's/Cushing's ulcers).
  • Masking: Steroid users and the elderly may not exhibit classic rigidity. Maintain a low threshold for CT.

Physical Examination

  • General: Patient lies perfectly still; any movement (including breathing) is painful.
  • Abdomen: "Board-like" rigidity, involuntary guarding, and loss of liver dullness to percussion.

Mandatory Investigations

  • Erect CXR: Free air under the diaphragm in 75% of cases. Absence of free air does NOT rule out PPU.
  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis (with IV contrast): Gold standard. Identifies the site of perforation, volume of extraluminal air/fluid, and excludes other pathologies (e.g., pancreatitis, mesenteric ischemia).
  • Labs: CBC (leukocytosis), BMP (metabolic acidosis if septic), LFTs, Amylase/Lipase, and Coagulation.
1.2

Risk Stratification (Boey Score)

The Boey Score is the most validated tool for predicting PPU mortality. Calculate this for every patient.

1.3

Immediate Pre-operative Preparation

  • Resuscitation: Aggressive crystalloid boluses. Target urine output >0.5 ml/kg/hr.
  • NGT Decompression: Mandatory to reduce further soilage.
  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum covering Gram-negative and anaerobic organisms (e.g., Piperacillin-tazobactam).
  • PPI Therapy: Start IV PPI (e.g., Pantoprazole 80mg bolus then infusion).
  • Consent Non-Negotiables: Graham patch failure/leak, conversion to open (if laparoscopic), subphrenic abscess, and the need for future OGD.
PILLAR 2 The Operative Game Plan
2.1

Setup & Exploration

  • Position: Supine. Laparotomy: Midline incision. Laparoscopy: French position (legs abducted).
  • Incision: Upper midline laparotomy is standard for emergency cases.
  • Exploration: Systematic search for the perforation (usually anterior duodenum or antrum). Aspirate all succus entericus immediately.
2.2

Graham Patch Technique (The Standard)

The goal is a tension-free plug, not a primary closure.

  • Step 1: Place three or four 2-0 or 3-0 silk/vicryl sutures across the perforation (full thickness). Do not tie them yet.
  • Step 2: Select a healthy, well-vascularized pedicle of omentum.
  • Step 3: Place the omentum over the hole (the "plug").
  • Step 4: Tie the sutures over the omentum snugly but not tight enough to strangulate the omental blood supply.
  • Step 5: Biopsy ALL gastric ulcers (high malignancy risk). Duodenal ulcers do not require routine biopsy.
  • Step 6: Massive Washout. Use 6-10 Litres of warm saline until the effluent is crystal clear. This reduces post-op abscess rates.
2.3

Laparoscopic Approach

  • Safe in hemodynamically stable patients with symptoms <24 hours.
  • Requires proficiency in intracorporeal suturing.
  • Higher risk of inadequate washout; must be meticulous in checking all four quadrants.
2.4

The Critical View

  • Source Control: Perforation clearly identified and completely covered by viable omentum.
  • Tension-free: Omental pedicle reaches the site without pulling on its blood supply.
  • Clear Effluent: Final washout fluid contains no visible succus or debris.
  • No Secondary Pathology: Gallbladder, appendix, and small bowel checked to ensure no concurrent pathology.
PILLAR 3 Technical Pearls & Bail-Out Strategies
3.1

Consultant Pearls

Consultant Pearl #1: Longitudinal Sutures Place sutures in a longitudinal orientation (parallel to the long axis of the duodenum) to avoid narrowing the lumen and causing gastric outlet obstruction.
Consultant Pearl #2: The Malignant Gastric Ulcer Never assume a gastric ulcer is benign. Perforated gastric cancers are real. Take 4-quadrant biopsies of the ulcer edges. If it looks very suspicious, consider a formal resection if the patient is stable.
3.2

The Bail-Out Point

  • Giant Perforation (>3cm): A simple Graham patch may fail. Consider a jejunal serosal patch or a formal distal gastrectomy if the patient is stable and a senior is present.
  • Friable Tissue: If sutures "cheese-wire" through the tissue, do not keep trying. Use larger bites of healthy serosa further away or consider a G-tube through the perforation site (Witzel maneuver) as a temporary measure.
PILLAR 4 Post-operative Care & Longitudinal Follow-up
4.1

Immediate Recovery

  • NGT: Remove once output is low and bowel function returns (usually POD2-3).
  • Diet: Sips on POD1; advance as tolerated.
  • H. pylori Eradication: The single most important step for long-term cure. Start triple therapy (PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin/Metronidazole) once tolerating oral intake.
  • PPI: Continue high-dose IV then oral PPI for 6-8 weeks.
4.2

Clinic Follow-up

  • 6-8 Week Visit: MANDATORY follow-up OGD for all gastric ulcers to confirm healing and exclude malignancy (even if intra-op biopsy was negative).
  • Breath Test: Confirm H. pylori eradication 4 weeks after finishing therapy.
PILLAR 5 Complication Management
5.1

Complication 1: Leak & Re-Perforation

  • Recognition: Sudden return of peritonitis or high-volume bilious output in a drain.
  • Correction: Emergency return to theatre. Redo patch or formal resection.
5.2

Complication 2: Subphrenic Abscess

  • Recognition: Swinging fever, tachycardia, and "hiccups" on POD5-7.
  • Correction: CT-guided percutaneous drainage and targeted IV antibiotics.
