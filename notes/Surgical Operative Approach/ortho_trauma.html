Pelvic Ring Fractures & Haemorrhage Control

Definition & Pathophysiology

Disruption of the bony pelvic ring involving sacrum, iliac wings, pubic rami, or sacroiliac joints. The pelvis is a closed ring, disruption at one point implies a second injury elsewhere. Mortality of open-book APC-III fractures: 15–25%. 85% of haemorrhage originates from the presacral venous plexus (Zone 3). APC injuries open the pelvis, increasing its volume and abolishing tamponade.

Lateral to foramina: lowest neurological risk
Zone II
Through foramina: moderate L5/S1 risk
Zone III
Central/transforaminal: highest risk; bladder/bowel dysfunction

Haemorrhage Control Algorithm

  1. Activate MTP: trigger at SBP <90 refractory to 1L crystalloid
  2. Pelvic binder at greater trochanters, NOT iliac crests. Reduces pelvic volume ≤40%. NOT for LC fractures.
  3. Resuscitation: 1:1:1 pRBC:FFP:Plt. Permissive hypotension MAP 50–65 mmHg. TXA 1g IV within 3h (CRASH-2).
  4. Stable → CT angiography → angioembolisation if blush
  5. Unstable → pelvic packing vs REBOA Zone 3
⚠ Danger ZonePelvic binder at GREATER TROCHANTERS not iliac crests. LC fractures must NOT receive a binder, worsens lateral compression. Consistent ABSITE/MRCS distractor.
★ Board Pearl85% of pelvic haemorrhage is venous (presacral plexus). Arterial bleeders (15%) are higher volume, arterial blush on CTA requires angioembolisation regardless of packing status.

The 6 Ps, Chronological Order

SignTimingSignificance

Pain on passive stretchEarlyPassive stretch of compartment muscles → exquisite pain. Highly sensitive.
Pressure (tense)Early–midWoody, tense, non-pitting compartment on palpation

Lower Leg: 2-Incision 4-Compartment Fasciotomy

  • Lateral incision (over fibular shaft, 10–15 cm): decompresses anterior (tibialis anterior, EHL, EDL, deep peroneal nerve, anterior tibial vessels) and lateral compartments (peroneus longus/brevis, superficial peroneal nerve)
  • Medial incision (2 cm posterior to medial tibial border): decompresses superficial posterior (gastrocnemius, soleus) and deep posterior compartments (tibialis posterior, FDL, FHL, tibial nerve, posterior tibial vessels)
⚠ Danger ZoneDeep posterior compartment is the most commonly missed. Requires medial incision extended with soleus detached from tibia. Missing it = ongoing ischaemia despite fasciotomy.
★ Board PearlDo NOT elevate the limb above heart level post-fasciotomy, reduces arterial perfusion pressure and worsens ischaemia. Keep limb flat or dependent.

Vascular-Associated Orthopaedic Injuries

Hard vs Soft Signs

CategoryHard Signs → OR immediatelySoft Signs → CTA first
HaemorrhagePulsatile active bleeding; expanding haematomaHistory of bleeding, now controlled
IschaemiaAbsent pulse; pallor; paralysis; paraesthesia; poikilothermiaABI <0.9; diminished pulse; bruit
ActionImmediate operative exploration: NO CTACT angiography to define anatomy

Knee Dislocation: Popliteal Artery

  • Popliteal artery tethered at adductor hiatus + anterior tibial artery origin → vulnerable to stretch
  • Vascular injury in 20–40% of knee dislocations
  • ABI <0.9 or hard signs → CTA or direct operative exploration
  • Revascularisation within 6 hours: amputation rate <10%. Beyond 8h: >80%

Supracondylar Humerus: Brachial Artery

Vascular StatusManagement
Pulseless but PINK (warm, perfused hand)Urgent ORIF first: reduction commonly restores flow. If pulse returns: done. If not: explore.
Pulseless and WHITE (cold, ischaemic)Emergent ORIF + immediate vascular exploration and repair
★ Board PearlPulseless pink hand: ORIF first. Reduction releases the kinked brachial artery in most cases. Do not take directly to vascular exploration without first reducing the fracture.

1.9

Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES)

Gurd's Criteria: 1 Major + 4 Minor Required

CategoryCriteria
Major (≥1 required)Petechial rash (axillae, upper chest, conjunctivae, pathognomonic); PaO₂ <60 on FiO₂ 0.4; Cerebral involvement
Minor (≥4 required)Tachycardia >110; Pyrexia >38.5°C; Thrombocytopenia (drop >50%); High ESR; Fat globules in urine; Hb drop >20g/L
  • Timeline: 24–72 hours post long-bone fracture (femur > tibia > pelvis)
  • Petechiae present in only 50%, do not wait for rash to diagnose
  • Supportive care: lung-protective ventilation (6 mL/kg IBW, PEEP ≥5)
  • Prevention: early fracture stabilisation reduces FES incidence by 50–75%
★ Board PearlPetechiae: upper chest, axillae, conjunctivae. CXR: bilateral snowstorm infiltrates at 24–48h. Best prevention = early fracture stabilisation, this is the exam answer.

Fracture Pattern Classification

Factors Impairing Healing

  • Smoking: non-union risk ×2–3. Counsel cessation pre-operatively.
  • NSAIDs: avoid first 4–6 weeks: COX-2 inhibition impairs MSC recruitment
  • Diabetes: HbA1c >7% significantly impairs healing. Optimise pre-operatively.
  • Steroids: suppress osteoblast function; increase RANKL
  • Malnutrition: albumin <35 g/L predicts complications
★ Board PearlOperative fixation required when: intra-articular step-off >2 mm; unstable fracture; open fracture; vascular/nerve injury; polytrauma requiring early mobilisation.

Humerus, Elbow & Forearm

Radial Nerve: Humeral Shaft

  • Radial nerve in spiral groove, mid and distal third fractures at risk
  • Wrist drop + loss of finger/thumb extension + sensory loss dorsum 1st web space
  • Primary palsy: 90% recover spontaneously. Observe × 3–4 months; EMG at 6–8 weeks.

  • Holstein-Lewis fracture: distal third spiral: highest radial nerve entrapment risk

Wrist & Hand

Distal Radius Eponymous Fractures

EponymDescriptionKey Feature
Colles'Dorsally displaced + angulatedDinner fork deformity; most common wrist fracture
Smith'sVolar displacementFOOSH onto flexed wrist; usually operative
Barton'sIntra-articular rim + carpal subluxationAlways operative: volar locking plate

Scaphoid Fractures

  • Blood supply enters distally, proximal pole AVN risk: 30–40%
  • X-ray visible in only 70% acutely, normal X-ray does NOT exclude fracture
  • MRI: gold standard: sensitivity ~100%, changes within 24h
  • Proximal pole fractures → operative (Herbert headless compression screw)
⚠ Danger ZoneClinical scaphoid fracture + negative X-ray = treat as scaphoid fracture (thumb spica) until MRI clarifies. Missing it → non-union → AVN → DISI collapse → wrist arthritis.

Key Hand Injuries

  • Mallet finger: continuous DIP extension splint × 6–8 weeks. 30 seconds of flexion resets the clock.
  • Jersey finger: FDP avulsion; ring finger most common; surgical repair within 10 days
  • Boxer's fracture (5th MC neck): acceptable angulation up to 40°; check rotation

  • Referred knee pain via obturator nerve, classic missed diagnosis

3.2

Crush Syndrome & Rhabdomyolysis

Prolonged muscle compression → ischaemia → cell death → release of myoglobin, K⁺, PO₄³⁻, urate, CK on reperfusion. Myoglobin precipitates in acidic urine → tubular obstruction + direct toxicity → acute tubular necrosis.

⚠ Danger ZonePre-treat with aggressive IV crystalloid BEFORE releasing crush. Crush release = massive K⁺ surge on reperfusion → VF and cardiac arrest. Start 1–2L NS/Hartmann's before extrication.
ParameterThresholdSignificance
CK≥5,000–10,000 U/LSevere: high AKI risk
MyoglobinDipstick heme+ with no RBCsMyoglobinuria: not haematuria
Potassium>6.5 mEq/L or ECG changesLife-threatening: calcium gluconate immediately
Urine output target200–300 mL/hMaintain until urine clears

Hyperkalaemia Management Ladder

  1. Calcium gluconate 10 mL 10% IV, membrane stabilisation
  2. Insulin 10 units IV + 50 mL 50% dextrose, intracellular shift
  3. Sodium bicarbonate IV, intracellular shift + urinary alkalinisation
  4. Salbutamol 10–20 mg nebulised, beta-2 mediated intracellular shift
  5. Haemodialysis, K⁺ >7.0 refractory; oliguria/anuria

Bone & Soft Tissue Tumours

Radiographic Approach: 5 Questions

  1. Where is it? Epiphysis (GCT), metaphysis (osteosarcoma), diaphysis (Ewing's, myeloma)
  2. What is it doing to the bone? Lytic vs sclerotic vs mixed
  3. What is the bone doing to it? Geographic margin (benign) vs permeative (aggressive)
  4. Periosteal reaction? Solid (benign) vs sunburst/Codman's triangle (malignant)
  5. Soft tissue mass? Implies cortical breach: aggressive behaviour

Soft Tissue Sarcoma: 5-5-5 Rule

Any mass that is >5 cm + deep to fascia + growing = urgent sarcoma centre referral. Most common metastatic site: lung.

Metastatic Bone Disease

Common Primaries: BLT with Ketchup + Prostate

Breast
#1 cause of pathological fracture overall; mixed lytic/sclerotic
Lung
#1 cause in men >50y; lytic lesions; poor prognosis
Thyroid
Lytic, expansile; solitary; pre-op embolisation
Kidney (RCC)
Hypervascular: pre-op embolisation essential
Prostate
Sclerotic/blastic; ↑PSA; ↑ALP; bone scan hot
Myeloma
Punched-out lytic; bone scan can be NEGATIVE, use whole-body MRI/PET
