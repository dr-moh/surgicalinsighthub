Surgical Monograph: Hernia and Hernia Repair

Complete framework covering groin, ventral, incisional, and rare pelvic floor hernias. Rives-Stoppa dissection, TAR component separation, and emergency management of strangulation.

PGY-3 Framework Technical Playbook
⚠ Ruthless Reality Hernia surgery is the highest-volume operation in general surgery worldwide. It is also the operation most frequently performed inadequately — not because the techniques are complex, but because the decisions before and after the operation are made without rigour. Which hernia needs repair? Which technique is correct for this patient? What mesh is appropriate? When is mesh contraindicated? What do you do with the obstructed hernia? What do you do with the strangulated one? These are the questions that define outcomes.
PILLAR 1 Pre-operative Optimization & Preparation
1.1

Clinical Workup

History: Key Urgency and Approach Markers

  • Duration and change over time: A hernia present for years without change differs fundamentally from one enlarging rapidly over weeks. Rapid expansion suggests elevated intra-abdominal pressure (ascites, masses, chronic cough) — resolve the driver before repairing the hernia. A previously reducible hernia that is now irreducible is an emergency until proven otherwise.
  • Reducibility categories:
    • Fully reducible: Contents return completely to the abdomen with gentle pressure in the supine position; proceed to elective repair.
    • Partially reducible: Some contents reduce while some do not. Often indicates incarcerated omentum adherent to the sac. Not necessarily strangulated, but requires urgent assessment.
    • Irreducible (incarcerated): Contents are trapped within the sac; requires urgent surgical evaluation.
    • Strangulated: Contents suffer ischemic compromise due to constriction at the hernia neck; surgical emergency.
  • Symptoms of obstruction or strangulation: Colicky pain, bilious vomiting, and absolute constipation suggest bowel obstruction due to the hernia. Constant, severe pain at the site accompanied by systemic sepsis (tachycardia, fever, hypotension) indicates strangulation.
  • Intra-abdominal pressure drivers:
    • Chronic cough: Repairing a hernia without addressing a chronic cough (COPD, smoking) guarantees recurrence. Perform smoking cessation and respiratory optimization first.
    • Constipation: Optimize bowel habits with fiber and laxatives prior to repair.
    • Urinary obstruction: Address BPH medically before hernia repair to prevent straining.
    • Ascites: Uncontrolled ascites causes extremely high recurrence rates. Manage the underlying cirrhosis or malignancy first.
    • Obesity: BMI >35 significantly increases recurrence rates. Weight loss is strongly advised prior to elective repair.
  • Prior hernia surgery: Prior mesh repairs alter anatomy and tissue planes. A failed mesh repair requires a pre-operative CT to map the anatomy and plan the revision.
1.2

Hernia Examination

Physical Exam Protocol

  • Dynamic Assessment: Examine the patient both standing and coughing. A hernia that disappears when supine may emerge immediately upon standing. A true hernia demonstrates an expansile cough impulse.
  • Systematic Exam: Document precise anatomical location, size (longest dimension × width), reducibility, and contents (soft omentum vs. firm bowel). Transilluminate scrotal swelling in pediatric patients to differentiate a hernia from a hydrocele (hydroceles transilluminate, hernias do not). Check the overlying skin for erythema or discoloration (signs of strangulation).
  • Taxis (Manual Reduction): Only attempt manual reduction on a non-tender, non-strangulated hernia after providing adequate analgesia, placing the patient in the Trendelenburg position. Do NOT attempt forceful taxis on a tender, irreducible hernia; this risks reducing ischemic bowel back into the abdominal cavity (*en masse* reduction), delaying diagnosis and causing intra-abdominal peritonitis.
1.3

Mandatory Investigations

Elective Panel

  • Blood Panel: FBC, BMP, coagulation profile.
  • Urinalysis: Mandatory in older males to screen for BPH/urinary obstruction.
  • BMI: Documented BMI >35 requires pre-op counseling due to increased infection risks.

Emergency Panel

  • Lactate (ABG): Lactate >4 mmol/L indicates bowel ischemia.
  • FBC: Leucocytosis (WBC elevation) indicates strangulation or tissue necrosis.
  • BMP: Severe vomiting causes hypokalemic hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis and dehydration-induced renal impairment.

Imaging Modality Selection

  • Ultrasound: First-line for groin masses with diagnostic uncertainty (differentiating direct vs. indirect inguinal hernias, lymph nodes, or lipomas). Useful dynamically with a Valsalva maneuver for occult hernias in athletes.
  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis: The gold standard for complex incisional hernias (defining defect size, loss of domain, and contents) and for diagnosing internal, obturator, Spigelian, and lumbar hernias. In emergencies, CT confirms obstruction transition points and ischemia (mesenteric edema, free fluid, bowel wall enhancement failure).
  • Loss of Domain (LOD) Assessment: If the hernia volume exceeds 50% of the abdominal cavity volume on CT, returning contents directly during surgery will cause abdominal compartment syndrome (ACS). Requires pre-operative progressive pneumoperitoneum (PPP) or lateral muscle Botulinum toxin injections.

1.5

General Principles & Mesh Selection

Mesh vs. Tissue Repair

  • Indications for Mesh: Adult patients with fascial defects >2 cm or high-tension tissue repairs.
  • Contraindications: Active local infection, gross peritoneal contamination (perforated bowel), or pediatric repairs (where mesh restricts abdominal wall growth).

Mesh Materials Framework

  • Heavyweight Polypropylene (PP): Rigid with high tensile strength but triggers a strong inflammatory response. High risk of chronic post-operative pain; largely replaced by lightweight PP.
  • Lightweight Polypropylene: Large pore size, less foreign material, lower inflammatory response, and reduced chronic pain. The standard for Lichtenstein and laparoscopic groin repairs.
  • ePTFE & Composite Mesh: ePTFE is highly smooth and non-adherent. Composite mesh combines PP (strength) with a barrier layer (ePTFE or hydrogel) to prevent bowel adhesions in intraperitoneal onlay mesh (IPOM) repairs.
  • Biological Mesh: Derived from human/animal dermis or pericardium. Resists infection and undergoes tissue remodeling, but carries high costs and higher recurrence rates. Reserved for contaminated fields or infected mesh salvage.
  • Biosynthetic (Absorbable) Mesh: Slowly absorbed over months (e.g., poly-4-hydroxybutyrate). Provides temporary structural support, reducing long-term mesh infection risks. Useful in clean-contaminated fields.

Fixation Choices

  • Suture: Gold standard. Use permanent monofilament (polypropylene) for permanent mesh, absorbable (PDS) for temporary settings.
  • Tackers: Spiral tacks used in laparoscopic repairs. Absorbable tackers reduce chronic pain compared to metal tacks.
  • Fibrin Glue: Avoids mechanical nerve entrapment. Indicated for laparoscopic repairs with low recurrence rates and minimal chronic pain.
1.6

Immediate Pre-operative Preparation

  • Antibiotic Prophylaxis: Cefazolin 1-2g IV within 60 minutes of incision. Clean mesh repairs require single-dose prophylaxis to reduce surgical site infections (SSI). In emergency contaminated cases, utilize Piperacillin-Tazobactam 4.5g IV.
  • Venous Thromboembolism (VTE) Prophylaxis: Mechanical compression devices for all GA cases. Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH) for high-risk patients. In emergency strangulations, initiate LMWH 12-24 hours post-operatively once hematoma risk has stabilized.
  • Consent Imperatives: Discuss recurrence rates (inguinal 1-5%, ventral/incisional 10-20%), chronic post-operative groin pain (10-12% incidence, 2% disabling), nerve injury, seroma/hematoma, mesh infection, and the risk of bowel resection or stoma formation in emergency cases.
PILLAR 2 The Operative Game Plan
2.1

PROCEDURE A: Laparoscopic Inguinal Hernia Repair

Laparoscopic Approaches

  • TAPP (Transabdominal Preperitoneal): Peritoneum is entered first to access the preperitoneal space. Excellent for bilateral hernias and recurrent cases after prior open repairs. Allows direct inspection of abdominal organs. Requires peritoneal suture closure.
  • TEP (Totally Extraperitoneal): Preperitoneal space is dissected directly without entering the peritoneal cavity. Minimizes bowel injury and adhesion risks. Steep learning curve; space is lost if the peritoneum is breached.
  • Critical Anatomy (Bogros/Retzius):
    • Space of Retzius: Preperitoneal space behind the pubic symphysis.
    • Space of Bogros: Lateral preperitoneal space containing the iliac vessels and cord structures.
    • Triangle of Doom: Bounded medially by the vas deferens and laterally by the gonadal vessels; contains the external iliac artery and vein. Do NOT place tacks or sutures here.
    • Triangle of Pain: Lateral to the gonadal vessels; contains the femoral nerve, lateral femoral cutaneous nerve, and genitofemoral nerve. Avoid mechanical fixation.
    • Corona Mortis: Anastomosis between the external iliac and obturator vessels crossing Cooper's ligament. High bleeding risk during direct tacking.

Step-by-Step TAPP Protocol

  1. Access: Umbilical 10mm camera port and two lateral 5mm working ports. Inspect the groin anatomy.
  2. Peritoneal Flap: Incise the peritoneum transversely 3-4 cm above the hernia defect, extending from the medial umbilical ligament laterally to the ASIS. Expose Cooper's ligament, the pubic symphysis, and the iliac vessels.
  3. Dissection: Reduce direct sacs cleanly. For indirect hernias, dissect the sac off the gonadal vessels and vas deferens (parietalization).
  4. Mesh: Place a 15x10 cm lightweight PP mesh covering the direct, indirect, and femoral spaces. Fix Cooper's ligament medially using fibrin glue or absorbable tacks.
  5. Peritoneal Closure: Close the flap completely using a running absorbable suture. Any gap allows bowel herniation and obstruction.
2.2

PROCEDURE B: Femoral Hernia Repair

Surgical Approaches

  • Lockwood (Low) Approach: Direct incision below the inguinal ligament. Best for elective repair or direct sac reduction.
  • McEvedy (High) Approach: Vertical incision above the inguinal ligament, entering the preperitoneal space. Ideal for strangulated femoral hernias as it allows direct access to the bowel and the femoral ring.
  • The Femoral Ring: Inelastic boundaries (inguinal, Cooper's, lacunar ligaments, and the femoral vein). The Gimbernat (lacunar) ligament must be incised medially to reduce an incarcerated hernia. Beware the corona mortis.

Step-by-Step McEvedy Repair

  1. Access: Vertical incision medial to the epigastric vessels. Enter the space of Retzius.
  2. Reduction: Apply gentle traction on the sac from above. If constricting, incise the lacunar ligament medially. Open the sac to confirm bowel viability.
  3. Repair: Close the femoral canal by suturing the inguinal ligament to the pectineal (Cooper's) ligament with interrupted 0-polypropylene sutures. Leave sufficient lateral space to avoid compressing the femoral vein.
2.3

PROCEDURE C: Umbilical Hernia Repair

Operative Techniques

  • Mayo Tissue Repair: Fascial edges are overlapped in a double-breasted ("vest-over-pants") fashion. Appropriate only for small defects (<2 cm) or pediatric hernias.
  • Ventral Sublay (Retromuscular) Mesh: Mesh is placed behind the posterior rectus sheath or preperitoneally. Offers the lowest recurrence rate.
  • IPOM (Intraperitoneal Onlay Mesh): Laparoscopic repair placing composite mesh directly against the peritoneum, secured with transfascial sutures and tacks.

Step-by-Step Open Sublay Repair

  1. Dissection: Semicircular incision, isolating the hernia sac. Clean the fascial defect edges.
  2. Space Entry: Enter the retro-rectus space on both sides. Dissect 3-4 cm around the defect.
  3. Posterior Closure: Close the posterior rectus sheath and peritoneum using a running absorbable suture.
  4. Mesh: Place a midweight PP mesh in the retromuscular space with 3-5 cm of overlap, securing it with interrupted sutures.
  5. Anterior Closure: Close the anterior fascia primarily over the mesh.
2.4

PROCEDURE D: Epigastric Hernia Repair

  • Anatomy: Linea alba defects between the xiphoid and umbilicus. Frequently contain only ischemic preperitoneal fat. Epigastric hernias are often multiple; inspect the entire midline.
  • Technique: For small defects (<4 cm), primary suture closure (0-PDS) is acceptable. Larger or multiple defects require a sublay mesh spanning all fascial openings.
2.5

PROCEDURE E: Incisional Hernia Repair

Component Separation & Mesh Position

  • Loss of Domain: If the hernia volume exceeds 50% of the abdominal volume, component separation or pre-operative Botulinum toxin muscle paralyzing injections are mandatory to prevent abdominal compartment syndrome.
  • Anterior Component Separation (Ramirez): Incision of the external oblique aponeurosis lateral to the semilunar line. Gians 8-10 cm of medial advancement. Disadvantage: extensive skin flaps lead to high seroma and wound infection rates.
  • Posterior Component Separation (TAR - Transversus Abdominis Release): Release of the transversus abdominis muscle medially, exposing the preperitoneal space laterally. Avoids raising subcutaneous flaps. The preferred technique for large midline defects.

Step-by-Step Rives-Stoppa retromuscular Repair

  1. scar Excision: Complete excision of the old midline scar. Enter the peritoneal cavity safely.
  2. Adhesiolysis: Free all bowel and omental adhesions. Measure the defect.
  3. Space Entry: Incise the posterior rectus sheath. Dissect the space between the rectus muscle and posterior sheath laterally to the semilunar line.
  4. Posterior Closure: Approximate the posterior rectus sheaths/peritoneum in the midline.
  5. Mesh Placement: Position a large heavyweight PP mesh in the retromuscular space, overlapping the defect by at least 5 cm. Secure with transfascial sutures.
  6. Anterior Closure: Close the anterior rectus sheath primarily over the mesh using loop PDS. Place retromuscular and subcutaneous drains.
2.6

PROCEDURE F: Parastomal Hernia Repair

  • Surgical Challenge: Parastomal repairs carry high recurrence rates (up to 50%). Local tissue repair is obsolete.
  • Sugarbaker Technique: The standard laparoscopic/robotic approach. The stoma bowel is lateralized against the abdominal wall, and a large composite mesh is placed over the defect, trapping the bowel loop underneath. Reduces recurrence compared to keyhole designs.
2.7

PROCEDURE G: Obturator Hernia

  • Demographics: The "little old lady's hernia." Occurs in elderly, thin, multiparous females presenting with small bowel obstruction.
  • Howship-Romberg Sign: Pain along the medial thigh extending to the knee on hip extension/abduction due to obturator nerve compression.
  • Management: Lower midline laparotomy. Reduce the trapped bowel loop. If necessary, incise the obturator membrane medially (watch for the obturator artery). Perform bowel resection for gangrenous segments and close the obturator canal with interrupted sutures.
2.8

PROCEDURE H: Spigelian Hernia

  • Anatomy: Occurs through the Spigelian aponeurosis along the semilunar line, typically below the arcuate line. Often interparietal (contained beneath the external oblique aponeurosis), making it impalpable.
  • Repair: Transverse incision over the defect. Open the external oblique, reduce the sac, and perform primary fascial suture closure or place a preperitoneal mesh.
2.9

PROCEDURE I: Lumbar Hernia

  • Anatomical Spaces:
    • Grynfeltt (Superior) Triangle: Bounded by the 12th rib, sacrospinalis, and internal oblique. The most common site.
    • Petit (Inferior) Triangle: Bounded by the iliac crest, latissimus dorsi, and external oblique.
  • Repair: Flank incision, reduction of fat/colon contents, and placement of a preperitoneal mesh secured to the surrounding muscular borders.
2.10

PROCEDURE J: Internal Hernia

  • Pathology: Herniation through congenital recesses or acquired mesenteric defects (common after Roux-en-Y gastric bypass - Petersen's space). High risk of rapid closed-loop obstruction.
  • Management: Emergency laparoscopy/laparotomy. Reduce the bowel loop, resect if ischemic, and close the mesenteric defects with non-absorbable sutures, avoiding compromise to the mesenteric blood supply.
2.11

The Strangulated Hernia — Emergency Protocol

  • Recognition: An irreducible, tender groin or ventral mass with overlying skin erythema and systemic sepsis.
  • Resuscitation: Insert NGT, Urinary catheter, start IV fluids, and administer Piperacillin-Tazobactam 4.5g IV immediately. Do not delay surgery for imaging if signs of strangulation are clear.
  • Safety Rule: Never reduce strangulated hernia contents blindly. Open the hernia sac first. Dissect the neck to release constriction, inspect the loop directly, and assess viability.
  • Bowel Resection:
    • Primary Anastomosis: Feasible in stable patients with minimal contamination.
    • Stoma Formation: Indicated if the patient is unstable, has gross fecal contamination, or demonstrates severe bowel wall edema.
    • Physiological Limit: If the patient's physiology dictates a rapid closure, perform a temporary abdominal closure and plan a re-look in 24-48 hours.
  • Mesh Selection: Synthetic mesh is contraindicated in contaminated fields. Perform primary tissue repair (Cooper's suture, Mayo, or suture bridge) or utilize a biological/biosynthetic mesh. Plan a staged reconstruction if a large defect remains.
PILLAR 3 Pearls & Bail-Outs
3.1

Surgical Pearls

  • Cooper's Ligament Suturing: When suturing the pectineal ligament, take deep, solid bites of the periosteum. Do not place sutures too laterally to prevent compression or injury to the external iliac vein.
  • Recurrent Hernia Dissection: Never dissect directly through the scarred midline. Access the abdominal cavity through a virgin incision superior or inferior to the old scar, establish the safe peritoneal space, and dissect the hernia neck from the inside out.
  • The Incarcerated Bladder: In direct or medial inguinal hernias, the bladder wall frequently forms the medial wall of the sac. If you see a fleshy, vascular structure, do not cut it. Instill 200ml of saline into the bladder through the Foley catheter to define the boundaries.
3.2

The Bail-Out Point

  • The Untensionable Defect: If the fascial edges cannot be approximated after component separation, do not force a repair. Perform a "bridged" repair using a biosynthetic or composite mesh. Approximating fascia under high tension guarantees postoperative necrosis, pain, and early recurrence.
  • Abdominal Compartment Syndrome (ACS): If returning large hernia contents causes Peak Inspiratory Pressures to exceed 30 cm H2O or urinary bladder pressures to rise above 20 mmHg, stop. Perform a temporary closure using a silastic sheet or negative pressure dressing. Optimize the patient in the ICU before returning for definitive closure.
PILLAR 4 Post-operative Care
4.1

Immediate Recovery

  • Activity Restrictions: Strictly limit heavy lifting (>5 kg) for 4-6 weeks to allow fascial healing and mesh integration.
  • Pain Control: Schedule scheduled acetaminophen and NSAIDs. Minimize opioids to prevent postoperative constipation and straining.
  • Bowel Protocol: Initiate scheduled stool softeners (Lactulose or Polyethylene Glycol) for 2-3 weeks. Straining at stool directly threatens the repair.
4.2

Clinic Follow-up

  • 2-Week Visit: Inspect the wound for SSIs and check for seromas. Seromas are highly common after large retromuscular repairs; do NOT aspirate a sterile seroma. Aspiration introduces bacteria, converting a benign fluid collection into a catastrophic mesh infection.
  • 6-Week Visit: Re-assess abdominal wall integrity. Gradually clear the patient to return to full physical activity and athletic training if the repair is stable.
PILLAR 5 Complication Management
5.1

Seroma & Infection

  • Seroma Management: Fluid collections in the dead space are expected. Treat with binders and reassurance. Only aspirate under strict sterile conditions if the seroma causes skin necrosis or is actively infected.
  • Mesh Infection: Presents with localized erythema, wound breakdown, or a chronic sinus tract. If the mesh is microporous or heavy synthetic PP, conservative therapy will fail. Complete surgical excision of the infected mesh is mandatory for source control.
5.2

Chronic Pain & Recurrence

  • Chronic Groin Pain (CPHP): Defined as pain persisting >3 months post-herniorrhaphy. Most commonly neuropathic (ilioinguinal, iliohypogastric, or genitofemoral nerve entrapment). Manage with scheduled neuropathic agents (Gabapentin) and targeted local anesthetic blocks. Surgical neurectomy is indicated only for refractory pain after 6-12 months of conservative failure.
  • Hernia Recurrence: Typically manifests within 1-2 years. If the primary repair was open, perform the revision laparoscopically to utilize a virgin preperitoneal dissection plane, and vice-versa.
