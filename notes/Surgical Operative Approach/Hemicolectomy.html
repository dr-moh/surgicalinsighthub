Hemicolectomy

Right, Left, and Extended Variants

ERAS Protocol Oncological Resection TNM Staging
⚠ Read Before Proceeding Most residents approach colon resection as a plumbing problem: take out the segment, join the ends. That framing will get you into trouble. The colon sits in intimate relationship with the duodenum, ureter, gonadal vessels, and major mesenteric vasculature. The anastomosis you construct will either hold or it won't, and when it doesn't, the consequences are septic, prolonged, and sometimes fatal. Understand the embryology, respect the fascial planes, and treat the anastomosis as the most important 10 minutes of the case. Because it is.
PILLAR 1 Pre-operative Optimization & Preparation
1.1

Clinical Workup

History: details that directly change your operative plan

  • Indication: malignancy vs. inflammatory bowel disease vs. volvulus vs. ischemia vs. diverticular disease. Each changes your resection margins, lymphadenectomy extent, and anastomotic strategy.
  • Prior abdominal surgery: adhesions change access; prior right hemicolectomy means a left or extended resection; prior pelvic surgery increases ureteric risk.
  • Bowel habit change, rectal bleeding, weight loss, obstructive symptoms: duration and progression matter.
  • Steroid or immunosuppressant use (IBD, transplant): impairs healing, increases anastomotic leak risk. May require defunctioning stoma even when you wouldn't otherwise.
  • Nutritional status: a malnourished patient with albumin <25g/L has a significantly higher anastomotic leak and wound complication rate; this is modifiable pre-operatively.
  • Smoking, diabetes, obesity: each independently increases leak risk; document and optimize.
  • Family history: Lynch syndrome, FAP. If relevant, changes your oncological resection extent and genetic counseling obligations.

Examination: what must be confirmed

  • Abdominal distension, peritonism, visible peristalsis: are you operating electively or urgently?
  • Digital rectal exam: mandatory in any colorectal case. Establishes rectal tone, identifies low rectal lesions, detects blood.
  • Stoma siting: mark pre-operatively by a stoma nurse if there is any possibility of a stoma (right iliac fossa for ileostomy, left iliac fossa for colostomy). This is a pre-op obligation, not an afterthought.

Mandatory Investigations

  • Colonoscopy with biopsy: for malignancy: confirms histology, localizes the lesion (tattooing is essential for laparoscopic cases), excludes synchronous lesions (present in 3-5% of colorectal cancers).
  • CT chest/abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast: staging for malignancy; vascular anatomy (SMA/IMA variants); liver metastases; ureteric course; lymphadenopathy.
  • MRI liver: if CT suggests hepatic lesions. Characterize before resection; simultaneous liver resection is a decision requiring HPB input.
  • CEA: pre-operative baseline for colorectal malignancy; post-op surveillance marker. A rising CEA after resection mandates restaging.
  • CBC & BMP: Anaemia is common in right-sided colonic malignancy. Optimize pre-operatively (iron infusion if time allows). Group and screen/crossmatch if significant anaemia.
  • Albumin: non-negotiable nutritional marker. <30g/L = consider pre-operative nutritional support; <25g/L = high risk for anastomotic complications (consider defunctioning).
  • Urinalysis: if pelvic/sigmoid pathology; colovesical fistula produces pneumaturia and recurrent UTI.

Cardiopulmonary Optimization

  • Formal cardiology review for patients with known IHD, heart failure, or significant arrhythmia. This is a major abdominal case with significant fluid shifts.
  • PFTs and respiratory review if significant COPD. Post-op pulmonary complications are a major cause of morbidity after colorectal surgery.
  • Prehabilitation: for elective cancer cases, a structured exercise program in the weeks before surgery demonstrably reduces post-op complications.

1.3

Immediate Pre-operative Protocols

  • Bowel Preparation:
    • Mechanical prep is controversial for right hemicolectomy (often no benefit, possible harm).
    • For left hemicolectomy and sigmoid resections: combined mechanical bowel prep + oral antibiotics (neomycin + metronidazole) reduces SSI and anastomotic leak. This is standard practice at high-volume centers.
    • For emergency cases: no prep; wash the bowel at operation if necessary.
  • Antibiotic Prophylaxis: Cefazolin 1-2g IV + Metronidazole 500mg IV within 60 minutes of incision. Covers skin flora and anaerobes. Repeat dosing for cases >3 hours. Single dose is sufficient for elective clean-contaminated cases.
  • DVT Prophylaxis: Colorectal cancer patients are among the highest DVT/PE risk surgical patients. Mechanical compression devices on before induction. LMWH pre-op for intermediate/high-risk patients. Extended LMWH for 28 days post-op for colorectal malignancy is guideline-supported.
  • Ureteric Stents: Consider pre-operative placement for locally advanced malignancy, severe diverticular disease with pericolic inflammation, or prior pelvic surgery/radiation. Stents make the ureter palpable intraoperatively.
  • Consent Non-Negotiables: Anastomotic leak (2-5% elective right; 3-8% left-sided), stoma formation, ureteric injury, hemorrhage, wound infection, ileus vs. mechanical obstruction, sexual/bladder dysfunction (left/sigmoid), adhesional SBO, and oncological margins.
PILLAR 2 The Operative Game Plan
2.1

Setup & Positioning

  • Anesthesia: General anesthesia with endotracheal intubation. Epidural analgesia strongly recommended for open cases (core component of ERAS). Nasogastric tube inserted after induction, removed at end of case. Urinary catheter is mandatory.
  • Positioning (Right Hemicolectomy): Supine, arms out. Table flat or slight Trendelenburg. Left lateral tilt during dissection to move small bowel away from the right colon.
  • Positioning (Left Hemicolectomy/Sigmoid): Modified Lloyd-Davies (lithotomy-Trendelenburg) with legs in stirrups. Allows access to the perineum. Right lateral tilt during dissection. Ensure legs are well padded to prevent compartment syndrome.
  • Laparoscopic vs. Open: Laparoscopic is oncologically equivalent and the standard of care. Open indications: emergency, locally advanced, extensive adhesions, anastomotic leak re-operation, BMI >45.
  • Instruments: Linear cutting stapler (GIA/Echelon) 60-80mm. Circular stapler (CDH/EEA) 25-29mm for left-sided anastomosis. Vessel-sealing device for mesenteric vessel control. Self-retaining retractor (Bookwalter or Omni-Tract) for open.
2.2

Step-by-Step: Right Hemicolectomy

Phase 1: Access and Exploration

Midline laparotomy. Systematically explore the abdominal cavity before resection: palpate liver for metastases, run the small bowel, feel the mesentery for nodal disease, and assess primary tumour mobility. Document all findings.

Phase 2: Mobilization

Medial-to-lateral (preferred for malignancy): Elevate the terminal ileum and right colon mesentery off the retroperitoneum by entering Toldt's fascia from the medial side. The plane lies between the colonic mesentery and the retroperitoneal structures (duodenum, right ureter, gonadal vessels, Gerota's fascia). Avascular, gossamer-thin areolar tissue separates easily; bleeding means you are in the wrong plane.

Phase 3: Vascular Ligation

For malignancy, high vascular ligation at the vessel origin is required for adequate lymph node harvest (minimum 12 nodes). Ligate the ileocolic artery and vein at their origin from the SMA/SMV. Ligate the right colic artery (variable). Divide the right branch of the middle colic artery. Critical anatomy to protect: SMV, Duodenum (D2/D3), and Right Ureter.

Phase 4: Bowel Division

Distal division: transverse colon at the junction of the right and middle thirds (ensure 5cm margin for cancer). Proximal division: terminal ileum, 10-15cm proximal to the ileocecal valve. Use GIA stapler. Send specimen for macroscopic margin assessment.

Phase 5: Ileocolic Anastomosis

Stapled side-to-side (functional end-to-end): Align terminal ileum and transverse colon in isoperistaltic orientation. Make enterotomies, insert GIA stapler, and fire. Close the common enterotomy. Inspect staple lines for bleeding, ensure no tension, confirm good blood supply, and verify no twist.

Phase 6: Closure of Mesenteric Defect

Close the mesenteric window with continuous 2-0 Vicryl to prevent internal hernia. In laparoscopic cases, defect closure is mandatory to prevent strangulated small bowel.

2.3

Step-by-Step: Left Hemicolectomy / Sigmoid Resection

Phase 1 & 2: Access, Exploration, and Mobilization

Midline laparotomy and confirm lesion location. Medial-to-lateral: Identify the inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) at its origin from the aorta. Incise the peritoneum at the base. Develop the retroperitoneal plane to identify and protect the left ureter and gonadal vessels. Splenic flexure takedown (entering lesser sac, dividing splenocolic/phrenocolic ligaments) is required for most left hemicolectomies to achieve a tension-free anastomosis.

Left-Sided Danger Zone: IMA, Ureter, and Retroperitoneum

Phase 3: Vascular Ligation

IMA ligation: For cancer, high tie at the aortic origin (clears IMA root lymph nodes). Identify the left ureter before the IMA is divided (the ureter is directly posterior to the IMA at the aortic origin). Ligate the inferior mesenteric vein (IMV) separately at the inferior border of the pancreas for mesenteric length.

Phase 4: Bowel Division

Distal division: at the rectosigmoid junction or upper rectum using a linear stapler. The stapler must be applied perpendicular to the bowel. Proximal division: descending colon proximal to the tumour with adequate margin.

Phase 5: Colorectal Anastomosis (Stapled End-to-End)

  • Anvil placement: Insert anvil into the proximal colon and secure with a full-thickness purse-string suture tied snugly around the spike.
  • Transanal introduction: Circular stapler introduced per rectum. Advance spike through or adjacent to the rectal staple line.
  • Docking: Connect anvil spike to stapler body. Ensure no tension, no twist, and no adjacent tissue caught. Fire the stapler.
  • Check donuts: Both rings of excised tissue must be complete 360° circles. Send as separate specimens.
  • Leak test mandatory: Fill pelvis with saline, occlude proximal colon, insufflate air per rectum. No bubbles = satisfactory.

Phase 6: Closure

Close the mesenteric defect. Consider a pelvic drain for left-sided anastomoses. Mass closure of midline laparotomy using the Jenkins rule (suture length to wound length ratio ≥4:1) with small bites.

2.4

The Critical View in Hemicolectomy

Non-negotiable confirmations before division and anastomosis:

  • Both ureters identified: particularly the left ureter before IMA ligation; the right ureter before ileocolic vessel ligation.
  • Vascular supply confirmed: pink bowel, bleeding cut edges, warm antimesenteric fat.
  • No tension on the anastomosis: the bowel reaches without pulling; if in doubt, mobilize more.
  • Donuts are complete: both rings inspected and confirmed 360° before closing.
  • Leak test is negative: documented in the operative note.
PILLAR 3 Technical Pearls & Bail-Out Strategies
3.1

Consultant Pearls

Consultant Pearl #1: Respect the Embryological Planes The correct dissection plane is an embryological plane: the fusion fascia between the colonic mesentery and the retroperitoneum. In the correct plane, the dissection is nearly bloodless, and retroperitoneal structures remain down and visible. When you're bleeding in the retroperitoneum and the ureter is not where it should be, you're in the wrong plane. Stop. Re-enter the correct plane from a known point. Do not dissect through a bloody retroperitoneum hoping to find the right plane.
Consultant Pearl #2: The Anastomosis Is Only as Good as the Blood Supply A technically perfect staple anastomosis on ischemic bowel will leak. Transect the antimesenteric fat at the proposed anastomotic margin and confirm it bleeds. If the proximal end looks ischemic after splenic flexure takedown, resect more proximally. If the rectal stump looks ischemic, you've taken the IMA too high or stripped too much blood supply: call your senior before you proceed.
Consultant Pearl #3: The Tension-Free Anastomosis Residents underestimate the mobilization required for a tension-free left-sided anastomosis. Steps buy length: full splenic flexure takedown, IMV ligation at the inferior pancreatic border, and division of lateral peritoneal attachments. If still under tension, consider mobilizing the right colon. A stapler fired under tension will cause ischemia. Hurrying this step is the fastest route to a re-laparotomy.
3.2

The Bail-Out Point

Stop and escalate when:

  • Ureteric injury identified: Stop dissection, call urology immediately. Primary repair over a DJ stent is feasible for clean lacerations.
  • Anastomosis is under tension: Bring out a stoma. Do not convince yourself it will be fine.
  • Both donuts are not complete: Redo the anastomosis or bring out a stoma.
  • Positive leak test that cannot be repaired: Defunctioning loop ileostomy and abandon the anastomosis, or bring out a Hartmann's procedure. A known leak left in situ is indefensible.
  • Locally advanced tumour adherent to adjacent structures: Do not attempt a blind R0 resection without senior input. An R2 resection that damages the ureter is worse than a planned multidisciplinary approach.
  • Significant intraoperative blood loss from mesenteric vasculature: Direct pressure, call senior. Blind clamping risks devascularizing the small bowel.
  • Emergency case with unprepared bowel/contamination: Hartmann's procedure is the safe option. Bring out the stoma, plan reversal electively.
PILLAR 4 Post-operative Care & Longitudinal Follow-up
4.1

Immediate Post-op (First 24 Hours) & ERAS

  • Fluids: Restrict IV fluids aggressively. Excess IV fluid causes bowel oedema, delays return of gut function, and increases anastomotic leak risk.
  • Diet: Early oral feeding (soft diet POD1, full diet POD2) reduces ileus. The old "wait for flatus" doctrine is not evidence-based. Carbohydrate loading 2 hours pre-op reduces insulin resistance.
  • Pain management: Epidural analgesia (open cases) run for 48-72 hours. TAP blocks for laparoscopic cases. Scheduled acetaminophen + NSAIDs. Minimize opioids aggressively. Alvimopan accelerates return of bowel function if available.
  • Mobilization: Out of bed on the day of surgery. Ambulating POD1. Respiratory physiotherapy prevents pneumonia.
  • Monitoring: Urine output >0.5ml/kg/h. Tachycardia on POD2-4 is an anastomotic leak until proven otherwise.
  • Nasogastric tube: Remove at end of case unless significant intraoperative contamination. Routine post-op NGT does not reduce leak risk.
  • Stoma care: Stoma nurse review on POD1. New ileostomy should start functioning within 24-48 hours. High output (>1500ml/day) requires electrolyte replacement.
  • Discharge criteria: Target POD3-5 for laparoscopic, POD5-7 for open. Tolerating solid diet, pain controlled, stoma functioning, ambulating, no fever/tachycardia. Extended LMWH confirmed.
4.2

Clinic Follow-up

  • 2-week visit: Wound inspection, stoma review, pathology review (TNM stage, margins, nodes retrieved). Functional recovery assessment.
  • 6-week visit: Oncology referral confirmation for adjuvant chemotherapy. CT chest/abdomen/pelvis restaging. CEA level baseline. Stoma reversal discussion (reversal at 8-12 weeks is standard post-chemo).
  • Colorectal malignancy surveillance: 3-monthly for 2 years, then 6-monthly to 5 years. CEA at each visit. CT annually for 3 years. Colonoscopy at 1 year post-op, then 3-yearly. Liver ultrasound 6-monthly in some centers.
PILLAR 5 Complication Management
5.1

Complication 1: Anastomotic Leak

  • Recognition: Peak onset POD3-5. Minor leak: low-grade fever, elevated CRP, mild abdominal pain. Major leak: sepsis, peritonism, clinical deterioration. The single most important rule: Tachycardia on POD3-4 after colorectal anastomosis is a leak until proven otherwise.
  • Immediate workup: CBC, CRP, lactate. CT abdomen/pelvis with IV and rectal contrast confirms leak and guides management. Plain AXR is inadequate.
  • Correction (Contained leak): IV antibiotics, CT-guided percutaneous drainage, optimize nutrition. Defunctioning loop ileostomy avoids the morbidity of a full re-laparotomy in a stable patient.
  • Correction (Major leak / Septic): Resuscitate immediately. Emergency re-laparotomy. Wash out. If salvageable, repair and defunction. If non-salvageable, perform Hartmann's procedure (resect anastomosis, end colostomy).
5.2

Complication 2: Ureteric Injury

  • Recognition: Intraoperative: urine in the field, visualizing a catheter in a divided structure. Post-operative (delayed): flank pain/fever POD2-5, urinoma, rising creatinine, ileus, urine from drain.
  • Immediate workup: CT urogram identifies site of injury. Urology review immediately.
  • Correction (Intraoperative): Remove ligature/staple immediately. Place a DJ stent. Primary repair over stent for small injuries; uretero-ureterostomy for transection. Call urology before attempting repair.
  • Correction (Post-operative): Percutaneous nephrostomy first priority. Antegrade or retrograde DJ stenting. Formal surgical repair planned electively after 6-8 weeks. Must disclose injury to patient.
5.3

Complication 3: Post-operative Ileus vs. Early Mechanical Small Bowel Obstruction

  • Recognition (Ileus): Diffuse distension, absent bowel sounds, no flatus/stool by POD4-5. CT shows dilated small and large bowel without transition point.
  • Recognition (SBO): Progressive, colicky abdominal pain. CT shows dilated proximal bowel with a discrete transition point.
  • Immediate workup: Clinical assessment, correct electrolytes, CT abdomen/pelvis distinguishes ileus from mechanical obstruction.
  • Correction (Ileus): Correct hypokalemia, minimize opioids, NG tube decompression, ambulation. Neostigmine for severe Ogilvie's with monitoring.
  • Correction (SBO): Conservative trial (48-72h) only if no signs of strangulation. Early operative intervention for complete obstruction, closed loop obstruction, or clinical signs of strangulation.
