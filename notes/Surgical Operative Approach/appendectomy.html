Open Appendectomy

Anatomy, Pathophysiology, Neoplasms, and Surgical Technique

General Surgery Emergency Procedures Gastrointestinal
Upfront Philosophy

Appendectomy remains one of the most commonly performed operations worldwide. Up to 8% of the population will undergo this procedure. While laparoscopic appendectomy has become the standard in high-volume centres, the open approach is the operation every surgical resident must know cold. It is the technique relied upon when the laparoscope is unavailable, when the patient is too unstable for pneumoperitoneum, or when a laparoscopic case requires conversion.

Two critical concepts govern this monograph. First, removing a normal appendix (negative appendectomy) is not a trivial outcome. It exposes the patient to surgical risk with no benefit. A negative appendectomy rate of 15-20% is unacceptable in the era of high-resolution CT. Second, the appendix harbors tumors with a wide spectrum of malignant potential. Mastery of appendiceal surgery requires seamlessly integrating acute inflammatory management with precise oncological principles.

PILLAR 1 Core Foundations & Workup
1.1

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

Surgical Anatomy

  • Location: The appendix is located at the base of the cecum where the teniae coli coalesce. It is typically found 2.5 cm inferior to the ileocecal valve.
  • Attachments: It is attached to the terminal ileum by the ligament of Treves. The mesoappendix extends from the mesentery and contains the appendiceal vascular supply.
  • Dimensions: Usually 6 to 12 cm long (may exceed 16 cm), with a diameter ranging from 0.3 to 1 cm.
  • Vascular Supply: The appendicular artery is a branch of the ileocolic artery. Venous drainage occurs via the appendicular vein, draining into the superior mesenteric vein (SMV) and ultimately the portal vein. This anatomical pathway explains the rare but severe complication of pylephlebitis (septic thrombosis of the portal vein).

Positional Variations

While the base location is constant, the tip is highly variable, fundamentally altering the clinical presentation.

  • Intraperitoneal: Extending toward the pelvic brim and iliac fossa.
  • Retrocecal (Most common): Located posterior to the cecum, either intraperitoneal or extraperitoneal. The psoas muscle blunts anterior peritoneal signs, presenting primarily as flank pain with a positive psoas sign.
  • Pelvic: Hangs into the pelvis, irritating the rectum or bladder. Presents with suprapubic pain, dysuria, and mimics gynecologic pathology in women.
  • Preileal / Postileal: Anterior or posterior to the terminal ileum.

Pathogenesis of Appendicitis

The cascade begins with obstruction of the narrow appendiceal lumen. Common culprits include fecaliths, lymphoid hyperplasia, tumors, or parasites.

  1. Obstruction & Distention: Continued mucosal secretion leads to luminal distention.
  2. Visceral Pain: Distention stimulates visceral afferent nerve fibers (T8-T10), causing vague, referred periumbilical pain.
  3. Ischemia: Rising intraluminal pressure eventually exceeds venous pressure, causing venous engorgement, followed by arterial compromise and tissue ischemia.
  4. Bacterial Invasion: The ischemic mucosa breaks down, allowing bacterial transmigration through the wall.
  5. Somatic Pain: The inflammatory process reaches the serosa and irritates the adjacent parietal peritoneum, shifting the pain to sharp, localized somatic pain in the right lower quadrant (RLQ).
1.2

Clinical Presentation

Classic Symptoms

  • Pain Migration: The hallmark sign. Periumbilical pain migrating to the RIF over 4-12 hours.
  • Anorexia: Nearly universal. If the patient is genuinely hungry, reconsider the diagnosis.
  • Nausea/Vomiting: Typically occurs after the onset of pain. Vomiting preceding pain suggests gastroenteritis.
  • Fever: Low-grade (37.5-38.5°C). Higher fevers suggest perforation or established abscess.

Atypical Populations (High Risk for Missed Diagnosis)

  • Elderly patients: Classical signs are blunted. The omentum is less effective at containing perforation. They present later (averaging 3-4 days), have perforation rates up to 70%, and a 5-10x higher mortality rate.
  • Children under 5: Cannot localize pain accurately. Present with fever, vomiting, and irritability. Perforation rates approach 80-100%. Imaging is critical.
  • Pregnancy: The gravid uterus displaces the appendix superiorly and laterally. Pain is located higher in the flank. Peritoneal signs are blunted due to uterine shielding. MRI is the modality of choice.

  • Score 1-4: Unlikely. Observe and discharge with return precautions.
  • Score 5-6: Equivocal. Requires imaging (CT or Ultrasound) for decision-making.
  • Score 7-10: Probable/Certain. Proceed to surgery.

Laboratory Investigations

  • WBC & CRP: Leukocytosis with a left shift is common. A CRP >100mg/L strongly suggests complicated appendicitis (perforation/abscess). However, 20% of confirmed cases have normal white counts.
  • Urine hCG: Absolutely mandatory in all women of reproductive age before proceeding with emergency abdominal surgery.

Diagnostic Imaging

  • Ultrasound: First-line for children and pregnant patients. Looks for a non-compressible appendix >6mm, periappendiceal fluid, and appendicoliths. A negative ultrasound does NOT exclude the diagnosis.
  • CT Abdomen/Pelvis (IV Contrast): The gold standard for adults (Sensitivity 94-98%). Indicated for equivocal scores, elderly patients, or suspected complications.
  • MRI: The preferred modality in pregnant patients to avoid the 8-10mSv radiation dose associated with CT scans.

1.4

The Non-operative Decision: Antibiotics Alone

Based on the APPAC trial and subsequent meta-analyses, antibiotics alone represent a safe alternative to surgery for uncomplicated appendicitis in selected patients. However, this approach carries a significant recurrence rate.

Criteria for Non-Operative Management (All must be met):

  • CT-confirmed uncomplicated appendicitis (no perforation, no abscess, no appendicolith).
  • Patient is systemically well (no sepsis, no diffuse peritonitis).
  • Patient accepts a 20-35% risk of recurrence requiring appendectomy within 1 year.
  • Reliable clinical follow-up is guaranteed.

Resource-Limited Context Warning: If follow-up cannot be guaranteed, or the patient lives far from a surgical facility, operative management remains the definitively safer choice.

Regimens & Failure:

  • Standard Regimen: Ertapenem 1g IV daily for 3 days, followed by oral amoxicillin-clavulanate for 7 days. Alternatively, Ceftriaxone or Cefazolin with Metronidazole.
  • Failure Criteria: Convert to surgery immediately if pain worsens beyond 24-48 hours, fever develops, peritonism arises, or inflammatory markers fail to fall.

1.6

Immediate Pre-operative Preparation

  • Resuscitation: Establish 2 large-bore peripheral IVs. Resuscitate with crystalloids to target a MAP >65 and urine output >0.5ml/kg/h. Crucially, do not allow prolonged resuscitation to delay surgery. Source control of the sepsis is the priority.
  • Antibiotics: For uncomplicated cases, a single prophylactic dose of Cefazolin 2g IV + Metronidazole 500mg IV within 60 minutes of incision. For complicated cases, utilize Piperacillin-tazobactam or Ampicillin/Gentamicin/Metronidazole, continuing for 5-7 days post-operatively.
  • DVT Prophylaxis: Apply mechanical compression devices pre-induction. Administer LMWH for intermediate/high-risk patients.
  • Informed Consent: Must explicitly document the risks of: negative appendectomy, wound infection (SSI), intra-abdominal abscess, appendiceal stump leak, potential extension to a right hemicolectomy, and infertility risks in females due to pelvic adhesions.
PILLAR 2 The Operative Game Plan
2.1

Setup & Anesthesia

  • Anesthesia: General anesthesia with endotracheal intubation is standard. Insert an NGT only if severe ileus is expected. A urinary catheter is required for perforated or prolonged cases.
  • Positioning: Supine, arms extended. Applying a slight left lateral tilt (5-10°) allows the cecum and small bowel to fall medially, drastically improving exposure of the right iliac fossa. Ensure the surgical field is at an ergonomic height for the surgeon's hands.
  • Prep & Drape: Prepare the abdomen from the umbilicus to the pubic symphysis, and laterally to both flanks. Drape widely. Never constrain yourself with a narrow field in case conversion to a midline laparotomy becomes necessary.

2.2

Surgical Incisions

The choice of incision must be made deliberately based on the patient's habitus and the certainty of the diagnosis.

Figure 4: Incisions Used in Open Appendectomy
  • Gridiron (McBurney's) Incision: The classic approach. An oblique incision centred on McBurney's point, oriented perpendicular to the spinoumbilical line. It is a muscle-splitting approach, yielding a very low hernia risk. However, it severely limits extension and pelvic access.
  • Rocky-Davis (Lanz) Incision: A transverse incision centred on McBurney's point, following Langer's skin tension lines. Provides superior cosmesis and is slightly easier to extend medially. This is the preferred incision for elective and uncomplicated cases.
  • Midline Laparotomy: An absolute necessity when the diagnosis is uncertain, when generalized peritonitis is present, or when a right hemicolectomy is anticipated. Provides unmatched access to the entire peritoneal cavity.
2.3

Step-by-Step: Open Appendectomy (Gridiron Approach)

Phase 1: Approach

  • Incise the skin and Camper's fascia. Use electrocautery through Scarpa's fascia.
  • Identify the glistening fibers of the external oblique aponeurosis running inferomedially. Split the aponeurosis bluntly in the direction of its fibers using closed scissors.
  • Retract the edges to expose the internal oblique muscle (fibers running perpendicular to the external oblique). Bluntly split the internal oblique and the underlying transversus abdominis muscle.
  • Pick up the thin, glistening peritoneum between two hemostats to ensure no underlying bowel is caught. Incise with scissors and extend the opening to admit two fingers. Place a Langenbeck retractor or wound protector.

Phase 2: Appendix Identification

  • Identify the cecum by its whitish color, appendices epiploicae, and the defining taeniae coli.
  • Follow the taeniae coli inferiorly; they consistently converge at the base of the appendix.
  • If the appendix is retrocecal and not readily visible, gently apply traction to deliver the cecum. If adherent, perform a Whitehead maneuver: incise the lateral peritoneal reflection of the cecum (similar to a Kocher maneuver) to mobilize it medially and expose the retrocecal space.

Phase 3: Mesoappendix Division

The mesoappendix carries the appendicular artery. Secure hemostasis is vital.

Figure 5: Division of the Mesoappendix
  • Clamp the mesoappendix near the appendiceal base. Divide the tissue between hemostats.
  • Ligate the proximal hemostat with a 2-0 Vicryl transfixion suture to definitively secure the appendicular artery.
  • Continue sequentially from the tip to the base until the entire mesoappendix is divided. Bipolar diathermy may be used in thin, uninflamed tissue, but is unreliable in edematous cases.

Phase 4: Appendix Base Management

This step dictates the risk of post-operative stump leaks. Meticulous technique is non-negotiable.

  • Apply a non-crushing clamp strictly at the base to create a crushed tissue groove. Move the clamp 1 cm distally.
  • Tie a 0-Vicryl or 2-0 Vicryl ligature precisely into the crushed groove. Secure with a surgeon's knot and two extra throws.
  • Place a clamp distal to the ligature to prevent spillage, and divide the appendix with a scalpel.
  • Stump Invagination (The Z-Stitch/Purse-string Debate): Traditional teaching mandated burying the stump with a purse-string suture into the cecal wall. Current evidence shows invagination adds no benefit in preventing leaks and may cause intussusception. Invaginate ONLY if the stump tissue is highly friable and the ligature security is doubtful.

Phase 5: Specimen Handling & Washout

  • Pass the specimen off the field immediately. Sending the appendix for histopathology is mandatory to rule out underlying neoplasms.
  • For uncomplicated cases, locally irrigate the RIF with 500ml of warm saline.
  • For perforated cases, irrigate thoroughly with 2-3 liters, aspirating all purulent fluid from the pelvis and paracolic gutters. Drains are reserved solely for localized abscess cavities or gangrenous stumps, not for generalized peritonitis.

Phase 6: Closure

  • Close the transversus abdominis and internal oblique together using continuous 0-Vicryl. Ensure all layers are incorporated to prevent herniation.
  • Close the external oblique aponeurosis.
  • For heavily contaminated or perforated cases, utilize Delayed Primary Closure (DPC). Pack the subcutaneous space with saline gauze and close at 48-72 hours. This drastically reduces the SSI rate from 30% down to <5%.
2.4

Appendiceal Neoplasms

The "inflamed" appendix may harbor dangerous pathology. Intraoperative findings or final histopathology dictate major oncological decisions.

  • Carcinoid Tumors (Neuroendocrine): The most common appendiceal tumor, typically located at the tip.
    • Tumors <1 cm: Simple appendectomy is curative.
    • Tumors 1-2 cm: Appendectomy is usually sufficient; surveillance is recommended.
    • Tumors >2 cm, or those involving the base/mesoappendix: Right Hemicolectomy is mandatory. If discovered post-operatively on pathology, the patient must return to the OR for resection.
  • Mucinous Neoplasms (LAMN / HAMN): These present as a smooth, mucus-distended mucocele. DO NOT RUPTURE IT. Spillage causes Pseudomyxoma Peritonei, a devastating diffuse peritoneal disease. Perform appendectomy with extreme care. If the base is involved, a right hemicolectomy is required.
  • Adenocarcinoma: Unlike intestinal types, appendiceal adenocarcinomas often disguise themselves as gynecological malignancies and spread along peritoneal surfaces. Right hemicolectomy and urgent oncology referral are required.
2.5

Specific Intraoperative Scenarios

  • The Normal-Looking Appendix: Do not close the abdomen and assume "mesenteric adenitis." Run the terminal ileum for 60cm looking for a Meckel's diverticulum or Crohn's disease. In females, inspect the ovaries and fallopian tubes. Palpate the cecum for occult carcinoma. Remove the normal appendix anyway to eliminate diagnostic confusion in the future.
  • The Phlegmon: The appendix is buried in a dense inflammatory mass. Do not dissect blindly into the mass. You risk catastrophic bleeding or injuring the underlying right ureter. Drain the area, close the abdomen, and plan an interval appendectomy in 6-8 weeks.
  • Base Involved in Perforation: If the perforation involves the cecal wall, a simple base ligature will fail.
Figure 6: Appendo-cecectomy for Base Involvement

If the surrounding cecal tissue is healthy, perform a local wedge resection (Appendo-cecectomy) and close in two layers. If the cecum is necrotic or highly friable, escalate to a formal Right Hemicolectomy.

2.6

The Critical View in Open Appendectomy

Non-negotiable confirmations before abdominal closure:

  • The appendix has been completely removed: The taeniae coli converge precisely to a closed point, with no residual stump length.
  • The stump ligature is absolutely secure: Gentle traction does not displace the knot.
  • Hemostasis is absolute: The mesoappendix and appendicular artery are dry after releasing all retractors.
  • The specimen has been sent to pathology: This is a medicolegal mandate.
PILLAR 3 Technical Pearls & Bail-Out Strategies
3.1

Consultant Pearls

Consultant Pearl #1: The Retrocecal Appendix

When the appendix is hidden behind the cecum, do not pull forcefully on the bowel. This causes serosal tears and can rupture a tense, gangrenous appendix into the peritoneal cavity. Instead, systematically follow the taenia coli to the base. If necessary, execute the Whitehead maneuver by incising the lateral peritoneal reflection to mobilize the cecum medially, exposing the retrocecal space cleanly.

Consultant Pearl #2: The Base Ligature

Appendiceal stump leaks result in catastrophic fecal peritonitis. They occur when ligatures are placed too distally (leaving a secreting mucosal stump), when tissue is too friable, or when knots are loose. Always crush the base first to create a secure groove, tie firmly into the groove, and verify security. Do not close the abdomen over a questionable stump.

3.2

The Bail-Out Point

Stop and escalate when:

  • You cannot identify the appendix: It may be subhepatic, or left-sided (situs inversus). Do not struggle through a 5cm incision. Extend the wound, call your senior, or utilize intraoperative laparoscopy.
  • The phlegmon is inseparable: Place a drain and back out. Blind dissection leads to ureteric injury and enterotomies.
  • The cecal wall is compromised: A safe stump cannot be created. Do not attempt a primary closure on necrotic tissue. Call for senior assistance to perform a right hemicolectomy.
  • Generalized faeculent peritonitis is present: A gridiron incision is entirely inadequate for source control and washout. Convert immediately to a full midline laparotomy.
PILLAR 4 Post-operative Care & Longitudinal Follow-up
4.1

Immediate Recovery (First 24 Hours)

  • Uncomplicated Cases: Advance to free fluids and a regular diet by POD1 to stimulate bowel function. Utilize scheduled acetaminophen, NSAIDs, and a diclofenac suppository for opioid-sparing analgesia. Mobilize early and target discharge on POD1.
  • Complicated Cases: Continue IV Piperacillin-tazobactam or equivalent for 5-7 days. De-escalate to oral antibiotics when the patient is afebrile for 24 hours and inflammatory markers are dropping. Remove surgical drains only when output is serous and <30ml/day.
  • Vigilant Monitoring: Watch closely for the "CRP bounce" on POD4-6. A falling CRP that suddenly spikes is the most sensitive indicator of a developing intra-abdominal abscess.
4.2

Clinic Follow-up

  • 2-Week Visit: Inspect the incision. Mandatory review of the histopathology report to rule out unexpected carcinomas or carcinoid tumors.
  • 6-Week Visit: For complicated cases managed with Delayed Primary Closure, confirm complete wound healing and assess for early incisional hernias.
  • Interval Appendectomy: For phlegmons managed with antibiotics, schedule surgery for 6-8 weeks post-discharge. While controversial in high-resource settings, interval appendectomy is highly recommended in resource-limited contexts to avoid the 20-35% risk of dangerous recurrence.
PILLAR 5 Complication Management
5.1

Complication 1: Wound Infection (SSI)

The most frequent complication, particularly when heavily contaminated wounds are closed primarily.

  • Recognition: Superficial SSIs present POD3-10 with erythema and purulent discharge. Deep incisional SSIs present POD5-14; the skin may look healed, but the patient remains febrile with deep induration.
  • Correction: Open the wound entirely. Probe the depth to break down loculations. Irrigate and pack with saline gauze to heal by secondary intention. Systemic antibiotics are unnecessary unless there is extending cellulitis or systemic sepsis.
5.2

Complication 2: Intra-abdominal Abscess

The most dangerous delayed complication following perforated appendicitis.

  • Recognition: Persistent fever, pelvic pain, diarrhea/tenesmus, and the classic "CRP bounce" on POD4-6. Pus typically tracks down into the pelvis due to gravity.
  • Workup: CT Abdomen/Pelvis with IV contrast is mandatory to map the collection.
Figure 3: CT Scan of Percutaneous Abscess Drainage
  • Correction: Percutaneous CT or Ultrasound-guided drainage is the first-line treatment with a 90% success rate. For deep pelvic abscesses, transrectal or transvaginal ultrasound-guided drainage is highly effective and avoids re-operation.
5.3

Complication 3: Stump Leak & Pylephlebitis

Appendiceal Stump Leak

Occurs when the base ligature slips or cuts through necrotic tissue.

  • Recognition: Presents POD3-7 as sudden generalized peritonitis, faeculent wound breakdown, or faeculent output from an existing drain.
  • Correction (Controlled): If draining via an established surgical drain and the patient is stable, maintain the drain. Treat with NBM, TPN, IV antibiotics, and Octreotide. Most will seal in 4-6 weeks.
  • Correction (Free Peritonitis): Emergency laparotomy. Perform a right hemicolectomy with a primary anastomosis or end-ileostomy depending on physiologic stability.

Pylephlebitis

A rare, lethal complication where the septic focus in the appendix causes suppurative thrombosis of the appendicular vein, which propagates to the superior mesenteric vein and portal vein.

  • Recognition: High swinging fevers, rigors, right upper quadrant pain, and jaundice.
  • Correction: Aggressive, prolonged IV antibiotic therapy. Surgical source control is mandatory. Anticoagulation is indicated to prevent clot propagation into the mesenteric arcades.
