Paediatric Surgery · Colorectal

Anorectal Malformation (PSARP)

Systematic approach to PSARP. Peña classification, operative steps for high vs low malformations, and the critical view of the rectum-urethra interface.

Paediatric Core PSARP Steps
PILLAR 1 Decision Framework

1.1 Krickenbeck Classification

  • Low (Perineal): Fistula opens on perineum; primary repair possible in newborn period.
  • Intermediate/High: Rectourethral (bulbar/prostatic), rectovesical (bladder neck).
  • Cloaca: Single perineal opening (female only); most complex variant.
PHILOSOPHY: Classification by fistula level is the single most important determinant of operative strategy.

1.3 VACTERL Screening

  • Vertebral: Sacral ratio (Normal >0.7) predicts continence.
  • Cardiac: Echo before any anaesthesia; defects in 30%.
  • Renal: Ultrasound in first 24h; bilateral agenesis is lethal.
PILLAR 2 The Operative Game Plan

2.1 The 24-Hour Rule

Wait 24 hours before deciding on the approach. Meconium takes time to track through the fistula, and gas needs time to reach the distal rectum for imaging.

2.2 Divided Sigmoid Colostomy

  • Mandatory: Divided (not loop) colostomy with mucous fistula.
  • Reason: Prevents stool spillage into the urinary tract and enables accurate distal colostogram.
PILLAR 3 Technical Pearls
Consultant Pearl: PSARP (Peña) requires a muscle stimulator on the field. Accuracy of muscle placement is the only variable the surgeon can control.
