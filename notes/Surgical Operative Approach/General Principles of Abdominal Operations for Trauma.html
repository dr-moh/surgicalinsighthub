General Principles of Abdominal Operations for Trauma

Standardized PGY-3 trauma monograph covering vascular zones, surgical incisions, extensions, medial visceral rotation, systematic exploration, and damage control principles.

PGY-3 Monograph Trauma Core 5 Pillars
⚠ Upfront Surgical Philosophy (Brutal Rule) If damage control packing does not stop the bleeding, do not terminate the operation; re-explore, identify, and control surgical bleeding immediately.
PILLAR 1

Pre-operative Optimization & Preparation

1.1

Indications & General Principles

Clinical Decision Points

  • Laparotomy for bleeding vs peritonitis: Bleeding laparotomy demands immediate, rapid vascular control. Peritonitis laparotomy focuses on contamination control and source clearance.
  • Primary priority: Arrest active bleeding first. Methodical exploration of all structures to identify and repair other non-life-threatening injuries occurs only after hemorrhage control is established.
  • Damage control threshold: Initiate abbreviated laparotomy before physiological depletion (coagulopathy, hypothermia, acidosis) occurs. Consider injury severity, patient physiological reserves, facility limits, and surgeon skillset.
  • Organ salvage limits: Removal versus repair of organs (spleen, kidney) is determined by injury scale and patient physiologic stability. Splenectomy is standard in hemodynamically unstable patients.
  • Open abdomen mandate: In damage control, leave the abdomen open using temporary closure techniques to prevent intra-abdominal hypertension and abdominal compartment syndrome.
1.2

Surgical Anatomy (Retroperitoneal Zones)

Anatomical Landmarks

  • Linea alba: Midline aponeurosis extending from xiphoid to pubic symphysis, separating the rectus muscles. Widest 2-3 cm above the umbilicus, making it the safest entry site.
  • Abdominal wall layers: External oblique, internal oblique, transversus abdominis, and rectus abdominis. Rectus sheath is formed by the aponeuroses of the first three muscles.

Retroperitoneal Vascular Zones Classification

Zone Anatomical Limits Major Contents Exploration Mandate (Blunt) Exploration Mandate (Penetrating)
Zone 1 (Midline) Aortic hiatus to sacral promontory. Divided into supramesocolic and inframesocolic. Suprarenal/infrarenal aorta and IVC, celiac axis, SMA, SMV, renal vessels. Mandatory exploration (High-risk exsanguination). Mandatory exploration (Vascular injury highly probable).
Zone 2 (Flank) Paracolic gutters, kidneys, renal vessels, ureters. Kidneys, renal arteries/veins, ureters. Observe if stable and non-expanding. Mandatory exploration (Ureteral/vascular leaks).
Zone 3 (Pelvis) Pelvic retroperitoneum. Iliac arteries and veins, ureters. Observe unless expanding/pulsatile (loss of pelvic tamponade). Mandatory exploration (Iliac vascular injury control).
Zone 4 (Perihepatic) Perihepatic space and porta hepatis. Hepatic artery, portal vein, retrohepatic IVC, hepatic veins. Observe stable hematomas (high mortality in opening). Observe stable retrohepatic hematomas; open only if actively bleeding.

1.3

Patient Positioning & Preparation

Positioning Protocols

  • Supine positioning: Arms abducted to 90 degrees to facilitate bilateral large-bore intravenous access for rapid resuscitation.
  • Lithotomy option: Indicated if there is suspicion of rectal, distal sigmoid, or anal canal injuries to permit transanal assessment.
  • Operating table setup: Keep bed rails completely exposed and free of drapes to allow secure mounting of fixed retractors (e.g., Bookwalter).

Preparation and Draping Boundary

  • Prep boundaries: Extend skin prep from the chin to the knees, and laterally to the operating table surface (posterior axillary lines).
  • Groin exposure: Groins must be prepped in the sterile field because saphenous vein harvest may be required for vascular grafts.

PILLAR 2

The Operative Game Plan (Access & Exploration)

2.1

Midline Laparotomy & Entry

Incision Selection

  • Standard incision: Full midline laparotomy between the xiphoid process and the pubic symphysis.
  • Indication: Hemodynamically unstable patients with blunt trauma, penetrating trauma, and unknown missile trajectories. Routine xiphoid-to-pubis incision is not advised if a localized injury is confirmed.

Step-by-Step Peritoneal Entry

  1. Skin & Subcutis: Perform rapid incision with scalpel. Do not waste time with subcutaneous hemostasis in unstable patients.
  2. Fascial Identification: Identify the linea alba 2-3 cm above the umbilicus. This is the widest section, reducing the risk of entering the rectus sheath.
  3. Linea Alba Division: Divide the linea alba sharply. Sweep preperitoneal fat away to expose the underlying peritoneum.
  4. Peritoneal Entry: Perform entry sharply or bluntly using a finger just superior to the umbilicus, which is the thinnest point.
  5. Extension: Extend the peritoneal incision superiorly and inferiorly using scissors under direct finger guidance.

2.2

Incision Extensions

Right Subcostal Extension

  • Indication: Complex posterior liver or retrohepatic major venous injuries requiring superior, right-sided exposure.
  • Technique: Incise 1-2 fingerbreadths below the right costal margin, extending from the midline laparotomy.
  • Skin Necrosis Prevention: Avoid an acute angle between the midline and subcostal incisions to preserve blood supply.
  • Muscle Division: Divide the rectus abdominis, external oblique, internal oblique, and transversus abdominis. Ensure ligation/control of the superior and inferior epigastric arteries.

Median Sternotomy Extension

  • Indication: Associated intrathoracic injuries or severe liver injuries requiring atriocaval shunting or total hepatic vascular isolation.
  • Technique: Extend the midline incision superiorly through the sternum to the sternal notch.

2.3

Special Instruments & Retractors

  • Bookwalter Retractor: Fixed surgical retractor. Mount to exposed bed rails to provide hands-free retraction and exposure in deep pelvic or upper abdominal zones.
  • Headlights: Essential equipment to provide directed illumination into deep anatomical recesses.
  • Vessel Sealing System (LigaSure): Electrothermal bipolar device. Accelerates division of the bowel mesentery during resection and assists in non-anatomic liver and spleen mobilizations.

2.4

Medial Visceral Rotation Maneuvers

Right Medial Visceral Rotation (Cattell-Braasch)

  • Indication: Exposure of the retroperitoneum, infrarenal aorta, inferior vena cava (IVC), and right renal vessels.
  • Technique: Incise the peritoneal reflection along the white line of Toldt from the cecum to the right hepatic flexure.
  • Mesenteric Dissection: Continue dissection beneath the root of the small bowel mesentery, incising the retroperitoneum from the ileocecal junction to the ligament of Treitz.
  • Mobilization: Rotate the right colon and small bowel superiorly and to the left, exposing the vena cava and aorta.

Kocher Maneuver

  • Indication: Mobilization of the duodenum to expose the second and third portions, the infrahepatic IVC, renal vessels, and posterior pancreatic head.
  • Technique: Incise the peritoneal reflection lateral to the duodenum, rotating it medially.

2.5

Systematic Exploration

Directed packing

  • Bleeding Control: Upon entering the abdomen, immediately control major bleeding via directed packing and compression.
  • Avoid Blind Packing: Blind four-quadrant packing is less effective than directed compression. Do not pack uninvolved quadrants (e.g., in isolated left upper quadrant stab wounds).
  • Bowel Protection: Completely eviscerate the small bowel to expose the retroperitoneum, keeping it wrapped in warm, moist towels.

Systematic Exploration Protocol

  1. Small Bowel: Inspect from the ligament of Treitz to the rectum. Retract the transverse colon superiorly to expose the ligament of Treitz. Examine both sides of the bowel and the mesenteric border.
  2. Colon: Eviscerate the small bowel to inspect the right and left colon. Explore any paracolic hematomas in penetrating injuries.
  3. Stomach & Proximal Duodenum: Inspect the anterior stomach wall by pulling the transverse colon inferiorly. Inspect the posterior stomach wall and pancreas by dividing the gastrocolic ligament to enter the lesser sac.
  4. Solid Organs & Diaphragm: Palpate the liver and spleen. Place laparotomy pads behind them to elevate them into the wound. Palpate the diaphragm and verify the presence and size of both kidneys.

PILLAR 3

Technical Pearls & Bail-Out Strategies

3.1

Consultant Pearls

Pearl #1: Temporary Aortic Occlusion For severe, non-compressible infradiaphragmatic bleeding, perform manual compression of the aorta at the diaphragm. Aortic cross-clamping can be facilitated by dividing the left crus of the diaphragm at the 2 o'clock position (avascular plane). If a supramesocolic hematoma prevents this, perform a left thoracotomy for thoracic aortic clamping, or utilize REBOA.
Pearl #2: Hematoma Exploration Rules
  • Penetrating Trauma: Always explore all retroperitoneal hematomas. Exception: Stable retrohepatic hematomas (Zone 4) should not be opened because exposure carries a high risk of catastrophic hemorrhage from the retrohepatic IVC or hepatic veins.
  • Blunt Trauma: Do not explore stable hematomas. Exception: Paraduodenal hematomas, or any large, rapidly expanding, or leaking hematomas must be explored.
Pearl #3: Venous Access Site Restriction In penetrating pelvic or abdominal trauma with hemodynamic instability, avoid placing venous access lines in the lower extremities. If a major iliac vein or inferior vena cava injury is present, resuscitation fluids will escape through the vascular injury into the retroperitoneum.
3.2

The Bail-Out Points

  • Failure of packing: Gauze packing is a temporary compression maneuver. If packing does not arrest hemorrhage, do not proceed with closure or leave the operating room. Re-explore, isolate the bleeding vessel, and secure control.
  • Multiple bowel perforations: In cases of multiple perforations located in close proximity, resecting the entire involved segment and performing a single anastomosis is faster and safer than performing multiple separate repairs. Perform resection only after all perforations are mapped.
  • Ongoing coagulopathy: If the patient becomes hypothermic (<35°C), acidotic, and coagulopathic, immediately abort definitive repairs. Apply a temporary abdominal closure device and transfer the patient to the ICU.
PILLAR 4

Post-operative Care & Longitudinal Follow-up

4.1

ICU Resuscitation Targets & Monitoring

Structured ICU Resuscitation Goals

  • Temperature: Maintain patient temperature >35°C using active warming blankets and warmed ventilators.
  • Acid-Base Balance: Resolve metabolic acidosis (correct base deficit to <4 mmol/L, clear lactate).
  • Coagulopathy Correction: Guided by viscoelastic assays (TEG/ROTEM). Administer platelets, cryoprecipitate, and fresh frozen plasma.

Longitudinal Monitoring

  • Intra-abdominal Pressure (IAP): Monitor bladder pressures every 4 hours if the fascia is closed or tight.
  • Airway Pressures: Watch for rising peak airway pressures, which indicate diaphragm elevation due to visceral edema.
  • Fluid Balance: Limit crystalloid infusions. Maintain MAP >65 mmHg using vasopressors to prevent worsening tissue edema.
4.2

Intestinal Anastomosis Rules

  • Technique Outcomes: Outcomes are equivalent for hand-sewn versus stapled anastomoses in trauma settings.
  • Layer Configuration: One-layer versus two-layer techniques show similar leak rates. Continuous versus interrupted sutures show equivalent performance.
  • Pediatric Considerations: A single-layer anastomosis is recommended in pediatric cases to minimize the risk of anastomotic stenosis.
4.3

Abdominal Closure & Drainage

  • Closed Drainage: Indicated only in selected cases, such as complex liver or pancreatic head injuries where bile or pancreatic duct leaks are anticipated. Avoid routine prophylactic drainage.
  • Fascial Closure: Attempt primary fascial closure when visceral edema allows. If the patient is at risk of abdominal compartment syndrome, leave the fascia open.
  • Skin Management: Leave the skin open in contaminated cases (e.g., hollow viscus spills) to prevent surgical site infections, fascial dehiscence, or evisceration.
PILLAR 5

Complication Management

5.1

Intra-abdominal Hypertension & Compartment Syndrome

  • Intra-abdominal Hypertension (IAH): Defined as a sustained intra-abdominal pressure >12 mmHg.
  • Abdominal Compartment Syndrome (ACS): Defined as a sustained IAP >20 mmHg associated with new organ dysfunction (e.g., oliguria, rising peak airway pressures, hypotension).
  • Surgical Decompression: Perform emergency decompressive laparotomy if ACS develops. Open the fascia and establish a temporary vacuum closure system.
  • Medical Adjuncts: Optimize sedation, administer neuromuscular blockers, perform nasogastric and rectal tube decompression, and utilize fluid restriction/diuresis.
5.2

Wound Dehiscence and Surgical Site Infections

  • Early Recognition: Indicated by erythematous wound margins, purulent drainage, or fascial separation.
  • Management: Open the wound, obtain cultures, debride non-viable tissue, and initiate negative pressure wound therapy or wet-to-dry dressings.
  • Delayed Reconstruction: Plan reconstruction for ventral hernias at 6-12 months after the initial trauma insult.
