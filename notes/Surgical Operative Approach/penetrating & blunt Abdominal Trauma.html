Penetrating & Blunt Abdominal Trauma

Damage Control, Diagnostic Pathways, and Operative Atlas

Trauma Surgery Damage Control Resuscitation Organ Injury Scaling
SECTION 1 Core Foundations & The Evidence Base
1.1

Pathophysiology & Clinical Framework

Abdominal trauma is the third leading cause of traumatic death worldwide, accounting for approximately 10-15% of all trauma fatalities. In the United States alone, roughly 1.5 million emergency department visits annually involve abdominal trauma. Blunt mechanisms dominate civilian practice (85-90%), driven by road traffic collisions, falls, and assault; penetrating injuries, principally gunshot wounds (GSW) and stab wounds (SW), predominate in urban trauma centres in South Africa, the United States, and Latin America, constituting 20-40% of admissions in these environments. Male sex (3:1 ratio), age 15-45, and alcohol intoxication are consistent epidemiological associations.

Mechanism-to-Injury Map

Understanding mechanism is not academic. It directly governs which organs are at risk and how aggressively you should pursue occult injury.

Blunt Injury — Three Force Vectors:

  • Deceleration shear: At solid organ-mesentery junctions (liver laceration at falciform ligament; duodenal hematoma; renal pedicle avulsion). Signature injury in MVA.
  • Compression / crush: Bowel against spine (seatbelt syndrome, L2-L3 Chance fracture + mesenteric tear). Direct blow to solid organs.
  • Burst / hydraulic: Intraperitoneal pressure spike. Hollow viscus rupture (stomach when full; bladder with full distension).

Penetrating Injury — Tissue Destruction Determinants:

  • Stab wounds: Low-velocity, tissue disruption proportional to blade trajectory. 30% do NOT enter the peritoneum despite anterior abdominal location.
  • Low-velocity GSW (< 600 m/s, handgun): Permanent cavity = missile diameter. Bowel most frequently injured (50%).
  • High-velocity GSW (> 800 m/s, rifle): Temporary cavitation 30x missile diameter. Massive energy transfer; bowel blast injury beyond visual margin of injury.
  • Shotgun: Range-dependent. < 7m = devastation equivalent to high-velocity; > 7m = low-velocity scatter pattern.

The Haemorrhage Cascade

Solid organ injury initiates a predictable physiological spiral. Intraperitoneal haemorrhage from hepatic, splenic, or mesenteric sources triggers baroreceptor-mediated tachycardia and vasoconstriction. If uncontrolled, the patient enters the lethal triad: acidosis (base deficit < -6), hypothermia (< 35°C), coagulopathy (INR > 1.5). Recognition of the lethal triad mandates a Damage Control Surgery (DCS) strategy rather than definitive repair. Class III-IV shock with peritonism = operative. The physiological threshold for DCS is: pH < 7.2, temperature < 35°C, or transfusion > 10 units pRBC within 6 hours.

1.2

Key Learning Objectives

  • Rapidly stratify abdominal trauma patients into non-operative, interventional radiology, or immediate operative pathways using mechanism, physiology, and FAST/CT data.
  • Execute a systematic Primary and Secondary Survey for abdominal trauma, identifying hard signs mandating immediate laparotomy without CT.
  • Describe the AAST OIS grading for liver, spleen, kidney, bowel, and major vasculature, and correlate grade with management strategy.
  • Outline the Damage Control Surgery philosophy: initial haemostasis, contamination control, planned relook, and staged definitive repair.
  • Apply current EAST/WSES NOM criteria for blunt solid organ injury including role of Angioembolisation in Grade III-V splenic/hepatic trauma.
  • Manage the open abdomen: temporary closure techniques, ACS prevention, and fascial closure strategy.
  • Navigate the difficult penetrating trauma scenario: right-sided colon injuries with faecal contamination, juxtahepatic venous haemorrhage, and Zone 1 retroperitoneal haematoma.

1.4

The Consultant's Debate

Grade IV Splenic Lacerations

Grade IV splenic lacerations (involving segmental or hilar vessels producing major devascularisation > 25% of the spleen) sit precisely at the fault line of modern trauma surgery.

  • FOR Non-Operative Management (with Angioembolisation): Angioembolisation reduces NOM failure in Grade IV from 35-40% to < 12% (EAST 2024). Splenic salvage preserves immune function. Post-splenectomy sepsis (OPSI): lifetime risk 3.2%, mortality 50%. CT-angio reveals 'blush' (active extravasation) in ~60% Grade IV — direct therapeutic target for IR. Requires haemodynamic stability (SBP > 90 after 1-2L), no associated hollow viscus injury, ICU monitoring.
  • AGAINST (Proceed to Operative Intervention): NOM failure in Grade IV requiring delayed splenectomy carries higher morbidity than index splenectomy. 'Blush' on CT does not guarantee successful embolisation. IR suite delay in unstable patient = preventable death. Associated injuries (pancreatic tail, left renal, diaphragm) alter operative plan — best identified under direct vision.
The Consultant's Position Grade IV with haemodynamic stability and CT blush = angioembolisation + 48-72hr ICU NOM with serial exams. Any deterioration (HR > 120, falling Hb > 2g/dL within 6h, peritonism) = proceed to OR immediately. Do NOT let IR delay a crashing patient. The spleen is worth saving, but not at the cost of life.

2.2

Resuscitation, ERAS & Perioperative Optimisation

Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR)

DCR is a package: permissive hypotension, immediate haemostatic resuscitation, and avoidance of crystalloid excess.

  • Target SBP: 80-90 mmHg (MAP 50-65) in penetrating trauma until surgical haemostasis. 90-100 mmHg in blunt trauma with suspected TBI.
  • Ratio: pRBC:FFP:Platelets = 1:1:1. Activation of MTP at > 10 units pRBC anticipated within 24h.
  • Tranexamic Acid (TXA): 1g IV over 10 min within 3 hours of injury, then 1g over 8 hours. Do NOT give beyond 3 hours.
  • Calcium replenishment: Ionised Ca2+ < 1.1 mmol/L after transfusion = 1g CaCl2 IV (citrate chelation in stored blood).
  • Avoid the Lethal Triad: External warming, restrict crystalloid (target < 1.5L total pre-haemostasis), bicarbonate ONLY if pH < 7.1 and ventilated.

ERAS in Trauma

The 2025 Principles: Traditional ERAS was built for elective surgery. Trauma ERAS applies the same logic where physiology allows.

  • Early enteral nutrition within 24-48h post-DCS (nasojejunal past the pylorus in open abdomen).
  • Multimodal analgesia: regional nerve blocks, ketamine infusion, paracetamol. Opioid minimisation from day one.
  • DVT prophylaxis: enoxaparin 40mg SC once haemostasis confirmed and platelets > 50.

SECTION 3 The Operative Atlas
3.1

Surgical Anatomy & Danger Zones

The abdomen in trauma is not the same as the abdomen in elective surgery. Blood, clot, and distorted anatomy conspire against you. Knowing the fixed landmarks before you cut is non-negotiable.

Retroperitoneal Zone Decision Rules

  • Zone I (Central / Midline): Contains abdominal aorta, IVC, superior mesenteric vessels, celiac axis, proximal renal vessels. ALWAYS explore in penetrating trauma. Explore in blunt if expanding or pulsatile.
  • Zone II (Lateral / Perinephric): Perirenal fat, renal vessels, kidney. Penetrating: explore if medially located or suspected vascular injury. Blunt: do NOT explore unless expanding, pulsatile, or urogram shows devascularisation.
  • Zone III (Pelvic): Iliac vessels, bladder, distal ureter, rectum. Blunt pelvic haematoma: almost never explore (disastrous bleeding if venous plexus disrupted). Use pelvic packing + angioembolisation. Penetrating: explore both sides.

Neurovascular No-Go Zones

  • Hepatic veins at caval confluence — blind haemostat application = IVC tear = exsanguination.
  • Pancreatic neck — duct transection here is irreparable without formal pancreatectomy.
  • SMA at root — circumferential dissection risks thrombosis; posterior wall repair is blind.
  • Sacral venous plexus — posterior Zone III pelvic exploration in blunt trauma disrupts this plexus and results in torrential uncontrollable haemorrhage.

Exploratory Laparotomy for Abdominal Trauma

  • Step 1: Positioning & Incision. Midline laparotomy from xiphisternum to pubis. QUICK. One knife stroke, electrocautery through subcutaneous fat, straight through linea alba. Do not pause for haemostasis at skin edge.
  • Step 2: Initial Haemostasis (Four Quadrant Pack). Immediately pack all four quadrants with laparotomy pads. Manually compress the aorta at the diaphragmatic hiatus if the patient is near-arrest. Allow anaesthesia 2-3 minutes to catch up with volume.
  • Step 3: Systematic Exploration. Remove packs sequentially. Survey: (1) Anterior gastric wall, (2) Lesser sac, (3) Duodenum, (4) Small bowel from Treitz to ileocaecal valve, (5) Mesentery, (6) Right colon, (7) Left colon to rectosigmoid, (8) Liver, (9) Spleen, (10) Retroperitoneum.
  • Step 4: Control Active Haemorrhage. Liver: manual compression, Pringle manoeuvre. Spleen: hilum compression, splenectomy. Mesenteric vessels: clamping then repair/ligation.
  • Step 5: Control Contamination. Rapidly close bowel perforations with Babcock clamps or linear stapler. Do NOT perform anastomosis in damage control phase. Copiously irrigate with warm saline.
  • Step 6: Damage Control vs. Definitive Repair. Apply physiological criteria: pH < 7.2, Temp < 35°C, ongoing coagulopathy = STOP, pack, close temporarily. If physiology acceptable: proceed to definitive repair.
  • Step 7: Temporary Abdominal Closure (TAC). Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT/Vac-Pak) preferred. Target fascial closure at 48-72h relook.
  • Step 8: Relook Laparotomy (48-72h). Systematic re-evaluation, unpack, assess bowel viability, perform definitive repair.

SECTION 4 Spatial Awareness & Radiological Correlation
4.1

Operative 3D Spatial Awareness

When you stand at the patient's right side and look through a midline laparotomy wound, this is your three-dimensional landscape:

  • Immediately Deep to the Skin: The falciform ligament bisects the field in the upper midline. Divide it immediately to gain access to the right hepatic lobe. Directly below the xiphisternum lies the gastrohepatic ligament.
  • Upper Abdomen (Right): The liver occupies the right upper quadrant. Deep and posterior to the right lobe is the retrohepatic IVC. You cannot see it without mobilising the liver.
  • Upper Abdomen (Left): The spleen sits in the left subphrenic space, tethered by ligaments. The tail of the pancreas touches the splenic hilum in 30% of people — divide the hilum too close and you may enter the pancreatic tail.
  • Central Zone: Lift the transverse colon upward to access the root of the small bowel mesentery, where the SMA and SMV are palpable — this is Zone I territory.
  • The Pelvis: In the male: bladder sits anteriorly, rectum posteriorly. The space of Retzius fills with clot in pelvic fracture. In the female: uterus and ovaries occupy the central pelvis — assess for gynaecological injury.
4.2

High-Yield Named CT Signs

  • The Sentinel Clot Sign: Highest density clot marks the site of maximal haemorrhage.
  • The Whirl Sign: Twisted mesenteric fat and vessels. In trauma = mesenteric injury with torsion.
  • The Seatbelt Sign on CT: Abdominal wall contusion/haematoma in a band distribution. When combined with mesenteric stranding: 50% sensitivity for bowel injury.
  • Pseudoaneurysm: Saccular contrast-filled outpouching, same density as aorta on arterial phase, persisting on delayed. Does NOT enlarge between phases (unlike active blush). Requires elective embolisation.
SECTION 5 Senior Fellowship & Board Review
5.1

High-Order Viva Scenarios - The Hot Seat

HOT SEAT 1 Q: A 28-year-old male motorcyclist arrives at 22:00. GCS 14, HR 128, BP 88/60. Significant abdominal tenderness. FAST: large amount of free fluid. You are the senior registrar on-call.
A: This man has Class III haemorrhagic shock with haemoperitoneum on FAST. The answer is NOT to go to CT. The decision is immediate exploratory laparotomy. Activate MTP, TXA 1g IV now, crossmatch 6 units, alert OR. Common mistake: sending an unstable FAST-positive patient to CT. This kills.
HOT SEAT 2 Q: Intraoperatively for a Grade IV liver laceration, you apply Pringle and pack. Bleeding slows but does not stop. You remove right-sided packs and find torrential bleeding from the posterior surface of the right hepatic lobe.
A: This is a juxtahepatic or retrohepatic IVC injury until proven otherwise — the most lethal abdominal injury. Immediate manual bimanual compression of the right lobe against the diaphragm. Maintain Pringle. Do NOT blindly apply clamps. Call for cardiac surgery backup (Atriocaval shunt). Damage control: Pack tightly, close, ICU. Do NOT attempt hepatic resection on a coagulopathic patient.
HOT SEAT 3 Q: A 35-year-old woman admitted 72 hours ago after a blunt splenic Grade II injury managed non-operatively is now febrile (38.9°C), HR 110, and reports worsening left shoulder tip pain. Hb has fallen from 10.2 to 7.8 g/dL.
A: This is NOM failure — either delayed splenic rupture or pseudoaneurysm rupture. Left shoulder tip pain (Kehr's sign) = left subphrenic free blood irritating the diaphragm. CT angiogram needed if stable; if haemodynamically deteriorating: OR.
HOT SEAT 5 Q: A 19-year-old unrestrained passenger in high-speed MVA has free fluid between bowel loops with mesenteric stranding but no solid organ injury, no free air, and no active blush on CT. Vitals stable.
A: This is a high-risk scenario for occult bowel injury. Serial examination every 2 hours is mandatory. NBM. Deteriorating exam at any time = diagnostic laparoscopy or exploratory laparotomy. The combination of seatbelt sign + CT mesenteric changes + mechanism = bowel injury proven until exploratory laparotomy demonstrates otherwise.
HOT SEAT 6 Q: Post-operatively day 2 following damage control laparotomy with open abdomen (Vac-Pak closure), peak airway pressures are rising, urine output has fallen to 15mL/hr, and abdomen is tense.
A: Abdominal Compartment Syndrome (ACS). Intra-abdominal Pressure (IAP) via bladder manometry: expect > 20 mmHg with new organ dysfunction. Management for Grade III-IV: decompressive laparotomy — open the Vac-Pak, reassess bowel viability. Formula: APP = MAP - IAP. Target APP > 60mmHg.
