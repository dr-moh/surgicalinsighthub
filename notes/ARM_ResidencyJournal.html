Brutal Rule
Classify every ARM by Krickenbeck before touching the patient — the operative approach is dictated entirely by the type, and operating without knowing the fistula level is how you destroy a sphincter complex that was never going to be repaired again.
Krickenbeck 2005 replaced Wingspread. Know it cold. The level of the fistula determines colostomy vs primary repair, PSARP vs LAARP, and the expected continence outcome.
01

Definition & Embryology

ARM = failure of the urorectal septum to descend completely by week 7 of gestation, leaving the hindgut and urogenital sinus incompletely separated. The spectrum runs from a simple covered anus to a complex cloaca where rectum, vagina, and urethra share a single perineal opening. The earlier the arrest, the higher the malformation and the worse the prognosis.
ParameterDetail
Incidence1 : 5,000 live births
Sex ratioMales slightly more common; fistula type differs by sex
Embryologic eventUrorectal septum descends weeks 4–7; arrest = ARM
Spectrum (low to high)Perineal fistula → anal stenosis → vestibular fistula → rectourethral bulbar → rectourethral prostatic → rectovesical → cloaca → no fistula
CloacaFemales only; single perineal opening; rectum + vagina + urethra converge into common channel

Krickenbeck replaced the Wingspread classification (1984) as the international standard. It groups ARMs by the site of the fistula opening, which directly determines the operative approach. Knowing the group = knowing the operation.

04

Surgical Anatomy & Continence Determinants

Continence after ARM repair depends on three things: the quality of the sphincter complex present at birth, the sacral nerve supply (S2–S4), and the accuracy of muscle placement at repair. The surgeon cannot create muscle that does not exist — they can only place what is there in the correct position. The sacrum predicts the nerves; the nerves predict the outcome.
StructureRole in ContinenceARM Relevance
PuborectalisCreates anorectal angle; most important for gross continencePresent in most ARMs; must be identified and preserved at PSARP
External sphincterVoluntary squeeze; prevents soiling with urgencyHypoplastic in high ARMs; identified by muscle stimulator intraop
Internal sphincterResting tone; prevents passive soilingOften absent or rudimentary in high ARMs
Levator ani complexPelvic floor support; contributes to continenceMidline split at PSARP — must be reapproximated precisely
Sacral nerve roots S2–S4Innervate all sphincter musclesSacral agenesis = absent nerves = no continence regardless of repair quality
SacrumBony landmark for nerve root presenceSacral ratio quantifies nerve supply — see Section 05
05

Sacral Ratio — Prognosis Calculator

The sacral ratio is a plain X-ray measurement that predicts continence before any operation is performed. It quantifies how much sacrum is present, which correlates directly with the number of intact sacral nerve roots. Calculate it on every ARM X-ray. Document it in every operative note.

Peña's rule: The sacral ratio is the single best predictor of long-term continence. Quote it in every ARM presentation. Examiners will ask for it.

Spinal Anomalies, Tethered Cord & Currarino Triad

Tethered spinal cord occurs in 25–35% of ARM patients. If undetected before PSARP, the traction on the cord during positioning and repair can cause permanent neurological injury. Screen before you operate.
AnomalyIncidenceConsequence if MissedScreening
Tethered cord25–35%Neurological deterioration post-PSARP; bladder/bowel dysfunctionMRI spine before PSARP
Sacral agenesis (partial)30–40%Predicts poor continence; quantify with sacral ratioAP + lateral pelvis X-ray
Presacral massRare — but lethal if missedAnterior meningocele or teratoma; rupture = meningitisMRI pelvis; do NOT biopsy transrectally
Currarino TriadRareSacral agenesis + presacral mass + ARM — all three togetherMRI; genetic (MNX1 mutation)
Never Miss

  • Presacral mass: never biopsy transrectally — anterior meningocele rupture causes meningitis
  • Currarino triad = sacral defect (scimitar sacrum on X-ray) + presacral mass + ARM — order MRI pelvis immediately

Newborn Assessment — The 24-Hour Rule

ARM is a clinical diagnosis made on perineal inspection. The 24-hour rule: wait 24 hours before deciding on operative approach — meconium takes time to track through a fistula, and the perineum needs time to show its anatomy. Rushing to the OR in the first hours leads to misclassification.
FindingMalesFemalesSignificance
No anal openingInspect perineum, scrotum, median raphe for meconiumInspect vestibule, vagina for meconiumConfirms ARM; type TBD
Meconium on perineumPerineal fistula — low ARMPerineal or vestibular fistulaPrimary repair possible
Meconium in urineRectourethral fistula (bulbar or prostatic)N/AHigh ARM — colostomy first
Single perineal openingN/ACloaca — single opening for rectum + vagina + urethraColostomy + urinary diversion
Two openings (urethra + fistula)N/AVestibular fistula — two openings visibleColostomy → PSARP
Flat perineum / absent midline grooveHigh ARM — poor muscle complexHigh ARMPoor prognosis sign
Anal stenosisNarrow but present anal openingNarrow but present anal openingDilatation or anoplasty
24-hour rule: Inspect at birth, then re-examine at 16–24 hours. Meconium tracking through a fistula takes time. Do not classify and operate before this window unless the baby is unstable.

Initial Management Algorithm

The first decision is binary: primary repair now (low ARM, stable neonate) or colostomy first (high/intermediate ARM, cloaca, unstable neonate). This decision is made within the first 24–48 hours of life based on Krickenbeck type and clinical status. Getting this wrong means a second operation to undo a primary repair done on the wrong type.
ARM TypeInitial OperationTimingDefinitive Repair
Perineal fistulaPrimary anoplasty / cutbackNewborn period (day 1–3)Done — no colostomy needed
Anal stenosisHegar dilatation or anoplastyNewborn periodDilatation program
Vestibular fistulaDivided sigmoid colostomyDay 1–3PSARP at 1–3 months
Rectourethral bulbarDivided sigmoid colostomyDay 1–3PSARP at 1–3 months
Rectourethral prostaticDivided sigmoid colostomyDay 1–3PSARP or LAARP at 1–3 months
Rectovesical (bladder neck)Divided sigmoid colostomyDay 1–3LAARP at 1–3 months
CloacaDivided sigmoid colostomy + urinary diversion if neededDay 1–3Cloacal repair at 3–6 months
No fistulaDivided sigmoid colostomyDay 1–3PSARP at 1–3 months
11

Colostomy — Divided Sigmoid, Not Loop

The colostomy for ARM is a divided sigmoid colostomy with a mucous fistula. A loop colostomy is contraindicated because stool can spill into the distal limb, contaminate the urinary tract via the fistula, and make the distal colostogram uninterpretable. Loop colostomy in ARM = preventable complication.
FeatureDivided Sigmoid ColostomyLoop Colostomy (Contraindicated)
Stool spillage to distal limbNone — completely separatedYes — contamination of fistula and urinary tract
Distal colostogram qualityClean — accurate fistula anatomyContaminated — unreliable
UTI risk via fistulaEliminatedHigh — recurrent UTI, urosepsis
Mucous fistulaSeparate small opening for distal limb drainageN/A
Colostomy siteSigmoid — enough distal bowel for pull-through—
Exam Trap
  • Loop colostomy in ARM is wrong. If an MCQ or viva asks about colostomy type for ARM, the answer is always divided sigmoid colostomy with mucous fistula.
  • Transverse colostomy is also wrong — leaves insufficient distal bowel for pull-through.
12

PSARP — Posterior Sagittal Anorectoplasty (Peña Technique)

PSARP is the definitive repair for most ARMs. Described by Alberto Peña in 1982, it provides direct visualization of the entire sphincter complex through a posterior sagittal incision. The key principle: place the rectum precisely within the muscle complex — not just near it.
StepDetail
PositionProne jackknife; electrical muscle stimulator on field to map sphincter
IncisionPosterior sagittal midline from coccyx to anal dimple
Muscle identificationElectrical stimulator identifies external sphincter and puborectalis — mark with sutures
Levator ani splitMidline split of levator complex; must be reapproximated precisely at closure
Fistula divisionIdentify and divide rectourethral fistula; close urethral side with absorbable sutures
Rectal mobilizationMobilize rectum to reach perineum without tension; preserve blood supply
AnoplastyPlace rectum within muscle complex; anastomose to skin at anal dimple
ClosureReapproximate levator ani, external sphincter; skin closure
Outcome determinantAccuracy of muscle placement + quality of sacrum/nerves present at birth
Peña's principle: The surgeon's job is to place the rectum within the center of the sphincter complex. The nerves and muscles present at birth determine the final continence result — not the surgeon's technique alone.

Laparoscopic-Assisted PSARP (LAARP)

LAARP combines laparoscopic dissection of the high rectum and fistula with a limited posterior sagittal approach for the anoplasty. It is the preferred approach for rectovesical (bladder neck) fistulas and high prostatic fistulas where the rectum cannot be reached from below without extensive dissection. The laparoscope provides direct visualization of the fistula at the bladder neck — impossible to see safely from the perineum alone.
FeatureLAARPOpen PSARP
IndicationRectovesical fistula, high prostatic fistulaMost ARMs including bulbar, vestibular, no fistula
Fistula visualizationDirect laparoscopic view of bladder neck fistulaBlind dissection from below for very high fistulas
Muscle complex accessLimited posterior sagittal for anoplasty onlyFull posterior sagittal — complete muscle visualization
Rectal mobilizationLaparoscopic — preserves blood supplyOpen — more extensive dissection
Trocar placementUmbilical + 2 lateral ports; patient supine then prone for anoplastyProne jackknife throughout
Continence outcomesComparable to open for high ARMsGold standard for low/intermediate ARMs

Cloacal Repair — Common Channel Length Determines Approach

Cloaca is the most complex ARM. The common channel length (measured on distal colostogram or at cystoscopy) is the single most important determinant of operative approach and prognosis. Short channel = simpler repair = better outcome. Long channel = total urogenital mobilization = higher complication rate.
Common ChannelApproachTechniquePrognosis
< 3 cmPosterior sagittal only (Peña)Total urogenital mobilization (TUM) — mobilize urethra + vagina as unit, bring to perineumFair to good continence
> 3 cmCombined abdominoperineal (Hendren)Laparotomy + posterior sagittal; vaginal replacement often neededPoor — complex reconstruction, high complication rate
StepDetail
Urinary diversion at colostomyVesicostomy or catheterizable channel if bladder outlet obstructed
Distal colostogramMeasures common channel length; defines anatomy before repair
Cystoscopy / vaginoscopyAt time of repair — confirms anatomy, guides dissection
Total urogenital mobilization (TUM)Peña technique for short channel — mobilize urethra + vagina en bloc
Vaginal replacementLong channel — vagina may need bowel segment (sigmoid) for reconstruction
TimingCloacal repair at 3–6 months after colostomy

Postoperative Care — Anal Dilatation Program

After PSARP, the neoanal opening will stricture without a dilatation program. Hegar dilators are used in a structured protocol starting 2 weeks post-repair. The goal is to reach the target size for age before colostomy closure. Colostomy closure before reaching target size = anastomotic stricture = reoperation.

4–8 monthsHegar 13

Ward Round / Never Miss

Every ARM Patient — Non-Negotiable Checklist
  • Calculate sacral ratio on every ARM X-ray — document it in the notes; it predicts continence and must be communicated to the family
  • Echo before any anesthesia — cardiac anomaly in 30%; undiagnosed TOF on induction = cardiac arrest
  • Renal US in first 24–48h — bilateral renal agenesis is incompatible with life; unilateral changes long-term management
  • MRI spine before PSARP — tethered cord in 25–35%; prone positioning during PSARP can cause permanent cord injury if tethered cord is not released first
  • Never do a loop colostomy — always divided sigmoid with mucous fistula; loop colostomy causes UTI, urosepsis, and an uninterpretable distal colostogram
  • Distal colostogram before PSARP — defines fistula anatomy; operating without it is operating blind
  • Presacral mass: never biopsy transrectally — anterior meningocele rupture = meningitis; approach anteriorly or posteriorly via sacral route
  • Start Hegar dilatations at 2 weeks post-PSARP — delay = stricture; stricture = redo operation
  • Do not close colostomy until target Hegar size is reached — confirm size at clinic visit before booking colostomy closure
  • Offer bowel management program to every soiling patient — regardless of ARM type; transforms quality of life
