Comprehensive study guide focusing on sliding vs. paraesophageal hernias, open abdominal repair steps, complications management, and resource-limited settings.

Foregut Core Open Surgery Focus 5 Pillars
PILLAR 1

Definition, Anatomical Staging & Hiatal Anatomy

1.1

Definition & Classification

A hiatal hernia is the protrusion of abdominal contents, most commonly the stomach, into the thoracic cavity through the esophageal hiatus of the diaphragm. It is the most common abnormality found on upper GI imaging and endoscopy.

Anatomic Classification

  • Type I (Sliding Hiatal Hernia): Represents 95% of all hiatal hernias. The gastroesophageal junction (GEJ) migrates above the diaphragm while stomach alignment remains normal. This type is strongly associated with GERD due to the disruption of the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) mechanism.
  • Type II (Pure Paraesophageal Hernia): Represents 5% of hernias. The GEJ remains in its normal position below the diaphragm, but the gastric fundus herniates through the hiatus alongside the esophagus. A true hernia sac is present. It rarely causes reflux but carries a significant risk of obstruction, volvulus, and strangulation.
  • Type III (Mixed Hiatal Hernia): The most common large paraesophageal hernia. It combines features of both Types I and II, with both the GEJ and the fundus migrating above the diaphragm.
  • Type IV (Complex Hiatal Hernia): Characterized by the herniation of other abdominal organs (colon, omentum, spleen, or small bowel) into the chest cavity in addition to the stomach.
Nomenclature Notes
  • Sliding Hernia = Type I hiatal hernia.
  • Paraesophageal Hernia = Types II, III, and IV collectively (pure Type II is exceptionally rare).
  • Giant Paraesophageal Hernia = Defined as >30% of the stomach herniated or >5 cm of thoracic stomach.
Clinical Pearl Type I hernias primarily cause chemical/reflux symptoms (GERD). In contrast, Types II–IV hernias present with mechanical symptoms (chest pain, dysphagia, early satiety, and iron-deficiency anemia from Cameron ulcers) and carry a life-threatening risk of gastric volvulus or strangulation.
1.2

Anatomy of the Esophageal Hiatus & Relations

Surgical Landmarks

  • Esophageal Hiatus: Formed predominantly by the right crus of the diaphragm, crossing at the T10–T11 level. The normal diameter is approximately 1.5–2 cm. It transmits the esophagus, the anterior and posterior vagus nerves, and the phrenoesophageal membrane.
  • Phrenoesophageal Membrane: A circumferential connective tissue layer connecting the esophagus to the diaphragmatic hiatus, helping to anchor the GEJ within the abdomen. Weakening or attenuation of this membrane is the pathophysiological basis of hiatal hernias.
  • Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES): A physiologic high-pressure zone (10–30 mmHg) comprised of intrinsic esophageal smooth muscle reinforced by the crural pinchcock. A minimum of 1.5–2 cm of intra-abdominal esophageal length is critical to maintaining the antireflux barrier.
  • Vagus Nerves: The anterior and posterior vagal trunks run along the esophageal wall and must be identified and preserved during mediastinal dissection. Bilateral injury leads to gastric stasis and gastroparesis.
  • Ligaments & Vessels: Gastrosplenic ligament (contains short gastric arteries), gastrocolic ligament, and gastrophrenic ligament. Note that an aberrant left hepatic artery arising from the left gastric artery (found in 25% of cases) may cross the hiatus and must be protected.
Attending's Anatomy Rule The phrenoesophageal membrane is the key structure for a durable repair. During surgery, it must be dissected and excised circumferentially off the crural margins to allow complete reduction of the hernia sac and mobilize sufficient intra-abdominal esophageal length.
PILLAR 2

Epidemiology, Etiology & Pathophysiology

2.1

Epidemiology & Incidence

  • Prevalence: Extremely common; radiographic abnormalities are found in 10–50% of adults, and endoscopic findings are noted in 20–60%. Autopsy series show hiatal hernias in up to 60% of the elderly population.
  • Demographics: Strongly increases with age (most symptomatic paraesophageal hernias present in the 60s–70s). Female predominance for paraesophageal hernias (1.5:1 ratio). Obesity (BMI >30) increases the risk of developing a hiatal hernia by 2–3 fold.
  • Morbidity Metrics:
    • Approximately 10–20% of patients with Type I hernias suffer from symptomatic GERD.
    • More than 80% of patients with paraesophageal hernias become symptomatic by age 70.
    • The risk of acute gastric strangulation is estimated at 1–2% per year; emergency repair carries a high mortality rate of 10–30%, compared to <1% for elective cases.
    • Cameron ulcers are identified in 5–10% of large hernias and are a major source of chronic iron-deficiency anemia.
2.2

Etiology & Risk Factors

  • Primary (Idiopathic): Age-related degeneration and weakening of the elastic tissue in the phrenoesophageal membrane.
  • Secondary (Acquired): Conditions that chronically increase intra-abdominal pressure:
    • Obesity (BMI >30), chronic cough (COPD, smoking, asthma), chronic constipation, ascites, pregnancy, and repeated heavy lifting.
    • Prior upper GI or bariatric surgery (disrupting the crural fibers and phrenoesophageal attachments).
    • Connective tissue disorders (e.g., Marfan syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, scleroderma).
2.3

Pathophysiological Mechanisms

Type I (Sliding Hernia) Pathophysiology

Chronic laxity of the phrenoesophageal membrane allows the GEJ to slide upward into the low-pressure thoracic cavity. This displacement eliminates the acute angle of His (which forms a natural "flutter valve") and separates the LES from the crural diaphragm. The loss of crural reinforcement, combined with decreased LES tone, permits gastric acid reflux. Prolonged acid exposure impairs esophageal clearance, leading to esophagitis, strictures, Barrett's esophagus, and adenocarcinoma.

Types II–IV (Paraesophageal Hernia) Pathophysiology

A focal defect in the anterior or lateral aspect of the phrenoesophageal membrane allows the gastric fundus to herniate alongside the esophagus while the GEJ remains anchored to the retroperitoneum. The herniating stomach pulls a peritoneal sac with it. Over time, the stomach can rotate within the mediastinum. Rotation along its longitudinal axis is called **organoaxial volvulus** (80% of cases), while rotation along its transverse mesenteric axis is called **mesenteroaxial volvulus** (20%). Volvulus can lead to gastric outlet obstruction, ischemia, necrosis, and perforation.

Cameron Ulcer Pathogenesis Cameron ulcers are linear mucosal erosions located on the gastric folds at the level of the diaphragmatic crura. They are caused by mechanical trauma (friction from the stomach sliding back and forth during respiration) combined with local ischemia from diaphragmatic compression. They lead to chronic, low-grade blood loss and severe iron-deficiency anemia.
PILLAR 3

Pathology, Presentation & Diagnostic Workup

3.1

Pathological Findings

  • Macroscopic: In Type I hernias, the GEJ sits >2 cm above the diaphragmatic hiatus with gastric mucosal folds crossing the diaphragm. In paraesophageal hernias, a large peritoneal sac contains the gastric fundus (and potentially colon/omentum) within the posterior mediastinum, often with linear Cameron ulcers.
  • Microscopic (Histopathology):
    • Hernia Sac: Composed of mesothelial cells lining fibrolamellar connective tissue (peritoneum).
    • Esophagitis: Basal cell hyperplasia, elongation of lamina propria papillae, and intraepithelial neutrophils or eosinophils.
    • Barrett's Esophagus: Intestinal metaplasia with specialized columnar epithelium containing acid-mucin producing goblet cells.
    • Cameron Ulcer: Represents mucosal denudation with underlying granulation tissue, fibrinoid necrosis, and inflammatory cell infiltration.
3.2

Clinical Presentation

Type I (Sliding Hernia) Presentation

  • GERD Symptoms: Retrosternal burning pain (heartburn) exacerbated by meals or lying flat, and acid regurgitation.
  • Extraesophageal GERD: Chronic cough, hoarseness (laryngopharyngeal reflux), asthma-like bronchospasm, and non-cardiac chest pain.

Types II–IV (Paraesophageal Hernia) Presentation

  • Mechanical Symptoms: Dysphagia for solids, early satiety, and postprandial retrosternal chest pressure/pain (often radiating to the back, mimicking angina).
  • Cardiopulmonary: Dyspnea and palpitations from direct compression of the lungs and left atrium by the intrathoracic stomach.
  • Anemia: Weakness and fatigue due to chronic iron-deficiency anemia from bleeding Cameron ulcers.
Emergency Alert: Gastric Volvulus Acute gastric volvulus represents a surgical emergency. It presents as **Borchardt's Triad**:
  1. Severe, sudden-onset epigastric or chest pain.
  2. Violent retching without the ability to vomit.
  3. Inability to pass a nasogastric (NG) tube.
If these signs are present, proceed immediately to the operating room to prevent gastric necrosis and perforation.
3.3

Diagnostic Investigations

Imaging Workup

  • Chest X-ray: Often shows a retrocardiac air-fluid level within the mediastinum.
  • Barium Swallow (Esophagram): The gold standard for anatomical evaluation. Defines the type of hernia, the position of the GEJ, the size of the hiatal defect, and the presence of gastric rotation/volvulus.
  • CT Scan (Chest/Abdomen): Useful to evaluate large paraesophageal hernias, identify herniated organs (Type IV), and check for signs of ischemia (e.g., pneumatosis or portal venous gas) in acute presentations.

Endoscopy & Physiologic Testing

  • EGD (Esophagogastroduodenoscopy): Mandatory before any elective surgery. Visualizes the GEJ relative to the diaphragmatic pinch, identifies esophagitis (graded via Los Angeles classification), and detects Cameron ulcers, Barrett's esophagus, or masses.
  • Esophageal Manometry: Essential to evaluate esophageal peristaltic function. Guides the choice of antireflux procedure (e.g., complete 360° Nissen wrap vs. partial 270° Toupet wrap for patients with ineffective esophageal motility).
  • 24-Hour pH / pH-Impedance Testing: Confirms pathologic acid exposure (DeMeester score >14.7) in patients with Type I hernias who have atypical symptoms or are refractory to medical therapy.
3.4

Diagnostic Criteria & Staging

Hernia Size Classification

  • Small: <3 cm of stomach herniated above the hiatus (mostly Type I sliding).
  • Moderate: 3–5 cm herniated (usually Type III mixed).
  • Large: >5 cm or >30% of the stomach herniated into the chest.
  • Giant: >50% of the stomach or the entire stomach herniated ("upside-down stomach").
3.5

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Differentiating Features Diagnostic Test
Coronary Artery Disease Exertional chest pain, relieved by nitroglycerin. ECG, Troponins, Stress Test.
Achalasia Progressive dysphagia for both solids and liquids; regurgitation of undigested food. Manometry (absent peristalsis, high LES pressure) and Barium Swallow ("bird's beak").
Esophageal Carcinoma Rapidly progressive dysphagia first for solids, then liquids; significant weight loss. EGD with Biopsy.
Bochdalek Hernia Posterolateral diaphragmatic defect (usually left-sided); rarely involves the esophageal hiatus. CT Scan.
PILLAR 4

Decision Framework & Open Surgical Repair

4.1

Management & Preoperative Preparation

Decision Framework

  • Asymptomatic Type I Hernias: No intervention is required.
  • Symptomatic Type I (GERD): Managed first-line with lifestyle modifications and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs). Surgery is indicated for PPI failure, large-volume regurgitation, or severe complications (strictures, dysplastic Barrett's).
  • Types II–IV (Paraesophageal): Elective repair is recommended for symptomatic patients, or patients with large/giant hernias (>5 cm or >30% stomach) to prevent mechanical complications. Age alone is not a contraindication, but frailty must be assessed.

Preoperative Optimization

  • Risk Stratification: Utilize the **Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI)** and the **mFI-5 Frailty Index**. Obtain cardiology clearance for patients with Class ≥2 risk or poor functional status (<4 METs).
  • Pulmonary Prep: Patients with large hernias compressing the lungs are at risk of post-op respiratory failure. Maximize COPD therapy, encourage smoking cessation for ≥4 weeks, and initiate pre-op incentive spirometry training.
  • DVT & Antibiotic Prophylaxis: Administer Cefazolin 2g IV (3g if >120 kg) within 60 minutes of incision. Initiate Caprini-guided DVT prophylaxis with Sequential Compression Devices (SCDs) and low-molecular-weight heparin (Enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily).
4.2

Operative Techniques: Open Abdominal Hiatal Hernia Repair

While laparoscopic repair is the standard in well-equipped centers, **open abdominal repair** remains the primary approach in low-resource settings, in emergencies (strangulated volvulus), for giant hernias, and in reoperative cases.

Step-by-Step Surgical Workflow

1. Incision and Exposure

Place the patient in the supine position. Tilting the table into 15–20° reverse Trendelenburg improves exposure of the upper abdomen. Perform an upper midline laparotomy from the xiphoid process to approximately 3–4 cm below the umbilicus.

Collis Gastroplasty (For Short Esophagus)

If the esophagus is shortened and cannot achieve 3 cm of intra-abdominal length despite maximal mobilization, a Collis gastroplasty is performed. Create a gastric neo-esophagus by firing a linear GIA stapler parallel to a 56–60 Fr bougie down the greater curvature, lengthening the esophagus before performing the fundoplication over the neo-esophagus.

PILLAR 5

Post-operative Care, Complications & Outcomes

5.1

Post-operative Care Protocol

  • Nasogastric (NG) Tube: In open abdominal repairs, leave the NG tube on free drainage for 24–48 hours until bowel function returns.
  • Diet Progression: Keep the patient NPO while the NG tube is in place. After removal, progress from clear liquids to full liquids. Discharge the patient on a soft/pureed diet, which must be maintained for 2–3 weeks to allow local edema to subside.
  • Activity Restrictions: Early ambulation is encouraged on POD 1. Strict lifting restrictions are mandatory: **no heavy lifting >10 kg for 8 weeks** to protect the diaphragmatic repair.
5.2

Complications & Management

Complication Mechanism & Presentation Surgical / Medical Management
Pneumothorax Injury to the pleura during mediastinal dissection. Presents as hypoxia, decreased breath sounds, or high peak airway pressures. If small (<20%) and stable, observe. If large, symptomatic, or unstable, place a 28 Fr chest tube.
Severe Dysphagia (Early) Edema at the crura or wrap, or a tight wrap. Presents as an inability to tolerate liquids or saliva. Differentiate normal edema from obstruction via a water-soluble contrast esophagram. Keep NPO, give IV fluids. Dilation should be delayed until >10–14 days.
Esophageal Perforation Direct thermal or mechanical injury during dissection. Presents as chest pain, fever, mediastinal air, or sepsis. Urgent re-exploration, primary repair of the esophageal wall with Vicryl, and buttressing using the gastric fundus. Place drains.
Slipped Wrap The wrap slips down onto the stomach, causing obstruction or severe recurrent reflux. Requires reoperation to take down the wrap and redo the repair correctly.
5.3

Prognosis & Long-Term Outcomes

  • Symptom Control: Open Nissen fundoplication combined with hiatal hernia repair achieves excellent reflux control in 85–95% of patients at 5–10 years.
  • Recurrence Rates: Radiographic recurrence is reported in 10–30% of patients at 3–5 years (higher in giant hernias, patients with obesity, or untreated chronic cough). However, only 2–5% of patients require a reoperation for symptomatic recurrence.
  • Gas Bloat Syndrome: Occurs in 30–40% of patients initially, presenting as an inability to belch or vomit and postprandial abdominal fullness. Resolves or improves in >90% of cases by 3–6 months.

Surgical Eponyms & Board Pearls

  • Borchardt's Triad: Diagnostic for acute gastric volvulus (chest/epigastric pain, violent retching without vomiting, inability to pass an NG tube). Mandates emergent laparotomy.
  • Cameron Ulcers: Linear mucosal erosions on the gastric folds at the level of the diaphragmatic hiatus, caused by friction and ischemia. A major cause of occult GI bleeding.
  • Collis Gastroplasty: Surgical technique to lengthen a shortened esophagus using a neo-esophageal tube created from the gastric fundus.
  • Belsey Mark IV: An open transthoracic 270° posterior fundoplication, reserved for complex recurrences or shortened esophagus.
