Pancreatic Trauma

Advanced Surgical Monograph covering AAST Organ Injury Scale revisions, ductal integrity decision pathways, damage control pancreas packing, and trauma pancreaticoduodenectomies.

Pancreatic Trauma AAST OIS 2025 Damage Control ABSITE Core
PILLAR 1

Core Foundations & The Evidence Base

1.1

Pathophysiology & Clinical Framework

Pancreatic injury is rare — roughly 1-2% of abdominal trauma, with published ranges as wide as 0.2-12% depending on the series — but the morbidity is disproportionate. The gland sits retroperitoneal across L1-L2, and blunt epigastric force crushes it against the spine. The neck, directly overlying the vertebral column, is the classic fracture point.

The central problem is ductal integrity, not parenchymal depth. An intact duct, even with a deep laceration, can often be managed non-operatively or with drainage alone. A disrupted duct means autodigestion, fat necrosis, fistula, and a hostile abdomen days later.

AAST Organ Injury Scale Update (2025) The AAST Organ Injury Scale itself was revised in 2025 — Notrica et al. published a refined version introducing more precise ductal injury classification and additional descriptors for complex trauma. If you trained on the old scale, know that examiners may now expect familiarity with the updated ductal-injury stratification, not just the legacy five-grade version.

The relationship between anatomy and damage severity can be modeled conceptually as:

\[\text{Grade} = f(\text{parenchymal disruption}, \text{ductal status}, \text{location relative to SMV})\]
1.2

Key Learning Objectives

  • Apply AAST-OIS grading (including the 2025 revision) and translate grade into operative decision-making.
  • Differentiate proximal (right of SMV) versus distal (left of SMV) ductal injury management.
  • Know the real diagnostic yield and timing limitations of CT, and when to escalate to MRCP or ERCP.
  • Execute damage control technique specifically for the pancreas — not generic "pack and go."
  • Define concrete criteria for when combined pancreaticoduodenal injury crosses the threshold into trauma pancreaticoduodenectomy.
  • Sequence operative priorities when pancreatic injury coexists with major vascular or duodenal injury.
  • Know when endoscopic stenting replaces resection, and when it doesn't.
1.3

Practice-Changing Evidence — Seminal Trials

Trial / Study Year Findings Board Relevance
AAST-OIS Pancreatic Scale Revision (Notrica et al.) 2025 Refined ductal injury classification and new descriptors for complex trauma. Examiners testing currency will probe whether you know the scale changed, not just the legacy version.

WTA Pancreaticoduodenal Cohort Study 2022-2024 Pyloric exclusion showed no consistent advantage over primary repair plus drainage in Grade III-IV duodenal injury. Reinforces selective, rather than routine, use of pyloric exclusion.
1.4

The Consultant's Debate: Resection vs. Endoscopic Stenting in Grade III Ductal Injury

FOR Resection AGAINST Resection (Stent-First)
Definitive source control in one sitting. No dependency on out-of-hours endoscopic expertise, no risk of stent migration, and no failure requiring a second procedure in an already compromised patient. The 2025 meta-analysis demonstrates an 89% clinical success rate in selected partial ductal injuries, avoiding a distal pancreatectomy (and its attendant 30% fistula risk) entirely.
Surgical resection eliminates the necrotic tail tissue that can lead to delayed chronic retroperitoneal sepsis or recurrent pseudocysts. The resection dataset is heavily pediatric (32%); its applicability to adult blunt major-mechanism trauma with complete ductal transection is limited.
Consultant's Position Partial ductal injury, stable patient, endoscopic expertise available: Stent first; reserve surgery for failure. Complete transection distal to the SMV, stable patient: Resect; stenting a fully transected duct is a low-probability play. Unstable patient (any ductal status): Neither applies; you are in damage control.
PILLAR 2

Clinical Protocol & Perioperative Care

2.1

Advanced Diagnostics

Nonspecific presentations are the norm due to confounding multiorgan injuries. Amylase and lipase are notoriously unreliable early markers.

Modality Sensitivity Specificity Clinical Utility & Traps
CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast (<12h) 60-85% ~90% Subtle findings (retroperitoneal fluid, minor hematomas) are common. Easy to miss ductal injuries early.
CT (repeat at 12-24h) >90% ~92% Highly defensible; edema and fluid collections declare themselves over time.
MRCP 80-90% ~95% Best non-invasive ductal integrity assessment in hemodynamically stable patients.
ERCP Diagnostic/Therapeutic N/A 89% success rate for therapeutic stenting. Combines direct visualization with intervention.
Amylase/Lipase Poor early (40-60%) Very poor **Classic exam trap:** A normal early level does NOT exclude pancreatic injury.
2.2

Resuscitation & ERAS 2025/2026 Optimization

Damage Control Resuscitation: Balanced 1:1:1 products, permissive hypotension pre-control, and strict crystalloid minimization (crystalloids worsen peripancreatic edema, ductal swelling, and fistula leak rates).

ERAS & Perioperative Protocols

  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum single agent (piperacillin-tazobactam) for 24-48h if enteric contamination is present. Avoid prolonged prophylaxis post-source control.
  • Nutrition: Early enteral feeding via post-pyloric/nasojejunal access is preferred over TPN once gut functionality returns.
  • Drains: Use closed-suction drains. Leave in place until amylase output trends down. Drain fluid amylase >3x serum at day 3 confirms a persistent fistula.
  • Pain Management: Multimodal, opioid-sparing protocols, incorporating epidural analgesia where hemodynamics permit.
Octreotide & Spleen Preservation (EAST GRADE Analysis) Octreotide: Routine use to prevent pancreatic fistula was specifically scrutinized by EAST guidelines and found wanting — do not reflexively start it.

Splenectomy with Distal Pancreatectomy: Routine splenectomy is not indicated; spleen preservation should always be attempted when patient physiology and anatomy allow.
2.3

Complication Management (Clavien-Dindo IIIB-V)

Complication Grade Incidence Management Protocol
Pancreatic Fistula (POPF Grade B/C) IIIA-IIIB 15-35% Percutaneous drainage and nutritional optimization. Octreotide is not routinely indicated. Reoperation is reserved for uncontrolled sepsis.
Intra-abdominal Abscess IIIB 10-25% First-line management is CT-guided percutaneous drainage.
Pseudocyst IIIA-IIIB 5-15% Observe if asymptomatic and <6cm. Endoscopic cystgastrostomy once the wall matures (4-6 weeks).
Delayed Hemorrhage (Pseudoaneurysm) IVA 3-8% Emergent CT angiography followed by angioembolization is first-line.
Anastomotic/Duodenal Disruption IVA-V Variable Re-exploration, wide drainage, and consideration of biliary/enteral diversion.
Death (Combined Vascular-Pancreatic) V 20-40% In Grade IV-V injuries, mortality is driven by hemorrhagic shock from associated major vessels, not the pancreatic component.
PILLAR 3

The Operative Atlas

3.1

Surgical Anatomy & Danger Zones

The pancreas has no true capsule; sutures easily tear through friable tissue. Access is gained via the gastrocolic ligament into the lesser sac (preferred) or by mobilizing the hepatic flexure.

Danger Zones

  • The Pancreatic Neck: Directly overlies the SMV-portal confluence. This is the single most dangerous point during dissection.
  • The Uncinate Process: Wraps behind the SMV.
  • Splenic Vasculature: Runs directly along the superior border of the pancreatic body and tail.
  • Duodenum: Shares a rich blood supply with the pancreatic head via the anterior and posterior pancreaticoduodenal arcades — injuring one frequently compromises the other.

A full Kocher maneuver is mandatory for any suspected injury to the head of the pancreas.

3.2

Step-by-Step Procedure: Decision Framework

Step 1: Exposure. Perform a midline laparotomy. Enter the lesser sac through the gastrocolic ligament. Perform a Kocher maneuver if the head is involved.

Step 2: Assess duct status. Direct inspection, intraoperative pancreatography if available, or gentle catheter probing. In most trauma centers, inspection and clinical judgment under time pressure dictate the diagnosis.

Step 3a: Distal injury, duct disrupted, stable patient. Perform a distal pancreatectomy. Spleen preservation must be attempted (avoid routine splenectomy). Secure the parenchymal division with stapled or sutured transection and place wide drainage. If a partial ductal injury is present and the patient is stable, endoscopic stenting is a reasonable post-op alternative to resection; discuss with GI endoscopy if feasible.

Step 3b: Proximal injury (head, right of SMV). Duct intact or partial tear — debride and drain widely; do not perform formal resection. Duct fully transected with combined duodenal injury — execute damage control: control hemorrhage, drain widely, perform selective pyloric exclusion only if duodenal repair is tenuous, and plan a return at 24-48h.

Step 3c: Unstable patient (any location). Pack, drain, and close (see damage control technique below).

Associated Injury Sequencing (Exam-Relevant) When pancreatic injury coexists with major vascular and duodenal trauma, prioritize as follows:
  1. Hemorrhage control: Vascular control always comes first. Period.
  2. Contamination control: Staple off or clamp duodenal/bowel injuries without committing to formal repair.
  3. Pancreatic damage control: Drain, do not resect, in the index operation.
  4. Definitive reconstruction: Delay until second-look laparotomy once physiology has normalized.
3.3

The Difficult Case

Hostile / Frozen Abdomen

Start dissection in an uninvolved, clean quadrant and work toward the injury. Use the lesser sac entry point furthest from prior scarring. Ensure proximal and distal vascular control is obtained before mobilizing tissue near the SMV-PV confluence.

Sudden Hemorrhage During Dissection

Apply direct pressure immediately. This is almost always an SMV or splenic vein injury — do not clamp blindly. Note that a Pringle maneuver is useless here, as it only controls hepatic arterial and portal inflow, not systemic venous backflow. Get vessel loops above and below the pancreatic neck; dividing the neck itself is often required to gain adequate exposure to identify and suture the bleeding vessel.

Damage Control Packing Technique

Do not simply stuff packs into the lesser sac. Place an omental pedicle over the injured gland before applying lap pads; this cushions the parenchyma and reduces mechanical necrosis from pack pressure. If you identify a ductal injury that cannot be repaired, place a marking suture or a small stent at the visible duct end so the second-look team isn't searching blindly in a swollen, distorted field. Leave wide, well-positioned closed-suction drainage even in the packed abdomen to ensure a controlled fistula rather than a free retroperitoneal leak.

Trauma Pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple) Threshold

This massive resection is reserved exclusively for cases where the head of the pancreas AND the duodenum are both devascularized beyond viable repair. This typically follows devastating injuries to the inferior pancreaticoduodenal arcade and associated mesenteric vessels where no reconstructable blood supply remains. It is NOT indicated for isolated pancreatic head injuries with an intact duodenum, and it is absolutely contraindicated as a primary procedure in an unstable patient. Perform damage control first; staged reconstruction should be delayed until the second or third operation.

PILLAR 4

Spatial Awareness & Radiological Correlation

4.1

Operative 3D Spatial Awareness

Standing at the patient's right side, with the lesser sac entered and the stomach retracted superiorly, the pancreas presents as a transverse, rubbery band with its tail tilting cephalad toward the splenic hilum. The neck sits directly anterior to the spine — you can palpate the vertebral body through it.

The SMV runs posterior to the neck and is hidden from direct view. This anatomical configuration explains why blind clamping at the neck is catastrophic; you must work by feel until you have mobilized the tissue sufficiently to expose the vessel.

4.2

Radiological Correlation (The Consultant's Read)

Systematically read trauma CT scans for pancreatic injury using this 6-step read protocol:

  1. Fluid in the lesser sac / anterior pararenal space: The earliest, but least specific, sign of retroperitoneal trauma.
  2. Discrete laceration line: A hypoattenuating line running perpendicular to the gland axis, particularly at the neck — this is the most reliable sign.
  3. "Sentinel clot" sign: A focal high-attenuation hematoma adjacent to an apparently intact gland; prompts a repeat scan in 12-24h.
  4. Peripancreatic fat stranding: Disproportionate stranding in the retroperitoneal space.
  5. Ductal discontinuity: Direct visualization of ductal disruption on thin-cut CT or MRCP. It is frequently absent on initial trauma CT scans; its absence does not exclude injury.
  6. Duodenal wall thickening or free air: Look closely near the pancreatic head to rule out combined duodeno-pancreatic injuries.

PILLAR 5

Senior Fellowship & Board Review

5.1

High-Order Viva Scenarios

Q1: A stable 28-year-old MVC patient presents with a peripancreatic hematoma at the neck, but no clear laceration is visible on the initial CT scan. What is your management?
*Expert Answer:* Obtain a repeat CT scan in 12-24 hours or perform an MRCP. Do not operate on an equivocal early scan given the high false-negative rates of initial trauma CTs for ductal injury.

Q2: You find an intraoperative Grade III pancreatic injury at the neck with a clearly transected duct. The patient is hemodynamically stable and has no other injuries. What is your action?
*Expert Answer:* Perform a distal pancreatectomy. Spleen preservation should be attempted (avoid routine splenectomy, in line with the EAST GRADE guidelines).

Q3: You find the same Grade III neck injury, but the patient is now hypotensive and has a coexisting bleeding liver laceration. How do you manage this?
*Expert Answer:* This mandates damage control. Control liver bleeding first, place an omental pedicle over the pancreas, pack, place wide drains, and mark the ductal end if visible. Do not perform resection in an unstable patient. Re-explore once physiology is corrected.

Q4: A stable patient has a partial ductal injury confirmed on MRCP, and endoscopic expertise is available. What is your preferred approach?
*Expert Answer:* Endoscopic stenting (ERCP) is now a legitimate first-line option. The 2025 meta-analysis demonstrates an 89% clinical success rate in selected partial injuries. Reserve surgical resection for stenting failure.

Q5: On post-operative day 5 after a distal pancreatectomy, the patient's drain output is 200mL, with a fluid amylase 4x serum. The patient is well, tolerating diet, and afebrile. What is your management?
*Expert Answer:* This is a Grade B postoperative pancreatic fistula (POPF). Keep the drain in place, optimize enteral nutrition, and do not routinely administer octreotide. Reoperation is only indicated if uncontrolled sepsis develops.

Q6: A patient with combined pancreaticoduodenal devascularization and no reconstructable blood supply to either organ has been packed and resuscitated, and is now stable at second-look laparotomy. What is your plan?
*Expert Answer:* This meets the threshold for staged trauma pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple). Reconstruct only now during the planned second operation, never as an index procedure in the emergency setting.

5.2

Classifications & Scoring Systems

AAST Pancreatic Organ Injury Scale (Legacy + 2025 Revision)

Grade Injury Description
I Minor contusion or superficial laceration; no duct injury.
II Major contusion or laceration; no duct injury or tissue loss.
III Distal transection or parenchymal injury with duct injury (left of SMV).
IV Proximal transection or injury involving the ampulla (right of SMV).
V Massive disruption of the pancreatic head.

Note: The 2025 AAST revision adds refined ductal injury sub-classification and specific descriptors for complex combined trauma.

  • 47480: Cholecystostomy (for concurrent biliary decompression).
  • 44602-44605: Duodenal repair.

RAPID BOARD REVIEW SUMMARY — Pancreatic Trauma Must-Know Facts

  • Octreotide / Splenectomy: EAST GRADE evaluation rejects routine octreotide prophylaxis for POPF and rejects routine splenectomy during distal pancreatectomy. Spleen-preservation is preferred.
  • Associated Injuries: Sequence must be: hemorrhage control → contamination control → pancreatic damage control → delayed reconstruction.
  • Whipple Indicator: Trauma pancreaticoduodenectomy is reserved for devascularization of both the head of the pancreas and the duodenum. Never perform Whipple in an unstable patient.
