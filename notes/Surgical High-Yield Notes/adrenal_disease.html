Adrenal Disease Pathology & Surgery

Complete high-yield resident framework covering incidentalomas, hypercortisolism, primary hyperaldosteronism, pheochromocytomas, adrenocortical carcinomas, and neuroblastomas.

PILLAR 1 Core Surgical Anatomy & Physiology
1.1

Vascular Anatomy & Asymmetric Venous Drainage

Macroscopic Anatomy

  • Location: Paired retroperitoneal endocrine organs situated superomedial to each kidney at the T12-L1 level. Weight is 4-6 grams each.
  • Arterial Supply: Rich vascular plexus supplied from three main sources:
    • Superior Adrenal Arteries: Multiple branches arising from the inferior phrenic artery.
    • Middle Adrenal Arteries: Branch directly from the lateral aspect of the abdominal aorta.
    • Inferior Adrenal Arteries: Arise from the renal artery.

Asymmetric Venous Drainage (Critical Bleeding Risk)

Adrenal Side Venous Anatomy & Drainage Path Dissection Pitfall & Complications
Right Side Very short (often <5 mm) and wide vein draining directly into the posterolateral wall of the Inferior Vena Cava (IVC). Traction or blind dissection can avulse the vein directly from the IVC wall, resulting in catastrophic pericaval hemorrhage.
Left Side Longer vein draining inferiorly into the left inferior phrenic vein, which subsequently empties into the superior aspect of the left renal vein. Tributary ligation is required. Safe dissection can be achieved by working from a lateral-to-medial plane.
Open Exposure of Adrenal Glands (Right & Left)
1.2

Histo-Physiology & Cortex-Medulla Interactions

Zonal Histology and Secretions

  • Adrenal Cortex (Mesodermal Embryology):
    • Zona Glomerulosa (Outer 15%): Secretes mineralocorticoids (aldosterone). Regulated primarily by Renin, Angiotensin II, and Potassium. ACTH-independent.
    • Zona Fasciculata (Middle 75%): Secretes glucocorticoids (cortisol). Regulated by pituitary ACTH via negative feedback.
    • Zona Reticularis (Inner 10%): Secretes adrenal androgens (DHEA/DHEA-S). Regulated by ACTH.
  • Adrenal Medulla (Neural Crest Embryology):
    • Populated by chromaffin cells (modified sympathetic postganglionic cells).
    • Secretes catecholamines (80% Epinephrine, 20% Norepinephrine).
Cortisol-Medulla Interaction The conversion of norepinephrine to epinephrine in chromaffin cells requires the enzyme phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT). Up-regulation of PNMT requires high local concentrations of cortisol, which drains directly from the surrounding cortex into the medullary sinusoids.

PILLAR 2 Pathophysiology & Endocrine Syndromes
2.1

Cortical Hypersecretion & Congenital Deficiencies

1. Cushing's Syndrome (ACTH-Independent)

  • Mechanism: Autonomous cortisol excess from an adrenal adenoma or adrenocortical carcinoma (ACC).
  • Systemic Effects: High glucocorticoid levels stimulate catabolism, muscle wasting, central adiposity (fat redistribution), Moon facies, Buffalo hump, purple striae >1 cm width, and immune suppression.
  • Contralateral Gland Atrophy: Autonomous cortisol output exerts absolute negative feedback on CRH and ACTH release. The contralateral normal gland suffers profound architectural and functional atrophy, requiring perioperative stress-dose steroid support.

2. Conn's Syndrome (Primary Hyperaldosteronism)

  • Mechanism: Autonomous aldosterone secretion from a unilateral cortical adenoma (APA, 65%) or Bilateral Adrenal Hyperplasia (BAH, 30%).
  • Nephron Physiology: Excess aldosterone binds to mineralocorticoid receptors in the distal nephron, driving sodium reabsorption via epithelial sodium channels (ENaC) in exchange for potassium and hydrogen ion excretion.
  • Clinical Sequelae: Volume-expansion hypertension, hypokalemic muscle weakness, metabolic alkalosis, and feedback suppression of plasma renin.

3. Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

  • Mechanism: Autosomal recessive deficiency in steroid synthesis enzymes, most commonly 21-hydroxylase (90-95% of cases).
  • Physiologic Loop: Defective cortisol production blocks normal negative feedback on the anterior pituitary. Persistent ACTH hypersecretion drives bilateral adrenal hyperplasia, shunting precursor molecules (17-hydroxyprogesterone) into the androgen pathway, leading to virilization.
2.2

Medullary Tumors & Primary Adrenal Malignancies

1. Pheochromocytoma

  • Definition: A catecholamine-secreting neuroendocrine tumor of medullary chromaffin cells.
  • Rule of 10s: 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal (paragangliomas), 10% malignant, 10% pediatric, and 10% familial/hereditary.
  • Receptor Activation:
    • α1-activation: Severe vasoconstriction, causing paroxysmal or sustained hypertension.
    • β1-activation: Reflex tachycardia, palpitations, and catecholamine-induced cardiomyopathy.
    • β2/α2 activation: Stimulates hepatic glycogenolysis and inhibits insulin release, causing secondary hyperglycemia.
  • Genetic Associations: MEN 2A/2B (RET mutation), VHL, NF1, and Succinate Dehydrogenase (SDH) mutations (SDHB, SDHC, SDHD). SDHB carries the highest risk of malignancy.

2. Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC)

  • Pathology: Extremely rare and aggressive malignancy (1-2 per million/year). Bimodal age distribution (<5 years and 40-50 years).
  • Function: 60% are functional, presenting with rapid-onset mixed hormone excess (Cushing's + virilization). Non-functional tumors present late with flank pain, a palpable mass, and cachexia.

3. Neuroblastoma

  • Pathology: The most common extracranial solid malignancy of childhood, arising from primitive neural crest cells in the sympathetic chain or adrenal medulla.
  • Prognostic Driver: Amplification of the MYCN oncogene is the strongest predictor of rapid progression and poor survival.
PILLAR 3 Diagnostic Workup & Staging
3.1

Biochemical Diagnostic Cascades

Cushing's Syndrome Diagnostic Pathway

  1. Screening (Confirm Hypercortisolism - require 1 positive):
    • 24-hour Urinary Free Cortisol (UFC) (2 collections; >3x upper limit of normal is highly specific).
    • Late-night salivary cortisol (sensitivity 92-100%).
    • 1 mg overnight Dexamethasone Suppression Test (DST) (morning cortisol >1.8 μg/dL or >50 nmol/L represents failure of suppression).
  2. Establish ACTH-Dependence:
    • Measure plasma ACTH at 09:00.
    • ACTH <2 pmol/L (suppressed) confirms primary adrenal disease. Proceed to adrenal CT.
    • ACTH elevated or normal indicates ACTH-dependent disease (pituitary or ectopic).
  3. Pituitary vs. Ectopic Localization (BIPSS Gold Standard):
    • Perform Bilateral Inferior Petrosal Sinus Sampling (BIPSS) to measure central vs. peripheral ACTH.
    • Central-to-peripheral ACTH ratio >2:1 baseline (or >3:1 post-CRH stimulation) confirms a pituitary source (Cushing's Disease). Lower ratios confirm an ectopic ACTH source.

Pheochromocytoma Diagnostic Pathway

  1. Biochemical Screening (Bio before Bow):
    • Measure free fractionated plasma metanephrines (highest sensitivity: 97-99%).
    • Confirm with 24-hour urinary metanephrines. Elevations >2-4 times the upper limit are diagnostic.
  2. Anatomical & Functional Localization:
    • Adrenal protocol contrast-enhanced CT.
    • 123I-MIBG Scintigraphy: Diagnostic gold standard for functional localization, detecting extra-adrenal paragangliomas and metastatic deposits.
Left Pheochromocytoma: Axial Contrast-Enhanced CT
Functional Localization: 123I-MIBG vs. DOTATATE PET
3.2

Primary Aldosteronism: Screening, Confirmation & AVS

  1. Screening: Measure the Aldosterone-to-Renin Ratio (ARR). An ARR >20-30 ng/dL per ng/mL/h with a plasma aldosterone concentration (PAC) >15 ng/dL suggests Conn's syndrome. Stop mineralocorticoid antagonists (spironolactone) for 6 weeks prior.
  2. Confirmation: Failure to suppress PAC below 10 ng/dL (277 pmol/L) after an intravenous infusion of 2 L of normal saline over 4 hours confirms autonomous aldosterone secretion.
  3. Subtype Lateralization (Adrenal Vein Sampling):
    • AVS is the gold standard to differentiate unilateral Aldosterone-Producing Adenoma (APA) from Bilateral Adrenal Hyperplasia (BAH).
    • Mandatory for surgical candidates >35-40 years old, or in cases of normal/bilateral CT findings.
    • Selectivity Index (Cortisol-ratio): Adrenal-to-peripheral cortisol ratio >2:1 baseline (>3:1 to 5:1 under ACTH stimulation) confirms correct catheter placement.
    • Lateralization Index (Aldosterone/Cortisol ratios): A ratio >4:1 between the hypersecreting and contralateral side confirms unilateral disease.

3.4

Weiss Criteria & FNA Seeding Risks

Histological Confirmation: The Weiss Criteria

Diagnosis of Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC) requires the presence of 3 or more of the following histological criteria:

  • High nuclear grade (Fuhrman grade 3 or 4).
  • Mitotic rate >5 mitoses per 50 high-power fields (HPF).
  • Atypical mitotic figures.
  • Clear cells making up ≤25% of the tumor volume.
  • Diffuse architectural pattern spanning >33% of the tumor.
  • Confluent necrosis.
  • Venous micro-invasion.
  • Sinusoidal micro-invasion.
  • Capsular invasion.
Absolute FNA Prohibitions Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) is strictly contraindicated in primary adrenal lesions:
  • Suspected ACC: High risk of needle-track tumor seeding and peritoneal implants. FNA cannot reliably differentiate benign adenoma from ACC cytologically.
  • Unblocked Pheochromocytoma: Direct needle puncture can trigger a massive release of catecholamines, precipitating a fatal hypertensive crisis.
PILLAR 4 Operative Management & Surgical Setup
4.1

Pheochromocytoma Preoperative Blockade Protocol

Operating on a patient without adequate blockade carries a near-100% risk of intraoperative hypertensive crisis and cardiovascular collapse. Follow this protocol for 10-14 days preoperatively:

  1. Alpha Blockade First (Non-negotiable):
    • Administer Phenoxybenzamine (non-selective, irreversible α-blocker): start 10 mg PO BID, titrate up to 20-40 mg PO BID.
    • Alternative: Doxazosin (selective α1-blocker) to minimize prolonged postoperative hypotension.
    • Titrate until the patient develops mild orthostatic hypotension, indicating vasodilation.
  2. Beta Blockade Second (Never First):
    • Introduce Propranolol (10-40 mg PO TID) to control tachycardia.
    • Warning: Never start a beta-blocker first. Blocking vasodilator β2 receptors while α1 receptors remain unblocked leads to unopposed α1 stimulation, causing a severe, paradoxical hypertensive crisis.
  3. Volume Re-expansion:
    • Encourage a high-salt diet and fluid intake (2-3 L of normal saline daily) to expand the contracted intravascular volume.

4.3

Vein-First Ligation & Intraoperative Crisis Protocols

The Vein-First Rule in Pheochromocytoma Dissection

  • Identify and isolate the primary draining adrenal vein early in the dissection.
  • Ligate and divide the vein immediately upon exposure, before mobilizing the gland.
  • Rationale: Prevents massive surges of catecholamines into the systemic circulation during surgical manipulation of the tumor.
Right Lateral Laparoscopic Adrenalectomy Steps

Crisis Management Protocols

  • Hypertensive Surge (During Manipulation):
    • Inform the anesthesia team immediately to suspend dissection.
    • Administer intravenous boluses of **Phentolamine** (1-5 mg IV) or initiate a continuous **Sodium Nitroprusside** or **Nicardipine** infusion.
  • Post-Ligation Hypotension (Vein Clamped):
    • Catecholamine levels fall abruptly after venous ligation.
    • Initiate rapid fluid resuscitation (crystalloid/colloid boluses).
    • Start a continuous **Norepinephrine** vasopressor infusion.
PILLAR 5 Postoperative Care & Long-term Surveillance
5.1

Glucocorticoid Replacement & Sick-Day Rules

Unilateral Adrenalectomy for Cushing's Syndrome

  • Atrophy Cover: Because the contralateral adrenal gland is atrophic from chronic ACTH suppression, initiate stress-dose steroids immediately in the perioperative period (Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV every 8 hours).
  • Outpatient Taper: Transition to oral hydrocortisone replacement (20 mg in the morning, 10 mg in the afternoon) and taper over 6-24 months.
  • HPA Recovery Testing: Assess morning (09:00) serum cortisol level. A level >500 nmol/L indicates recovery of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, allowing replacement therapy to be discontinued.

Bilateral Adrenalectomy Replacement

  • Lifelong replacement is mandatory:
    • Glucocorticoids: Hydrocortisone 20-30 mg daily (divided dose).
    • Mineralocorticoids: **Fludrocortisone** 50-200 μg orally daily.
  • Sick-Day Rules: Instruct the patient to double or triple the oral dose during periods of mild illness or fever. Administer intramuscular hydrocortisone if vomiting prevents oral intake.
5.2

Postoperative Protocols for Conn's & Pheochromocytoma

Post-Conn's Adrenalectomy Management

  • Medication Discontinuation: Stop all spironolactone and potassium supplements on Postoperative Day 1.
  • Hyperkalemia Risk: The contralateral adrenal cortex may suffer from chronic aldosterone suppression. Avoid potassium-containing intravenous fluids. Check serum potassium at 1 week post-op to monitor for hyperkalemia.
  • Hypertension Resolution: Blood pressure may remain elevated for weeks or months. Allow 3-6 months for vascular remodeling before evaluating for final resolution.

Post-Pheochromocytoma Adrenalectomy Management

  • Rebound Hypoglycemia: Monitor blood glucose levels every 4-6 hours postoperatively. Sudden withdrawal of catecholamines removes the suppression on beta cells, causing a transient surge in insulin release.
  • Biochemical Cure Documentation: Measure free plasma metanephrines 2-4 weeks postoperatively.
  • Long-Term Follow-up: Establish lifelong annual biochemical screening to monitor for recurrences or late metastases (10% rule).

Surgical Eponyms Checklist

  • Cattell-Braasch: Right medial visceral rotation. Mobilization of the right colon and small bowel root to expose the IVC, aorta, and right renal vessels.
  • Mattox Maneuver: Left medial visceral rotation. Mobilization of the left colon, spleen, left kidney, and pancreas to expose the suprarenal aorta.
  • Pringle Maneuver: Temporary occlusion of the hepatoduodenal ligament (portal triad) to control hepatic inflow during liver mobilization.
