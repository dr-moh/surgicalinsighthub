The Brutal Rule
"When colicky pain goes constant — that bowel is dying. Stop waiting. Operate."
The shift from intermittent colic to constant pain is strangulation until proven otherwise. Miss this sign once and you will bury someone. Every minute after that shift is necrosis accruing.
01

Pathophysiology

Obstruction creates a one-way trap — secretions accumulate, intraluminal pressure rises, and the bowel wall fails in a predictable sequence: distension → ischaemia → necrosis → perforation. Strangulation accelerates the cascade by cutting off venous return first, then arterial inflow — this is why a patient can look deceptively well while bowel is already dying inside.

MechanismConsequence
Luminal block → 7–9 L/day secretions accumulateDistension, ↑ intraluminal pressure
Third-spacing into bowel wall + peritoneumHypovolaemia, AKI, haemoconcentration
Vomiting (proximal SBO): H⁺, Cl⁻, K⁺ lossHypochloraemic, hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis
Bacterial stasis → gram-neg + anaerobe overgrowthEndotoxin production → bacterial translocation
Venous outflow blocked first → arterial inflow cutStrangulation cascade → necrosis → perforation
Closed-loop: both limbs occluded (volvulus/hernia)No decompression possible. Fastest progression to necrosis.
Laplace's Law: wall tension = (P × r) / thicknessCaecum perforates first — largest diameter in the colon
Caecal thresholds: concern at 9 cm; critical at 12 cm — perforation risk rises sharply beyond this.

SBO Aetiology

Adhesions from prior surgery account for nearly three-quarters of all SBO. In a patient with no prior abdominal surgery, think hernia first. Femoral hernias are small, easily missed in the groin crease, and carry the highest strangulation risk of any hernia type.

CauseFreqKey Point
Adhesions60–75%Post-op. #1 overall cause of SBO.
Hernia (incarcerated)10–15%Femoral = highest strangulation risk. Always examine the groin.
Neoplasm5–10%Primary small bowel tumour or extrinsic compression
Crohn's stricture5%Chronic transmural inflammation → fibrotic stricture
Intussusception1–3%Adults: always exclude malignant lead point
Gallstone ileus<1%Bouveret's syndrome or Rigler's triad on AXR
Radiation strictureRarePost-pelvic or abdominal radiotherapy
Virgin abdomen SBO (no prior surgery) = hernia until proven otherwise. Adhesions cannot form without a prior insult.
04

LBO Aetiology

Colorectal carcinoma dominates — most commonly at the sigmoid colon where the lumen is narrowest. Volvulus demographics differ starkly: sigmoid volvulus in elderly, institutionalised patients; caecal volvulus in younger adults with incomplete peritoneal fixation.

CauseFreqKey Point
Colorectal carcinoma60–70%Sigmoid most common site. Often presents late.
Sigmoid volvulus10–15%Elderly, institutionalised, chronic constipation
Caecal volvulus5%Younger patients; incomplete peritoneal fixation
Diverticular stricture5–10%Chronic diverticulitis → fibrotic narrowing
Ogilvie's syndromeFunctionalNo mechanical cause; colonic atony in ICU/post-op
Faecal impaction5%Elderly, immobile, opioid-related
LBO with competent ileocaecal valve = closed-loop obstruction of the entire colon. The caecum takes the full pressure load and will perforate first (Laplace's Law).
05

Clinical Presentation — SBO vs LBO

The four cardinal features — pain, vomiting, distension, constipation — vary in timing and severity by obstruction level. Proximal obstruction vomits early and distends minimally; distal obstruction distends massively and vomits late. Absolute constipation means no flatus and no stool.

FeatureSBOLBO
PainColicky, central, periumbilicalColicky, lower abdominal / hypogastric
VomitingEarly, profuse — bilious → faeculentLate, faeculent
DistensionLess prominent (minimal if proximal)Prominent, tympanic
ConstipationLate signEarly presenting feature
Absolute rule: Proximal = early severe vomiting. Distal = prominent distension. MCQ and viva staple.

06

Strangulation Red Flags

The clinical exam is unreliable in early strangulation — a patient can look deceptively well while bowel is already non-viable on CT. Lactate is your best single biochemical marker. If you are waiting for the full picture to develop, you are already late.

Operate — Do Not Wait
  • Colicky pain shifting to constant pain — the single most important clinical sign
  • Localised tenderness or peritonism / guarding
  • Fever >38.5°C combined with tachycardia >100 bpm
  • Serum lactate >2 mmol/L
  • WBC >15 × 10⁹/L
  • Free fluid on imaging disproportionate to degree of obstruction
  • CT: pneumatosis intestinalis, portal venous gas, absent bowel wall enhancement
07

Special Presentations

Each entity below has a pathognomonic AXR or CT finding — recognise these instantly. Gallstone ileus and volvulus are the classic traps. The diagnosis is sitting on the plain film if you know what to look for.

ConditionClassic Signs

Sigmoid volvulusCoffee bean / omega loop sign. Apex pointing to RUQ. Elderly or institutionalised.
Caecal volvulusKidney bean sign. Caecum displaced to LUQ. Younger patient.

08

Bedside — Do These First

  1. IV access ×2 + Hartmann's resuscitation immediately
  2. NG tube to free drainage — decompress, prevent aspiration
  3. Urinary catheter — target UO ≥0.5 mL/kg/h
  4. Nil by mouth
  5. Serial abdominal exam every 4–6 hours — detect early strangulation
  6. IV opioid analgesia — do not withhold
  7. Examine all hernial orifices + PR examination — mandatory every time
09

Resuscitation Targets

Hartmann's solution is preferred — 0.9% NaCl causes hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis which complicates strangulation biochemistry. Correct hypokalaemia before any anaesthetic.

Investigations

CT abdomen with IV contrast is the gold standard and must be obtained whenever there is diagnostic uncertainty or any concern for strangulation. AXR is fast and useful for pattern recognition but misses the cause in up to 50% of cases. Never delay CT when you suspect strangulation — the exam and bloods can mislead; CT rarely does.

13

SBO — Non-operative Management

Appropriate only for partial adhesive SBO without strangulation. The window is 48–72 hours maximum — beyond that, failure to improve mandates operative intervention. Never apply to a complete obstruction or any case with strangulation signs.

Indications: Partial adhesive SBO. No strangulation signs. Haemodynamically stable. No peritonism.
  • NG tube to free drainage
  • IV Hartmann's + electrolyte replacement
  • Nil by mouth
  • Serial abdominal exam every 4–6 hours
  • Gastrografin challenge at 24–48 h
  • Monitor: UO, lactate, WBC, CRP, abdominal signs
Window: Up to 72 h. Abandon immediately if strangulation signs, deterioration, or no improvement at 72 h.
14

Gastrografin Protocol (Bologna Guidelines)

Gastrografin is both diagnostic and therapeutic in adhesive partial SBO. Its hyperosmolar properties draw fluid into the bowel lumen, reducing mural oedema and potentially resolving the obstruction without surgery. A positive challenge — contrast reaching the colon within 24 hours — confirms partial obstruction and predicts non-operative success.

  1. Administer 100 mL gastrografin orally or via NG tube
  2. Obtain AXR or CT at 24 hours
  3. Contrast in colon at 24 h = partial SBO → continue non-operative management
  4. Contrast NOT in colon at 24 h = complete SBO → operative management
Mechanism: hyperosmolar → draws fluid into lumen → reduces bowel wall oedema. Reduces operative rate by ~20% in adhesive partial SBO.

Bowel Viability — Intraoperative

Viability assessment is a clinical skill — it cannot be delegated to imaging. Four criteria for viable bowel: pink colour, active peristalsis, arterial pulsations, bleeding when cut. When in doubt after a warm wrap, resect — the consequences of leaving non-viable bowel far outweigh the consequences of an anastomosis.

StatusSignsAction
ViablePink, peristalsis, pulsations, bleeds when cutPreserve
Non-viableBlack or green, no peristalsis, no pulsationsResect immediately
DoubtfulBorderline colour, sluggish peristalsisWarm wrap × 5 min. Still doubtful → resect.
Extensive uncertainLarge segment questionable viabilitySecond look laparotomy at 24–48 h

17

Sigmoid Volvulus

Endoscopic decompression succeeds in 70–85% of cases and avoids emergency surgery. But it is not definitive treatment. Without elective sigmoid colectomy during the same admission, recurrence approaches 50–90%. Do not discharge without a surgical plan.

Step 1 — Endoscopic decompression (first-line): Colonoscopy or rigid sigmoidoscopy. Success 70–85%. Leave rectal tube in situ 24–48 h to prevent immediate recurrence.
Step 2 — Elective sigmoid colectomy (same admission): Laparoscopic preferred. Definitive. Recurrence without surgery = 50–90%. Book before discharge.
Emergency Surgery If:
  • Failed endoscopic decompression
  • Signs of strangulation or bowel ischaemia
  • Peritonitis or free perforation
18

Caecal Volvulus

Caecal volvulus occurs in younger patients with incomplete peritoneal fixation of the right colon. Unlike sigmoid volvulus, endoscopic decompression has absolutely no role — the right colon is inaccessible and attempts are dangerous. This diagnosis means theatre, not endoscopy suite.

No endoscopic decompression — go straight to theatre
  • Right hemicolectomy = definitive treatment
  • Primary ileocolic anastomosis if patient stable
  • Caecostomy only for extreme surgical risk
19

Malignant LBO — CRC

In a stable patient with left-sided malignant LBO, SEMS as a bridge to elective surgery is standard of care. This converts an emergency operation — with high stoma rates and poor oncological outcomes — into a planned resection with staging, bowel prep, and MDT input.

SituationManagement
Stable, left-sided obstructionSEMS bridge → elective surgery within 4–8 weeks
SEMS unavailable / right-sidedEmergency right hemicolectomy + primary anastomosis
Perforation / peritonitisHartmann's procedure
Unresectable / palliativeSEMS or defunctioning colostomy
SEMS: technical success ~90%; clinical success ~75%. Contraindicated in perforation, peritonitis, and proximal colonic tumours.
20

Ogilvie's Syndrome

A diagnosis of exclusion — CT must rule out mechanical obstruction first. Driven by autonomic imbalance in critically ill or post-operative patients. Neostigmine restores parasympathetic tone to the colon — have atropine drawn up before you inject it.

Caecal DiameterAction
<9 cmConservative: IV fluids, stop opioids/anticholinergics, mobilise, rectal tube
9–12 cmIV neostigmine 2 mg slow over 3–5 min. Atropine at bedside. Success ~90%.
>12 cmColonoscopic decompression (success 75–85%). Insert decompression tube.
+ PerforationSurgical: caecostomy or Hartmann's
Neostigmine CI: mechanical obstruction, severe asthma, bradycardia, renal failure.

Intussusception

In children, intussusception is idiopathic in the vast majority — radiological reduction is diagnostic and therapeutic. In adults, there is almost always a malignant lead point — every adult case requires operative management.

GroupCauseManagement
Children (peak 3m–3y)Idiopathic (lead point rare)Air-enema reduction first-line. Success 75–90%. Avoids surgery.
AdultsMalignant lead point in majorityOperate. No role for radiological reduction.
22

Post-op Care (0–72 h)

Management centres on four pillars: adequate resuscitation, early detection of anastomotic leak, VTE prevention, and ERAS-driven early recovery. Avoid NSAIDs after any anastomosis — COX inhibition impairs anastomotic healing and increases leak risk.

DomainAction
Level of careHDU if strangulation repaired, extensive resection, or high operative risk
NG tubeFree drainage. Remove when <200 mL/day + bowel sounds present.
IV fluidsHartmann's. Wean when tolerating oral.
AnalgesiaIV opioids → paracetamol. Avoid NSAIDs post-anastomosis.
VTE prophylaxisEnoxaparin 40 mg OD from night of surgery + TED stockings
Antibiotics24 h prophylaxis; 5–7 days if strangulation or peritonitis
ERASMobilise day 1. Oral fluids day 1–2. Diet day 4–5.
